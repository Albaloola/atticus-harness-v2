# Petition for Habeas Corpus / Bill of Suspension (Scotland / UK)

## Atticus UK/Scots Legal Excellence Overlay

Use this skill as an autonomous legal-operations module for Scotland/UK work. Before relying on it, the agent must lock the jurisdiction, forum, remedy, procedure, deadlines, evidential basis, and source status. Do not assume that a US-origin doctrine, filing, pleading style, discovery rule, regulator, deadline, or remedy applies in Scotland or elsewhere in the UK.

### Mandatory operating rules

1. **Jurisdiction lock.** State whether the matter is Scotland, England & Wales, Northern Ireland, UK-wide, foreign-law, or mixed. If Scotland is plausible, distinguish sheriff court, Court of Session, tribunals, regulators, ombudsmen, and internal institutional processes.
2. **Official-source hierarchy.** Prefer legislation.gov.uk, Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service rules/forms, Court of Session and sheriff court rules, tribunal/regulator guidance, UK Supreme Court materials, GOV.UK, Scottish Government, ICO, FCA, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, Land Register of Scotland, registers of Scotland, and other primary public sources. Treat secondary commentary as orientation only.
3. **Live verification.** Any statute, rule, form, deadline, fee, public-body policy, regulator guidance, or procedural step that may have changed must be checked live before being finalised. Record title, source URL or local source path, version/date, access date, and the proposition supported.
4. **Evidence discipline.** Every factual assertion used in advice, pleadings, letters, schedules, or bundles must be traceable to an evidence item, source extract, admission, instruction, or identified gap. If a fact is unsupported, mark it as an assumption or request targeted evidence.
5. **Element-by-element reasoning.** Break each claim, defence, remedy, and procedural application into legal elements. Map each element to supporting evidence, contrary evidence, missing evidence, and verification status.
6. **Autonomous depth.** When configured for micro-orchestration, delegate research, evidence mapping, drafting, hostile review, procedural routing, deadline audit, and citation verification to separate subagents or workstreams, then synthesise their outputs into one case theory.
7. **External-action boundary.** Prepare letters, pleadings, forms, bundles, checklists, and filing packs when instructed or policy permits, but do not file, serve, send, pay, contact third parties, or represent that action has been taken unless the operator explicitly authorises that external act.
8. **Uncertainty handling.** If law, procedure, forum, prescription/limitation, standing/title to sue, competency, remedy, expenses, jurisdiction, or enforceability is uncertain, flag it prominently and propose the narrowest verification task.

### Expected work product

Where proportionate, produce a chronology, issue map, source log, evidence matrix, merits/risk table, remedy/damages table, procedural route note, draft document, bundle index, service/filing checklist, and operator handoff note. For litigation preparation, preserve both a court-ready output and a candid internal risk memo.

Produces a petition challenging lawfulness of detention, formatted for the Scottish courts (High Court of Justiciary or Court of Session), with grounds under domestic and Convention law.

[SCOTS: This skill has been adapted from US federal habeas corpus practice to the Scottish legal system. Scotland has no direct equivalent of AEDPA or 28 U.S.C. §§ 2241 to 2255. Habeas corpus relief exists through multiple routes, Bill of Suspension (criminal), petition to the _nobile officium_ (exceptional cases), and judicial review of detention (Article 5 ECHR / Human Rights Act 1998). The core structural approach to challenging detention is preserved, but the procedural framework is fundamentally different.]

## Prerequisites

1. **Conviction/detention records** - complaint, indictment, sentence, charges, and court reference numbers (e.g. sheriffdom / case number)
2. **Procedural history** - note of appeal, stated case, or prior Bill of Suspension outcomes at each level (Sheriff Appeal Court / High Court of Justiciary)
3. **Trial record** - transcripts, productions, minutes of proceedings, evidence relevant to claimed violations
4. **Custody details** - prison, prisoner number (HMP), name of governor or custodial officer
5. **Exhaustion of remedies** - proof that normal appellate routes have been exhausted or would be inadequate
6. **Time bar compliance** - applicable time limits under _Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995_ or _Prescription and Limitation (Scotland) Act 1973_; or justification for delay in exceptional cases

## Quick Start

1. Identify the appropriate Scottish remedy from the detention and conviction posture (see below).
2. Verify time limits, Article 5(4) ECHR requires prompt determination; delays require explanation.
3. Confirm exhaustion of normal appellate remedies or justify why direct challenge via habeas is necessary.
4. Draft each ground with its own legal basis (Convention right / domestic law / devolution issue), authority, and record citations.
5. Assemble petition sections per output structure below.

### Choosing the Correct Remedy

| Posture | Scottish Remedy | Court |
|---------|----------------|-------|
| Convicted, procedural irregularity in criminal proceedings | Bill of Suspension | High Court of Justiciary (criminal appeal) |
| Detention without conviction, defective warrant, no probable cause | Bill of Suspension | High Court of Justiciary |
| Post-conviction, new evidence / procedural bar renders detention unlawful | Petition to the _nobile officium_ | High Court of Justiciary (in exceptional circumstances only) |
| Detention by public body (immigration, mental health, extradition) | Judicial Review / Petition for Judicial Review | Court of Session (Outer House) |
| Violation of Article 5 ECHR (right to liberty) | Compatibility challenge under s. 57(2) Scotland Act 1998 or s. 6 Human Rights Act 1998 | Court of Session or High Court of Justiciary |
| Detention under unlawful legislation / ultra vires act | Devolution issue / reduction | Court of Session or Supreme Court (on devolution issue) |

### I. Introduction

Single paragraph: petitioner identity, detention/conviction details (date, court, charges, sentence), legal basis for challenge.

### II. Parties

- **Petitioner / Complainer**: full name, aliases, prisoner number, HMP / place of detention
- **Respondent**: the authority with legal custody (Governor of HMP, Scottish Ministers, Procurator Fiscal, UK Government department as appropriate)
- **Real parties in interest**: Lord Advocate / Advocate General for Scotland where devolution issues or compatibility challenges arise

### III. Jurisdiction and Competency

State the basis of the court's jurisdiction:

| Challenge Type | Basis of Jurisdiction | Competency Considerations |
|----------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Bill of Suspension | _nobile officium_ of the High Court of Justiciary / common law | Must raise procedural irregularity or oppression not remediable by appeal |
| Petition to _nobile officium_ | _nobile officium_ - reserved for exceptional cases where no other remedy exists | Narrow scope, court will not ordinarily intervene where normal appeal routes are available |
| Judicial Review of detention | Court of Session Act 1988, s. 27A; Rules of the Court of Session Ch 58 | Must have sufficient interest; time limit generally 3 months |
| Convention rights challenge | Human Rights Act 1998, s. 6 (unlawful for public authority to act incompatibly) | Must show petitioner is a victim (s. 7) |
| Devolution issue | Scotland Act 1998, Sch 6; s. 57(2) (Lord Advocate acts within devolved competence) | Must specify whether the act is ultra vires or incompatible with Convention rights |

Address Article 5 ECHR rights (right to liberty and security):

| Article 5 Element | Scottish Application |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| 5(1) - Lawful detention | Detention must be on one of the listed grounds and in accordance with a procedure prescribed by law |
| 5(2) - Reasons for arrest | Person arrested must be informed promptly of reasons and charge |
| 5(3) - Prompt production / bail | Must be brought promptly before a judge; entitlement to trial within reasonable time or release pending trial |
| 5(4) - Review of lawfulness | Right to take proceedings to challenge lawfulness of detention speedily |
| 5(5) - Compensation | Right to compensation for unlawful detention in violation of Article 5 |

### IV. Statement of Facts

Chronological narrative covering:
- Circumstances of arrest / detention (including whether warrant was obtained, basis for suspicion)
- Pre-trial proceedings (bail applications, custody time limits, preliminary hearings)
- Trial type (solemn / summary), evidence, verdict (by jury or sheriff)
- Sentencing (nature and length of sentence; minimum / mandatory provisions)
- Appeal history, court (Sheriff Appeal Court / High Court of Justiciary), case number, decision date, outcome, Any stated case or note of appeal previously lodged, Any prior petitions to the _nobile officium_ if applicable

### V. Exhaustion of Normal Remedies

| Ground | First Instance | Appeal Level | Final Appeal | Status |
|--------|---------------|--------------|--------------|--------|
| 1 | [Sheriff Court / High Court / date] | [Sheriff Appeal Court / date / outcome] | [High Court / date] | Exhausted / Excused |

If remedies are unexhausted, argue why the normal appellate route was inadequate or why the case falls within the exceptional _nobile officium_ jurisdiction. The _nobile officium_ is not available as an alternative to a regular appeal, it requires a "distinct and exceptional" reason.

### VI. Grounds for Relief

Number each ground separately. Per ground:

```
GROUND [N]: [Right violated]
Legal basis: [Convention article / domestic statute / common law]
Controlling authority: [Scottish / UK / ECtHR precedent]
Facts: [Specific record citations / productions]
How the detention is unlawful: [Analysis]
Prejudice / consequence: [What flows from the violation, conviction unsafe, detention invalid, damages]
```

**Common grounds and Scottish/UK authorities:**

| Ground | Scottish / UK / ECtHR Authority |
|--------|---------------------------------|
| Unlawful arrest / no reasonable suspicion | _O'Hara v Chief Constable of the RUC_ [1997] UKHL 6; Art 5(1)(c) ECHR |
| Detention without charge, custody time limit exceeded | _Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995_, s. 22 to 23; _Maguire v HMA_, 2003 SCCR 758 |
| Ineffective legal assistance | _Anderson v HMA_, 1996 SCCR 435; _McIntosh v HMA_, 1998 JC 135; _N v HMA_ 2003 SCCR 448 |
| Breach of right to silence / fair trial | Art 6 ECHR; _McLean v HMA_, 2010 JC 1; _Cadder v HMA_ [2010] UKSC 43 |
| Wrongful conviction, new evidence | _Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995_, s. 106; SCCRC reference; _nobile officium_ only if SCCRC route exhausted |
| Unlawful sentence, minimum / mandatory sentence challenge | _R v Smith_ [2005] UKHL 60; Art 7 ECHR (no punishment without law); devolution issue challenge |
| Incompatibility with Convention rights | Human Rights Act 1998, s. 4 (declaration of incompatibility); Scotland Act 1998, s. 29(2)(d) |
| Detention in breach of Article 5 procedural rights | _Brogan v UK_ (1988) 11 EHRR 117; _Assenov v Bulgaria_ (1998) 28 EHRR 652; _S, V and A v Denmark_ [2018] ECHR 209 |

### VII. Legal Argument

For each ground:

1. State the legal right engaged (Article 5 ECHR / Article 6 ECHR / common law / statute)
2. Identify the applicable standard, Scots common law, ECHR jurisprudence, or domestic statutory provision
3. Show how the respondent's act/omission violated that right
4. Demonstrate that the violation goes to the lawfulness of detention or safety of conviction
5. Address any limitation or justification relied upon by the respondent (e.g. public interest, national security; proportionality analysis where relevant)
6. Distinguish contrary authority, if any

**Key differences from US habeas practice:**

[SCOTS: Note, Unlike US hubcap practice under AEDPA:]
- There is no one-year limitations period equivalent to AEDPA; time limits depend on the remedy (summary: 10-day time limit for timeous Bill of Suspension; discretion for late presentation). For judicial review, the general time limit is 3 months (RCS 58.14(3)). Delay may be a bar to the _nobile officium_.
- There is no "deference" standard equivalent to AEDPA § 2254(d) in Scottish criminal appeals; the appeal court decides whether the conviction is safe / the sentence is excessive.
- The _nobile officium_ is an extraordinary equitable remedy, not a routine post-conviction tool. It is narrower than AEDPA second or successive petitions.
- There is no "exhaustion of state remedies" requirement in the US sense, but an applicant must generally show they have used available domestic remedies before invoking the _nobile officium_ or Convention rights.

### VIII. Plea in Law / Prayer for Relief

- For Bill of Suspension: crave that the detention be suspended and the petitioner liberated _simpliciter_, or that the warrant be "restricted" / modified, For Petition to _nobile officium_: crave the court to exercise its _nobile officium_ to grant such remedy as justice requires, For Judicial Review: crave reduction of the detention order, declarator that detention is unlawful, damages for unlawful detention, or interim liberation, For Convention challenge: crave declarator of incompatibility, damages under s. 8 HRA 1998, or such other relief as the court deems just

Include a prayer for expenses (judicial expenses following success, the general rule in Scottish litigation).

### IX. Verification and Signature

Signed by the petitioner personally (or solicitor on instruction). In the Court of Session, a Petition for Judicial Review must include a statement of service. Include full name, solicitor's reference where represented, and contact details.

## Guidelines

- **Convention rights are central** - Article 5 ECHR (right to liberty) is the primary vehicle for most detention challenges in Scotland post-HRA 1998. Frame every argument with reference to both domestic law and Convention jurisprudence.
- **Use only binding authority** - UK Supreme Court, High Court of Justiciary, and Court of Session decisions are binding. ECtHR decisions are "take into account" (s. 2 HRA 1998) but highly persuasive.
- **Check devolution competency** - if challenging an act of the Lord Advocate or Scottish Ministers, consider whether a devolution issue arises under Scotland Act 1998 Sch 6.
- **Document time limits scrupulously** - late Bills of Suspension require explanation; judicial review has a 3-month time limit (RCS 58.14(3)); the _nobile officium_ requires exceptional circumstances and delay is a bar.
- **Keep grounds distinct** - do not combine Convention rights and domestic law violations in a single ground unless they are co-extensive.
- **Cite the record for all factual assertions** - productions numbers, minutes of proceedings, transcript pages.
- **Comply with rules of court** - RCS Ch 58 for judicial review, Act of Adjournal for criminal procedure.
- **Mark unverified citations with [VERIFY]**.

## Troubleshooting

- **Missed statutory appeal time limit**: A Bill of Suspension is competent even late if it is "timeous in the circumstances" - the court has discretion. The petitioner must explain the delay fully. Alternatively, a petition to the _nobile officium_ may be available if the circumstances are truly exceptional, but it is not a substitute for a late appeal.
- **No existing appellate remedy** (e.g. guilty plea, or out of time): Consider SCCRC referral (s. 194C to 194L of the _Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995_) or the _nobile officium_ in truly exceptional circumstances. The _nobile officium_ does not create a general safety-net jurisdiction.
- **Immigration / extradition detention**: This is a separate statutory regime. Challenge via Judicial Review in the Court of Session (Outer House) or by petition to the _nobile officium_ of the High Court of Justiciary (narrower scope). Consider UK Borders Act 2007, Immigration Act 2016, and Extradition Act 2003.
- **Detention under mental health legislation**: Challenge detention under the _Mental Health (Care and Treatment) (Scotland) Act 2003_ via the Mental Health Tribunal for Scotland, with judicial review as fallback. The _nobile officium_ is available in exceptional cases.
- **Article 5(4) delay in review**: If the prisoner's challenge to detention has not been heard promptly, raise this as a separate ground under Article 5(4) ECHR, the remedy must be speedily obtainable.
- **Cadder v HMA [2010] UKSC 43 implications**: Where pre-trial confession evidence was obtained without access to a solicitor, the conviction may be unsafe. This ground is now largely settled but remains relevant for historical cases.

## Scotland/UK Adaptation

This skill has been substantially adapted from US federal habeas corpus practice under AEDPA (28 U.S.C. §§ 2241, 2254, 2255) to the Scottish legal system.

### Key Differences

| US Concept | Scottish/UK Equivalent |
|------------|----------------------|
| Federal habeas corpus (28 U.S.C. §§ 2241, 2254, 2255) | Bill of Suspension (criminal) / Petition to _nobile officium_ / Judicial Review |
| AEDPA one-year limitation (§ 2244(d)) | No equivalent; time limits depend on remedy (summary: 10 days for Bill of Suspension; 3 months for judicial review; no fixed limit for _nobile officium_ but delay is a bar) |
| AEDPA deference (§ 2254(d) - contrary to / unreasonable application) | No equivalent; appeal court decides safety of conviction afresh |
| US Constitution (Art. I, § 9; 4th, 5th, 6th, 8th, 14th Amendments) | European Convention on Human Rights (Art 5, 6) / Human Rights Act 1998 / common law |
| SCOTUS precedent | UK Supreme Court / High Court of Justiciary / Court of Session / ECtHR |
| State exhaustion / Rose v Lundy | Normal appellate remedies must be exhausted; _nobile officium_ is exceptional |
| Successive petition bar (§ 2244(b)) | _Nobile officium_ is inherently limited, not a routine second-tier remedy |
| Circuit court authorisation for successive petitions | SCCRC (Scottish Criminal Cases Review Commission) reference as gateway for fresh challenges |
| Federal district court | Court of Session (Outer House for judicial review) / High Court of Justiciary (criminal bills) |
| United States District Court venue | Detention in Scotland = single jurisdiction; all Scottish courts sit nationally |
| Prosecutorial authority | COPFS (Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service); Lord Advocate |
| Brecht harmless error standard | "Miscarriage of justice" / "unsafe conviction" test; no _Brecht_ equivalent |

### Procedural Summary

1. **Criminal detention after conviction**: Normal route is statutory appeal to the Sheriff Appeal Court or High Court of Justiciary under the _Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995_. Bill of Suspension is available where the normal appeal is inadequate or for procedural irregularities. Petition to the _nobile officium_ is a last resort.

2. **Pre-trial / warrant-based detention**: Bill of Suspension to challenge defective warrants, unlawful arrest, or oppression in obtaining detention. Consider actions for damages for wrongful detention.

3. **Administrative detention (immigration, mental health)**: Judicial Review in the Court of Session under Ch 58 of the Rules of the Court of Session.

4. **Convention rights challenges**: All of the above may incorporate arguments under the Human Rights Act 1998. Devolution issues under the Scotland Act 1998 add an additional layer.

5. **Damages**: Compensation for unlawful detention is available under domestic law (wrongous imprisonment) or s. 8 HRA 1998. No punitive damages (see reference guide).
