## Required Inputs

- **Indictment** - the indictment to be challenged (solemn procedure)
- **Statutes libelled** - particulars of offence, statutory provisions cited
- **Procedural timeline** - date of alleged offence, date of indictment, first diet, service

3. Draft using the output structure below.
4. If factual disputes exist, include a request for a hearing.
5. Verify all citations or mark `[VERIFY]`.

## Grounds for Challenge

### Defective Indictment

- Fails to state the time and place of the alleged offence (requirement under s. 57(2) Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 - "time and place must be specified with reasonable certainty")
- Missing essential particulars, the nature of the charge, acts, omissions, or circumstances (s. 58)
- Relevancy challenge, the facts libelled do not constitute an offence known to the law of Scotland, Duplicity, a single charge libels more than one offence (the conjunctive rule)
- Vague or insufficient specification, the accused cannot know the case they require to meet, Objection to the form of the indictment (formal invalidity)

### Time Bar

**Statutory time limits (solemn procedure):**
- Sheriff Court (sheriff and jury) - indictment must be served within 12 months of the date of the offence (s. 136 Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995); trial must commence within 12 months of first appearance, continue: the court may extend if good cause shown, High Court of Justiciary, no absolute time bar for solemn procedure except the 12-month rule (s. 65): trial must commence within 12 months of the accused's first appearance (bail undertaking or committal); extension only where "good cause" shown by the Crown, Common law time bar, for certain historical offences, distinct from statutory limits, Summar diligence periods are shorter, solemn procedure subject to s. 65

**Article 6 ECHR, reasonable time:** Art 6(1) requires trial within a reasonable time; breach may not found dismissal alone but can be a factor in oppression or mitigation of sentence.

### Oppression (Scottish Equitable Plea)

- **Personal bar** - *res judicata* / earlier proceedings for the same matter finally disposed of
- **Mora, taciturnity, and acquiescence** - excessive and unexplained delay by the Crown where the accused has changed their position to their detriment
- **Abuse of process** - conduct by the prosecution that makes a fair trial impossible or is otherwise unfair (e.g. destruction of evidence, lost productions)
- *Note: the Scottish plea of oppression is broader than the US due process / misconduct motion; it encompasses harassment, prejudice, and unfairness in the summary or solemn process*

### Double Jeopardy (*Ne Bis In Idem*) - Plea in Bar of Trial

- Prior acquittal or conviction for the same offence (per Double Jeopardy (Scotland) Act 2011 - expanded to cover retrials after acquittal only in very limited circumstances)
- *Res judicata* - a prior summary complaint or indictment that has been finally disposed of, where the new allegation is substantially the same
- **Section 1 of the 2011 Act:** a person who has been acquitted or convicted may not be prosecuted again for the same or substantially the same offence, with narrow exceptions (tainted acquittals, new evidence, ss. 3 to 5)
- **Collateral estoppel** - not a separate ground in Scotland; subsumed within *res judicata*

### Misconduct by the Crown

- **Precognition irregularities** - improper pressure on witnesses; inappropriate conduct by procurator fiscal or Crown counsel
- **Selective prosecution** - the Crown's discretion to prosecute is rarely reviewable; only where *mala fides* (bad faith) or discrimination can be shown
- **Crown failure to disclose** - duty under the current disclosure regime (Scottish Criminal Justice Reform); failure to disclose material evidence may found oppression or a continuation
- **Vindictive prosecution** - *mala fides* required; high threshold

### Jurisdictional / Competency Defects

- **Wrong court** - the indictment has been laid in the wrong court (Sheriff Court when it should be High Court, or vice versa); the court lacks jurisdiction over the offence
- **Lack of territorial jurisdiction** - the alleged offence occurred outside Scotland and no statutory extension applies
- **Superior court proceedings** - the case should have been proceeded summarily but was indicted (high-level summary-only matter sent to solemn); this is a matter of competency

## Output Structure

1. **Heading** - criminal case heading, court, case reference; title: "Minute for the Accused: Plea in Bar of Trial / Minute to Challenge Indictment"
2. **Introduction** - charges at issue, ground(s) for challenge, order(s) sought (desert simpliciter, desert pro loco et tempore, or amendment)
3. **Factual Background** - procedural and factual history from the record (dates of offence, indictment, service, first appearance, etc.)
4. **Legal Argument** - for each ground: (a) legal standard with authority, (b) application to the facts, (c) prejudice to the accused, (d) remedy sought
5. **Evidentiary Hearing Request** (if factual disputes exist) - identify issues and productions
6. **Conclusion** - state the order sought, desert simpliciter (absolute dismissal, no further prosecution) or desert pro loco et tempore (dismissal with permission to re-indict)

## Pitfalls and Checks

- **File early** - certain objections (relevancy, time bar, oppression) must be raised at first diet or preliminary diet; otherwise they may be treated as abandoned
- **Relevancy vs. specification** - a relevancy challenge attacks whether the facts libelled constitute an offence; a specification challenge attacks whether the accused has enough detail to prepare a defence
- **Time bar timelines** - build a detailed chronology of dates (offence, complaint, indictment, first appearance, service); the court will scrutinise extensions under s. 65
- **Oppression is fact-specific** - assess candidly; mere delay without prejudice rarely succeeds
- **Double jeopardy** - the Double Jeopardy (Scotland) Act 2011 narrows exceptions; assess fitness carefully
- **Crown misconduct is a high bar** - must show *mala fides* or actual prejudice; constructive submission before trial is critical
- **Citation hygiene** - verify every case cite or flag `[VERIFY]`; use current Scottish Criminal Case Reports / Justiciary Reports
- **Strategic fit** - consider whether a submission of no case to answer at close of Crown case (oath or no oaths) is a stronger route than pre-trial challenge

## Scotland/UK Adaptation

This skill has been adapted for use under Scottish criminal procedure. Key differences from the original US approach:

| US Concept | Scottish/UK Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Indictment (federal / state) | Scottish indictment (solemn procedure, High Court of Justiciary or Sheriff Court with jury) |
| Motion to dismiss / FRCP 7 / 12(b) | Preliminary diet (s. 71 to 76 Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995); first diet and preliminary diet in solemn procedure |
| Motion to suppress evidence | Not a standalone motion, challenge admissibility during trial or by preliminary diet; Scotland uses trial within a trial / voir dire where necessary |
| Fifth Amendment (double jeopardy) | Double Jeopardy (Scotland) Act 2011; *ne bis in idem*; plea in bar of trial |
| Grand jury indictment | No US-style grand jury, the Crown (COPFS) decides whether to indict by petition → indictment process |
| *Barker v. Wingo* (Sixth Amendment speedy trial) | s. 65 Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 (12-month rule for solemn); Article 6 ECHR reasonable time; common law oppression (mora) |
| Speedy Trial Act (18 U.S.C. §§ 3161 to 3174) | s. 65 (solemn); s. 136 (sheriff and jury); no exact equivalent, Scottish time limits are statutory but more flexible |
| Due Process / equal protection | Article 6 & 14 ECHR; common law oppression / *mala fides* |
| *Blockburger* test | Scottish test: *Cox v. HM Advocate* / *Buchan v. HM Advocate* - same facts as essential element; Double Jeopardy (Scotland) Act 2011 s. 1 |
| Prosecutorial misconduct, selective/vindictive | Crown Office misconduct, must show *mala fides*; high threshold |
| With or without prejudice | Desert simpliciter (with prejudice, no further prosecution) vs. desert pro loco et tempore (without prejudice, may re-indict) |
| Federal / state court | High Court of Justiciary / Sheriff Court (solemn) |
| USD costs awards | GBP (£); costs limited in Scottish criminal procedure, legal aid ordinarily covers defence |

The fundamental structural difference is the absence of a grand jury: the Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service (COPFS) decides on indictment after precognition. Preliminary diets are not automatic, they require a ground of challenge to be stated in advance. The three-verdict system also affects plea strategy: a submission of no case to answer may result in the Crown dropping the charge even if the indictment was initially sound.
