### Expected work product

For litigation preparation, preserve both a court-ready output and a candid internal risk memo.

Drafts a pre-trial objection to exclude specific evidence, testimony, or productions from being presented to the jury or sheriff. Structures minute, factual basis, legal argument, and proposed order per Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 and the Act of Adjournal (Criminal Procedure Rules) 1996.

**Note on terminology**: The US "motion in limine" has no direct Scottish equivalent. In Scottish practice, evidence objections are raised as:
- **Preliminary plea** (sheriff court or High Court) - a plea-in-law raising a legal issue
- **Minute of objection** - written objection to admissibility of specified evidence
- **Submission of no case to answer** - Crown evidence insufficient in law (s. 97, CP(S)A 1995)
- **Preliminary hearing** (High Court of Justiciary, Chapter 9B of the Criminal Procedure Rules) - addresses case management and evidence admissibility issues before trial
- **First diet** (sheriff court solemn procedure) - evidence admissibility challenges

## Prerequisites

Gather before drafting:

- **Case documents** - indictment/complaint, Crown statements, list of productions (including any CCTV, forensic, or documents), special defences and notices (alibi, incrimination, self-defence, etc.)
- **Target evidence** - specific evidence, witness testimony, or productions to exclude
- **Jurisdiction** - High Court of Justiciary or Sheriff Court (summary or solemn); local practice rules
- **Procedural stage** - first diet / preliminary hearing / trial diet
- **Prior rulings** - earlier judicial rulings on evidence admissibility; any section 76(1) application in the sheriff court

## Quick Start

1. Identify the evidence to exclude and the strongest exclusion ground under Scottish evidence law
2. Draft the minute of objection / preliminary plea with a descriptive title
3. Write a concise introduction (what evidence, what rule, why)
4. Build factual basis citing the Crown statements and productions
5. Argue each exclusion ground under a separate heading with reference to case law
6. Close with prayer and crave for relief

## Output Structure

### Procedural Heading & Title

Sheriffdom and Court (for Sheriff Court) or High Court of Justiciary (with seat: Edinburgh, Glasgow, Aberdeen, etc.), case reference number, parties per the indictment/precognition. Descriptive title naming the specific evidence or issue.

### Introduction (1 paragraph)

| Element | Content |
|---|---|
| What to exclude | Specific evidence, production, or Crown witness testimony |
| Legal ground | Section of CP(S)A 1995, Act of Adjournal, or common law rule |
| Why | One-sentence prejudice/inadmissibility rationale |

### Statement of Facts

- Neutral recitation of facts relevant to admissibility only, Cite Crown statements and productions by production number, Organise chronologically or topically for clarity, Include procedural history only if relevant to the admissibility question

### Legal Argument

Argue each exclusion ground under a separate sub-heading. Always argue alternative grounds where available.

| Ground | Rule | Key Elements |
|---|---|---|
| Irrelevance / insufficient corroboration | Common law / CP(S)A 1995 s. 78 | Evidence does not relate to a fact in issue; Crown lacks minimum two sources for each crucial fact |
| Prejudice outweighs probative value | Common law - *Mackenzie v HM Advocate* 1995 SLT 505 | Probative value outweighed by prejudicial effect (especially character/propensity evidence, s. 255 CP(S)A sheriff-solemn, s. 266(4)) |
| Character / prior convictions | CP(S)A 1995, ss. 255, 266 to 267, 268 (solemn); s. 101 (summary) | Improper character reference; bad character admitted only in limited circumstances (s. 255(1)-(2)); no US-style FRE 404(b) |
| Hearsay | Common law, Crawford, not codified; Norris v HM Advocate 1991 SCCR 266; s. 259 to 261 CP(S)A (solemn) for circumstances where hearsay is admissible | Out-of-court statement offered as evidence of truth; exceptions: dying declarations (s. 260), s. 261 (non-availability of witness), hearsay in affidavits |
| Hearsay (statutory exceptions) | CP(S)A 1995 ss. 259 to 261 | Witness unavailable, fear, dead, incapable, abroad, not found; prior statement may be admissible; s. 263(4) (adverse witness evidence) |
| Expert evidence | Common law - *HM Advocate v M* 2013; *Ritchie v HM Advocate* 2017 SCCR 125; no Daubert in Scotland | Qualification; relevance to issue; field of expertise; ultimate issue rule (non-binding but caution) |
| Conjunct evidence / unfair admission | Common law - *HM Advocate v H* 2011 | Prejudicial admission that is not relevant to a fact in issue |
| Unfairly obtained evidence | Common law - *Lawrie v Muir* 1950 JC 19; *HM Advocate v Turnbull* 1951 JC 96 | Evidence obtained illegally/irregularly; court may exclude if admission would be unfair |
| Identification evidence | Common law / CP(S)A 1995 ss. 280, 281 | Turnbull warnings (identification parade and positive ID); dock identification prohibited unless personal knowledge |
| Crown non-disclosure / delay | CP(S)A 1995 s. 121; *Chalmers v HM Advocate* 1954 JC 66; *McDonald v HM Advocate* 2005 | Crown duty to disclose all material information to the defence; s. 121 (duty to list productions); s. 113 (time bar in summary proceedings) |
| Special defences / notice | CP(S)A 1995 s. 78 | Alibi, incrimination, self-defence, automatism, diminished responsibility, must be lodged 14 days before first diet in solemn |

For each ground:

1. State legal standard with controlling Scottish case authority
2. Apply facts to each element
3. Distinguish or distinguish anticipated Crown arguments
4. Rebut likely Crown counter-arguments

### Conclusion

2-3 sentences synthesising key arguments. No new facts or citations. Frame exclusion as the legally required outcome based on Scottish criminal evidence law.

### Crave (Prayer for Relief)

Request: (1) sustain the plea / grant the minute, (2) exclude the specified evidence, (3) ordain the Crown not to lead or refer to the specified evidence, (4) sist procedure if appropriate, (5) all as the court deems appropriate.

### Signature Block & Service Details

Defence solicitor/advocate name, firm (if applicable), address, phone, email. Date of lodging. Certificate that copy served on Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service (COPFS) and any co-accused representatives.

## Pitfalls & Checks

- **Jurisdiction**: High Court preliminary hearings are governed by Chapter 9B of the Act of Adjournal (Criminal Procedure Rules) 1996; Sheriff Court first diets by Chapter 9 of the same Rules
- **Timing**: Preliminary pleas to the relevancy of the indictment must be lodged at first diet or preliminary hearing, do not leave to trial diet
- **Timing (evidence objections)**: Most evidence objections can be raised at trial (best practice: raise at first diet/preliminary hearing)
- **No codified evidence code**: Scottish evidence law is a mix of common law and CP(S)A 1995 provisions, there is no equivalent to the FRE or the US Federal Rules
- **Corroboration requirement**: Except where statute provides otherwise, Scottish criminal law requires corroboration, two independent sources for each crucial fact; corroboration is the single most important evidential distinction from US practice
- **Citations**: In Scottish High Court, cite as (Year) JC [page]; Sheriff Court appeal cases; House of Lords/Supreme Court Scottish appeals
- **Uncertain citations**: Mark with `[VERIFY]` any citation not confirmed against the source record
- **Record support**: Every factual assertion cites the production number or witness statement reference
- **Formatting**: Comply with court-specific margins, font, line spacing, and electronic case file (ECF) conventions for the Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service

## Scotland/UK Adaptation

This skill has been adapted from its original US-focused version (FRE-based criminal motions in limine) for use under Scottish criminal procedure and evidence law.

### Key US-to-UK/Scottish Conversions

| US Term | Scottish Equivalent |
|---------|-------------------|
| Motion in limine | Preliminary plea / Minute of objection / Preliminary hearing argument |
| FRE (Federal Rules of Evidence) | Scottish common law of evidence + Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 |
| FRE 401-402 (relevance) | Common law, evidence must be relevant to a fact in issue; no codified relevance rule |
| FRE 403 (unfair prejudice) | Common law - *Mackenzie v HM Advocate* 1995 SLT 505 (probative value vs. prejudicial effect) |
| FRE 404(b) (other crimes/character) | CP(S)A 1995 ss. 255, 266 to 268 and common law, character evidence restricted but different categorisation to FRE |
| FRE 801-807 (hearsay) | Common law hearsay rule + statutory exceptions ss. 259 to 261 CP(S)A 1995 - not codified like FRE |
| FRE 702-703 (expert testimony) | Common law, no Daubert; expert must be qualified, opinion within recognised field, relevance established |
| Daubert / Frye standard | Not applicable, Scottish courts apply common law test: relevant + qualified expert + reliable field of expertise |
| FRCP (Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure) | Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995; Act of Adjournal (Criminal Procedure Rules) 1996 |
| US District Court | High Court of Justiciary (solemn, murder, rape, serious crime) or Sheriff Court (solemn or summary) |
| Indictment (federal) | Indictment (High Court) or Complaint (sheriff summary) / Petition (sheriff solemn) |
| US jury (12 persons, unanimous) | Scottish jury: 15 persons (solemn); majority verdict (8 or more) |
| Bluebook citation format | OSCOLA (Oxford Standard for Citation of Legal Authorities) for Scottish law; (Year) JC [page] for Justiciary Cases |
| US Constitution / Bill of Rights (4th, 5th, 6th, 8th Amendments) | Human Rights Act 1998 (incorporating ECHR Art. 5, 6, 8) - UK equivalent of US constitutional protections |
| Discovery / Brady disclosure | *Chalmers v HM Advocate* 1954 JC 66 (Crown duty of disclosure); CP(S)A 1995 s. 121 |
| Pre-trial / Status Conference | First diet (sheriff court solemen) or Preliminary hearing (High Court) |
| Certificate of service | Service on COPFS + co-accused representatives; electronic case file (ECF) |
| No case to answer / judgment of acquittal | Submission of no case to answer (s. 97 CP(S)A 1995) - must be made at close of Crown case |

### Procedural Framework

- **Primary legislation**: Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 (CP(S)A); Act of Adjournal (Criminal Procedure Rules) 1996
- **Evidence law**: Uncodified, Scottish common law supplemented by CP(S)A 1995, ss. 94 to 166 (search warrants, corroboration, hearsay exceptions, character evidence, witnesses)
- **The corroboration requirement**: The single biggest difference. Scottish law requires corroborated evidence for each crucial fact in criminal cases, unless a statutory exception applies. The Crown must lead evidence from at least two independent sources for each essential fact.
- **Human Rights Act 1998**: Article 6 (right to a fair trial) underpins Scottish criminal procedure, similar to US 6th Amendment but with ECHR jurisprudence
- **Competent evidence**: Two fundamental admissibility tests: (a) does the evidence relate to a fact in issue; (b) would its probative value outweigh any prejudicial effect

### Practitioner Notes

- Scottish criminal practitioners (solicitors and advocates) appear in both the High Court and Sheriff Court, There is no "motion in limine" as a discrete US-style pre-trial motion, evidence issues are typically raised at first diet / preliminary hearing or during trial by objection, The most common pre-trial applications in Scottish criminal practice are: (a) preliminary plea to the relevancy of the indictment; (b) minute of objection to particular evidence; (c) application for disclosure under *Chalmers* / s. 121; (d) objection to delay / oppression, Scottish criminal trials have no civil equivalent of discovery/depositions, disclosure is through Crown statements and productions, The Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service (COPFS) is the Scottish public prosecutor, equivalent to the US DOJ / Federal Prosecutor's Office, Scottish juries have 15 members (solemn procedure) and may return majority verdicts (8 for conviction, 7 for acquittal) - unique to Scottish criminal law
