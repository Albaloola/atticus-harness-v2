4. **Evidence discipline.** Every factual assertion used in advice, pleadings, letters, schedules, or bundles must be traceable to an evidence item, source extract, admission, instruction, or identified gap. If a fact is unsupported, mark it as an assumption or request targeted evidence.

Drafts an enforceable clause limiting a licensee's exploitation of licensed IP to a defined permitted field.

## Prerequisites

Gather before drafting:

1. **Licensed IP** - patent numbers/claims, know-how description, or trade secret scope (consider UK Patents Act 1977 and common law confidence)
2. **Permitted field** - industry, application, customer type, geography, or combination
3. **Restricted fields** - express list of excluded uses/markets (infer if not provided)
4. **License structure** - exclusive vs. non-exclusive; sub-license rights; term
5. **Party information** - licensor, licensee, existing licenses or third-party constraints

## Clause Structure

### 1. Definitions

| Term | Guidance |
|------|----------|
| **Licensed Technology** | Patent numbers, claim references, or technical spec language verbatim |
| **Field of Use** | Delineate by: industry sector, therapeutic area, application method, customer class, geography |
| **Permitted Applications** | Affirmative list of approved uses |
| **Restricted Fields** | Exclusive list of prohibited uses; use "including but not limited to" |
| **Licensed Products/Services** | Tie to Permitted Applications; state whether improvements are included |

**Sector-specific delineation:**

| Sector | Typical Method |
|--------|----------------|
| Pharma/biotech | Therapeutic indication, species, delivery route |
| Software | Deployment type, end-user category, geography |
| Manufacturing | Product category, volume thresholds, end-use application |
| Semiconductors | Device class, performance tier, market segment |

### 2. Grant of Licence

```
Licensor hereby grants to Licensee a [exclusive/non-exclusive], non-transferable licence
under [Patent Nos. ___ / the Licensed Technology] solely within the Field of Use to:
  (a) make, have made, use, sell, offer for sale, and import Licensed Products; and
  (b) [practice the Licensed Methods] [use the Licensed Know-How],
subject in each case to the terms and conditions of this Agreement. All rights not
expressly granted are reserved by Licensor.
```

- State whether licence extends to continuations, divisionals, and foreign counterparts, Address whether licensee's field-of-use improvements are licensed back, If sub-licensing is permitted, require sub-licensees to be bound by identical field restrictions

### 3. Restrictions on Use

```
Licensee shall not, directly or indirectly:
  (a) use the Licensed Technology for any purpose outside the Field of Use;
  (b) supply Licensed Technology or components thereof to any third party with knowledge
      or reason to know such party will use them outside the Field of Use;
  (c) grant sub-licences, assign, or otherwise transfer rights to use the Licensed
      Technology outside the Field of Use;
  (d) take any action through Affiliates, contractors, or third-party arrangements
      intended to circumvent the Field of Use restriction.
```

### 4. Monitoring and Audit Rights

- **Records retention**: Licensee maintains usage records for [3 to 5] years (subject to UK GDPR data retention principles)
- **Reports**: Quarterly/annual compliance reports by application and market; tie to royalty statements
- **Audit**: Licensor may audit on [30-day] notice, [once/year], at Licensor's expense unless material discrepancy (>5%) shifts cost to Licensee
- **Cooperation**: Reasonable access to facilities, personnel, and systems

### 5. Remedies for Breach

| Remedy | Provision |
|--------|-----------|
| **Termination** | Immediate for material breach (unauthorised field use is per se material); [30-day] cure for minor breaches |
| **Enhanced royalties** | [2 to 3×] applicable rate on unauthorised field revenues |
| **Accounting for profits** | Profits from Restricted Field exploitation (alternative to damages) |
| **Liquidated damages** | Genuine pre-estimate of loss (Scots law requires this; penalty clauses are unenforceable) |
| **Interdict** | Interim and/or permanent interdict expressly preserved; acknowledge irreparable harm |
| **Post-termination** | Cease Restricted Field use; return/destroy Licensed Technology; assign unauthorised-use IP to Licensor |
| **Survival** | Field-of-use and confidentiality obligations survive termination |

### 6. Governing Law and Dispute Resolution

- **Governing law**: Scots law (recommended for Scottish parties) or English law
- **Dispute resolution**: Arbitration (Scottish Arbitration Centre / ad hoc under Arbitration (Scotland) Act 2010) with technically qualified panel, or the Scottish courts
- **Interdict carve-out**: Licensor retains right to seek interim interdict notwithstanding arbitration clause
- **Judicial expenses**: Each party bears own unless position found substantially unjustified (Scots court has discretion on expenses)

## Drafting Checks

- **Specificity**: Prefer enumerated application lists over broad descriptors, vague fields invite litigation
- **Anti-circumvention**: Always include affiliate/contractor circumvention prohibition
- **Sub-licence pass-through**: Sub-licences must incorporate field restrictions verbatim or by reference
- **FRAND**: If patents carry FRAND/SEP commitments, field-of-use restrictions may conflict - [VERIFY] applicability under *Unwired Planet v Huawei* [2017] EWCA Civ 1655 framework; UK courts apply the same framework following implementation of CJEU judgment in Huawei v ZTE
- **Competition law**: Overly broad restrictions can raise Competition Act 1998, Chapter I concerns - [VERIFY] CMA guidance; Technology Transfer Block Exemption Regulation (TTBER) 316/2014 (retained EU block exemption) applies in UK
- **Exhaustion of rights**: UK patent exhaustion applies under UK law (ss.60 Patents Act 1977); enforceable post-sale restrictions require field-of-use limits in the licence to avoid implied licence/ exhaustion
- **Post-Brexit**: EEA exhaustion no longer applies to the UK (UK has adopted its own exhaustion regime). TTBER remains available as retained EU block exemption but CMA may reach independent conclusions

## Scotland/UK Adaptation

**Court hierarchy:**
- UK Supreme Court (final appeal on UK-wide IP matters)
- Inner House of the Court of Session (civil appeals from Outer House)
- Outer House of the Court of Session (first instance for patent and complex IP matters)
- Sheriff Court (for lower-value IP contract disputes)
- UK Intellectual Property Enterprise Court (IPEC) - part of the High Court of England & Wales, not available in Scotland; Scottish IP matters proceed in the Court of Session

**Key terminology substitutions:**
- Grant of License → Grant of Licence, Licensee / Licensor are retained (standard UK usage)
- Injunction → Interdict; TRO → Interim interdict, Preliminary injunction → Interim interdict, Plaintiff → Pursuer; Defendant → Defender, Sherman Act § 1 → Competition Act 1998, Chapter I prohibition (s.2)
- DOJ/FTC competition guidance → CMA (Competition and Markets Authority) guidance / OFT guidance (historical)
- *Quanta Computer v LG* (US patent exhaustion) → UK exhaustion doctrine: s.60 Patents Act 1977; UK courts apply a narrower exhaustion framework than the US; post-Brexit, UK has its own national exhaustion regime, AAA/JAMS arbitration → Scottish Arbitration Centre / ICS / ad hoc under Arbitration (Scotland) Act 2010
- Governing law (NY, DE, CA) → Scots law (Scotland) or English law (E&W)
- Attorneys' fees → Judicial expenses, USD monetary amounts → Convert at approximately £0.75 per $1
- Punitive damages → Not available in Scots law; all damages are compensatory

**Statutory framework:**
- Patents Act 1977 (c.37) - UK patent law, Competition Act 1998 (c.41), Chapter I, s.2 (agreements preventing, restricting or distorting competition)
- Technology Transfer Block Exemption Regulation (TTBER) 316/2014 - retained EU law, Intellectual Property (Unjustified Threats) Act 2017 - threats provisions, Arbitration (Scotland) Act 2010 (asp 1)
- Contract law: Scots common law of contract (no need for consideration; *pacta sunt servanda*)
- Exhaustion: UK Intellectual Property Office guidance on national exhaustion post-Brexit

**Competition law, additional notes:**
- CMA has concurrent jurisdiction with sector regulators for Competition Act 1998
- Vertical agreements: retained EU Vertical Agreements Block Exemption Regulation but UK has its own regime (SI 2022/1397 replaced the retained EU VBER from June 2022)
- Confidentiality: Scrutiny of 1:1 licence terms by the CMA is rare; field-of-use restrictions are generally pro-competitive if properly scoped

## Foreign-Law / US-Origin Guardrail

This skill may contain inherited US terminology. For Scotland/UK use, translate rather than copy. Examples: discovery is not Scots commission and diligence/recovery of documents; tort is generally delict in Scots civil analysis; summary judgment is not automatically the Scots summary decree test; bankruptcy concepts may map to sequestration, liquidation, administration, or restructuring depending on party and forum; HIPAA/CCPA/SEC/EEOC/FTC/CFPB concepts require UK GDPR, DPA 2018, FCA, ICO, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, tribunal, or sector-regulator mapping as appropriate. If the matter is genuinely US or foreign-law, quarantine the foreign-law analysis and warn that local counsel/source verification is required.
