# Examination Objection Reference, Scotland [SCOTS]

## Atticus UK/Scots Legal Excellence Overlay

Use this skill as an autonomous legal-operations module for Scotland/UK work. Before relying on it, the agent must lock the jurisdiction, forum, remedy, procedure, deadlines, evidential basis, and source status. Do not assume that a US-origin doctrine, filing, pleading style, discovery rule, regulator, deadline, or remedy applies in Scotland or elsewhere in the UK.

### Mandatory operating rules

1. **Jurisdiction lock.** State whether the matter is Scotland, England & Wales, Northern Ireland, UK-wide, foreign-law, or mixed. If Scotland is plausible, distinguish sheriff court, Court of Session, tribunals, regulators, ombudsmen, and internal institutional processes.
2. **Official-source hierarchy.** Prefer legislation.gov.uk, Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service rules/forms, Court of Session and sheriff court rules, tribunal/regulator guidance, UK Supreme Court materials, GOV.UK, Scottish Government, ICO, FCA, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, Land Register of Scotland, registers of Scotland, and other primary public sources. Treat secondary commentary as orientation only.
3. **Live verification.** Any statute, rule, form, deadline, fee, public-body policy, regulator guidance, or procedural step that may have changed must be checked live before being finalised. Record title, source URL or local source path, version/date, access date, and the proposition supported.
4. **Evidence discipline.** Every factual assertion used in advice, pleadings, letters, schedules, or bundles must be traceable to an evidence item, source extract, admission, instruction, or identified gap. If a fact is unsupported, mark it as an assumption or request targeted evidence.
5. **Element-by-element reasoning.** Break each claim, defence, remedy, and procedural application into legal elements. Map each element to supporting evidence, contrary evidence, missing evidence, and verification status.
6. **Autonomous depth.** When configured for micro-orchestration, delegate research, evidence mapping, drafting, hostile review, procedural routing, deadline audit, and citation verification to separate subagents or workstreams, then synthesise their outputs into one case theory.
7. **External-action boundary.** Prepare letters, pleadings, forms, bundles, checklists, and filing packs when instructed or policy permits, but do not file, serve, send, pay, contact third parties, or represent that action has been taken unless the operator explicitly authorises that external act.
8. **Uncertainty handling.** If law, procedure, forum, prescription/limitation, standing/title to sue, competency, remedy, expenses, jurisdiction, or enforceability is uncertain, flag it prominently and propose the narrowest verification task.

### Expected work product

Where proportionate, produce a chronology, issue map, source log, evidence matrix, merits/risk table, remedy/damages table, procedural route note, draft document, bundle index, service/filing checklist, and operator handoff note. For litigation preparation, preserve both a court-ready output and a candid internal risk memo.

Quick reference for making and preserving objections during evidence on oath (commission) or proof (trial) in Scottish civil procedure.

> **Scotland/UK Adaptation:** This skill has been converted from US FRCP 30(c)(2) deposition objections to Scottish civil procedure. There is no direct equivalent to US depositions in Scotland. Evidence is taken on oath at proof (trial) or on commission (rare; for witnesses unable to attend court). Scottish civil evidence law is governed by the Civil Evidence (Scotland) Act 1988, the Law Reform (Miscellaneous Provisions) (Scotland) Act 1968, and the common law of evidence.

## Key Differences from US

| US (FRCP Deposition) | Scotland (Proof/Commission) |
|-----------------------|----------------------------|
| FRCP 30(c)(2) deposition objections | Evidence on oath at proof / commission hearing |
| Deposition before trial | Proof (trial) - objections made in real time |
| 30(b)(6) corporate deposition | No direct equivalent; citation of a representative to appear |
| FRE rules of evidence | Civil Evidence (Scotland) Act 1988; common law |
| FRE 502(d) clawback | No equivalent |
| Speaking objections | Discouraged in Scotland; judge as arbiter |

## Fundamental Rules

- **No jury** in most civil proofs, judge hears objections and rules immediately
- **Objections are made to the presiding sheriff or judge** - not to opposing counsel
- **Concise and specific** - state the ground briefly
- **No coaching through objections** - Scottish courts take a firm view on improper interference with cross-examination
- **General rule of admissibility** - Civil Evidence (Scotland) Act 1988 abolished the requirement for corroboration and relaxed hearsay rules

## Objections in Civil Evidence

### Competency Objections

| Objection | Trigger |
|-----------|---------|
| Hearsay (limited) | Civil Evidence (Scotland) Act 1988 §2 relaxed hearsay rule; but out-of-court statements by non-testifying witness may be challenged |
| Opinion (lay witness) | Witness may only testify to facts within personal knowledge (expert opinion excepted) |
| Privilege (confidential communications) | Legal professional privilege; communications with solicitor |
| Without prejudice | Communications marked "without prejudice" - not admissible |
| Previous judicial determination | Res judicata / act of sederunt objection |

### Relevancy Objections

| Objection | Trigger |
|-----------|---------|
| Irrelevant | Evidence does not tend to prove or disprove a fact in issue |
| Too remote | Evidence circumstantial without probative value |
| Prejudicial effect > probative value | Discretionary exclusion (judge's inherent power) |

### Form Objections (Examination in Chief)

| Objection | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| Leading | Suggesting the answer (not permitted in examination-in-chief) |
| Compound | Two questions in one |
| Vague | Unclear or ambiguous |
| Narrative | Overly broad question calling for a speech |
| Assumes facts | Presupposes unestablished fact |

### Cross-Examination

- Leading questions are **permitted**
- Must be confined to matters raised in examination-in-chief (or matters relevant to credibility)
- Cross-examination to credit (character) has specific limits

## Privilege (Scotland)

| Type | Scope |
|---|---|
| Legal advice privilege | Communications between solicitor and client for legal advice |
| Litigation privilege | Communications for existing/litigious proceedings (narrower than US work product) |
| Without prejudice | Communications made in genuine settlement negotiations |
| Confidentiality (limited) | Medical/confidential relationships, court balances interest |

**No equivalent to:** FRE 502(d) clawback; attorney work product doctrine (Scotland has litigation privilege); self-critical analysis privilege.

## Instructions Not to Answer / Object to Line of Questioning

In Scottish civil procedure:
- **Privilege** - witness/solicitor may claim privilege; judge determines validity
- **Relevancy** - judge may sustain objection and direct witness not to answer
- **Harassment** - judge may disallow oppressive questioning
- **No equivalent to FRCP 30(c)(2) three exclusive grounds** - judge controls proceedings

## Foreign-Law / US-Origin Guardrail

This skill may contain inherited US terminology. For Scotland/UK use, translate rather than copy. Examples: discovery is not Scots commission and diligence/recovery of documents; tort is generally delict in Scots civil analysis; summary judgment is not automatically the Scots summary decree test; bankruptcy concepts may map to sequestration, liquidation, administration, or restructuring depending on party and forum; HIPAA/CCPA/SEC/EEOC/FTC/CFPB concepts require UK GDPR, DPA 2018, FCA, ICO, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, tribunal, or sector-regulator mapping as appropriate. If the matter is genuinely US or foreign-law, quarantine the foreign-law analysis and warn that local counsel/source verification is required.
