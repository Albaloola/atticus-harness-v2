# UK Guidance: Cross-Examination in Scottish Civil & Criminal Proceedings

## Key Differences from US Practice

### Civil Proof (Trial) Procedure in Scotland

- **Proof** (not "trial") in Sheriff Court / **Proof before Answer** in Court of Session
- **Precognition statements** (witness statements prepared by solicitor) are the main pre-proof evidence source, not depositions
- **Commission and Diligence** is the Scottish equivalent of discovery/disclosure, used to recover documents and, in limited circumstances, take evidence on commission
- **Specification of documents** (not discovery request) - used to obtain documents from opposing party or third parties
- **No depositions** as a matter of routine; evidence on commission is rare and requires court approval
- **No equivalent to FRE 613** (prior inconsistent statements) - Scottish evidence law is governed by common law and the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 (criminal) or Civil Evidence (Scotland) Act 1988 (civil)

### Cross-Examination Rules

- **Leading questions** are permitted in cross-examination (same as US)
- **Prior inconsistent statements** can be put to a witness, foundation must be laid (witness must be given opportunity to explain the inconsistency) - similar to Queen Caroline's Rule
- **Spin to criminal**: cross-examination in Scottish criminal trials follows similar principles but with additional safeguards under the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995

### Point-of-Law References

- **No Daubert/Frye standard** in Scotland, expert evidence admissibility is governed by common law principles set out in cases such as *Davie v Magistrates of Edinburgh* 1953 and more recently *Kennedy v Cordia (Services) LLP* 2016 SC (UKSC) 59
- **No FRE** - Scottish evidence law is a mix of common law and statute
- **No Queen Caroline's Rule** by name, but the requirement to put prior inconsistent statements to a witness before leading extrinsic evidence is part of Scottish common law

## Scottish Sources for This Skill

| US Source | Scottish Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Deposition (page:line) | Precognition / Affidavit / Evidence on Commission |
| FRE 613 (prior inconsistent statement) | Common law, must put statement to witness in cross-examination |
| Daubert/Frye (expert methodology) | *Kennedy v Cordia* - expert evidence must be necessary, relevant, and within the expert's competence |
| Discovery request | Specification of documents / Commission and Diligence |
| Exhibit (US numbering) | Production / Doc. No. (Scottish court production numbering) |
| Trial | Proof (civil) / Trial (criminal) |
| Jury trial (civil) | Only in Court of Session for limited actions |

## Practical Note

Cross-examination technique is largely the same in Scotland as in the US. The skill's methodology, organising by witness and theme, extracting impeachment points, preparing leading question sequences, transfers directly. The main adaptations are:
1. Replace deposition cites with precognition/affidavit references
2. Replace FRE/Daubert references with Scottish common law
3. Use Scottish production numbering rather than exhibit numbering
4. Cite sheriff/court officer for any jurisdiction-specific rules on putting prior statements

Sources: Civil Evidence (Scotland) Act 1988; Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995; *Kennedy v Cordia (Services) LLP* 2016; Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service practice notes.
