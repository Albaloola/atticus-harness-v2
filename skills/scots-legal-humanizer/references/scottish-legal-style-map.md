# Scottish legal style map

This reference supports the Scots Legal Humanizer skill. It gives procedure-sensitive style choices, not legal advice.

## Global principles

- Use UK English spelling.
- Use active voice where it clarifies who did what.
- Use plain English unless a legal term of art is needed.
- Avoid old legalistic words unless a particular form requires them.
- Put the main proposition early.
- Break up separate propositions.
- Use short paragraphs and numbered points for formal submissions.
- Keep dates in the form "25 December 2025" unless the form requires otherwise.
- Use figures for sums of money, dates, periods, ages, measurements and percentages.
- Use "must" rather than "shall" unless quoting legislation or matching a required style.
- Use Latin only when it is a recognised term of art or there is no better translation.
- Use one legal proposition per paragraph in arguments where possible.

## Procedure map

### Simple procedure

Purpose: accessible, speedy, inexpensive, informal dispute resolution in the sheriff court.

Best style:
- Plain, direct, and chronological.
- Identify the order sought.
- State what happened and when.
- List evidence clearly.
- Avoid ordinary cause terminology unless quoting or explaining older terms.

Good opening:
"I ask the sheriff to order the respondent to pay £850. The claim arises from a contract dated 12 March 2024."

Bad opening:
"This dispute represents a pivotal and multifaceted failure of the respondent's contractual obligations."

### Ordinary cause sheriff court

Purpose: formal civil litigation, often with traditional pleadings.

Best style:
- Keep terms of art where they matter: pursuer, defender, initial writ, condescendence, defences, pleas-in-law, productions, proof, debate, interlocutor, expenses.
- Avoid unnecessary archaic language.
- Give fair notice of the essential elements of the case.
- Use precise factual averments and avoid argument inside fact-heavy sections unless the style permits it.

Good wording:
"The pursuer avers that the defender received the invoice on 4 April 2024 and did not make payment."

Alternative for party litigant or less formal document:
"The pursuer's position is that the defender received the invoice on 4 April 2024 and did not pay it."

### Court of Session and judicial review

Purpose: senior civil procedure, including judicial review.

Best style:
- Number paragraphs.
- Use concise propositions.
- Identify the decision, act, omission, rule, document, authority, and remedy.
- For notes of argument, summarise submissions. Do not write a full speech.
- Avoid more authorities than needed.

Good wording:
"The petitioner asks the court to reduce the decision dated 3 July 2024 and remit the matter for reconsideration. The decision letter gives no reason for rejecting the medical report dated 8 May 2024."

Bad wording:
"The respondent's decision, viewed through the lens of public law, cannot withstand proper scrutiny."

### Complaints and ombudsman procedures

Purpose: resolve dissatisfaction, obtain explanation, correction, review, redress, or service improvement.

Best style:
- State what was complained about.
- Give dates and documents.
- Explain why the response is not accepted.
- Explain impact without melodrama.
- State the remedy sought.
- Avoid turning the complaint into a court pleading unless litigation is genuinely contemplated.

Good wording:
"I do not accept the stage 1 response because it does not answer point 2 of my complaint. I asked why the report dated 8 May 2024 was not considered. The response does not mention that report."

Bad wording:
"The organisation has demonstrated a systemic and deeply troubling failure of accountability."

## Address and role choices

- Sheriff court written documents usually refer to "the sheriff" or "the court".
- Simple procedure uses claimant and respondent.
- Ordinary cause uses pursuer and defender.
- Court of Session petitions use petitioner and respondent.
- Appeals use appellant and respondent, depending on the specific appeal route.
- Complaints use complainant, complainer, customer, service user, patient, tenant, client, applicant, or requester, depending on scheme and context.

## Safe allegation scale

Use the lowest accurate level.

1. Neutral fact: "The letter was not answered."
2. Procedural concern: "The response does not address the point raised."
3. Evidential concern: "The documents appear to be inconsistent."
4. Breach language: "The respondent did not comply with the order dated..."
5. Serious allegation: "The statement was false" or "the conduct was dishonest" only if the user has clear factual support and intends that allegation.

## Remedy wording bank

Court:
- "I ask the sheriff to grant decree for [amount], with expenses."
- "The defender asks the court to dismiss the action."
- "The petitioner asks the court to reduce the decision and remit the matter for reconsideration."
- "The court is invited to continue the hearing to allow [specific step]."
- "The party asks for leave to lodge [document] late, for the reasons set out below."

Complaints:
- "I ask for a written explanation of..."
- "I ask for the decision to be reviewed by someone not involved in the original decision."
- "I ask for the record to be corrected."
- "I ask for an apology for..."
- "I ask for reimbursement of £[amount], supported by the enclosed receipts."
- "I ask the organisation to explain what steps it will take to prevent the same issue recurring."
