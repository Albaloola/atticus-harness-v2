# Oil and Gas Licensing (UK/Scotland)

## Atticus UK/Scots Legal Excellence Overlay

Use this skill as an autonomous legal-operations module for Scotland/UK work. Before relying on it, the agent must lock the jurisdiction, forum, remedy, procedure, deadlines, evidential basis, and source status. Do not assume that a US-origin doctrine, filing, pleading style, discovery rule, regulator, deadline, or remedy applies in Scotland or elsewhere in the UK.

### Mandatory operating rules

1. **Jurisdiction lock.** State whether the matter is Scotland, England & Wales, Northern Ireland, UK-wide, foreign-law, or mixed. If Scotland is plausible, distinguish sheriff court, Court of Session, tribunals, regulators, ombudsmen, and internal institutional processes.
2. **Official-source hierarchy.** Prefer legislation.gov.uk, Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service rules/forms, Court of Session and sheriff court rules, tribunal/regulator guidance, UK Supreme Court materials, GOV.UK, Scottish Government, ICO, FCA, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, Land Register of Scotland, registers of Scotland, and other primary public sources. Treat secondary commentary as orientation only.
3. **Live verification.** Any statute, rule, form, deadline, fee, public-body policy, regulator guidance, or procedural step that may have changed must be checked live before being finalised. Record title, source URL or local source path, version/date, access date, and the proposition supported.
4. **Evidence discipline.** Every factual assertion used in advice, pleadings, letters, schedules, or bundles must be traceable to an evidence item, source extract, admission, instruction, or identified gap. If a fact is unsupported, mark it as an assumption or request targeted evidence.
5. **Element-by-element reasoning.** Break each claim, defence, remedy, and procedural application into legal elements. Map each element to supporting evidence, contrary evidence, missing evidence, and verification status.
6. **Autonomous depth.** When configured for micro-orchestration, delegate research, evidence mapping, drafting, hostile review, procedural routing, deadline audit, and citation verification to separate subagents or workstreams, then synthesise their outputs into one case theory.
7. **External-action boundary.** Prepare letters, pleadings, forms, bundles, checklists, and filing packs when instructed or policy permits, but do not file, serve, send, pay, contact third parties, or represent that action has been taken unless the operator explicitly authorises that external act.
8. **Uncertainty handling.** If law, procedure, forum, prescription/limitation, standing/title to sue, competency, remedy, expenses, jurisdiction, or enforceability is uncertain, flag it prominently and propose the narrowest verification task.

Provides orientation on the UK petroleum licensing framework and the Scottish onshore oil and gas regulatory context.

[SCOTS: Note: This skill has been adapted from a US Oil and Gas Lease. US-style private mineral leases (lessor/lessee conveying subsurface rights) do not apply in Scotland or the UK. Petroleum rights vest in the Crown under the Petroleum Act 1998. Operators require a Petroleum Exploration and Development Licence (PEDL) from the North Sea Transition Authority (NSTA). Onshore oil and gas licensing is devolved to the Scottish Parliament. There is a moratorium on unconventional oil and gas (fracking) in Scotland. The general methodology of understanding rights, royalties, and obligations is retained, but the substantive legal framework is entirely different.]

## Prerequisites

1. **Petroleum licence** - PEDL reference number, operator name, licence area (map coordinates)
2. **Land rights** - access agreements with landowners (separate from PEDL); mineral rights and surface access
3. **Entity documents** - company registration (Companies House), capacity, authority
4. **Commercial terms** - licence duration, work programme commitments, fee structure
5. **Regulatory context** - NSTA, OPRED (environmental), Crown Estate Scotland / The Crown Estate, Planning Authority (Scottish local authority or Energy Consents Unit)
6. **Environmental assessments** - EIA, Habitats Regulations Assessment, planning consent

## Output Structure

### Step 1: Understand the UK/Scottish Legal Framework

| Element | UK/Scottish Position |
|---------|----------------------|
| Petroleum ownership | Crown (Petroleum Act 1998) - no private mineral ownership |
| Licensing authority | North Sea Transition Authority (NSTA) - offshore; Scottish Ministers, onshore licensing (devolved) |
| Licence type | Petroleum Exploration and Development Licence (PEDL) |
| Surface access | Requires separate landowner agreement (not mineral lease) |
| Planning permission | Required from local authority or Energy Consents Unit |
| Environmental regulator | SEPA (Scotland), OPRED (offshore), HSE |
| Crown Estate | Crown Estate Scotland (seabed within 12nm; some mineral rights) |

[SCOTS: Note: In the US, private landowners own mineral rights and can lease them to operators. In Scotland/UK, mineral rights are almost entirely vested in the Crown. Operators need a PEDL from the NSTA and separate agreements for land access. There is no "royalty" payable to the landowner, the landowner receives surface access compensation only, not a share of production. The UK system uses a licence fee and supplementary charge (Ring Fence Corporation Tax) rather than a royalty.]

### Step 2: Parties, Property & Rights

**Operator**: Full legal name, registered address (Companies House), group structure, NSTA licence holder.

**Landowner(s)**: Full legal names, addresses, Land Register title numbers (for surface access).

**Crown/State**: NSTA reference, licence area.

**Rights granted** - the PEDL grants:
- Exclusive right to search and bore for and get petroleum within the licensed area, Subject to the terms of the licence, the Petroleum Act 1998, and regulatory consents, Surface access must be separately agreed with landowners (option/wayleave/lease)

### Step 3: Licence Term

PEDL licences typically have:
- Initial term (2 to 6 years) - exploration and appraisal, Second term (4 to 5 years) - development planning, Third term (20 to 30 years) - production, Licence may be revoked for non-compliance with work programme

### Step 4: Consideration / Fees

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Licence fee | Annual fee payable to NSTA per km² |
| Infrastructure fee | Payable to Crown Estate Scotland or The Crown Estate for pipeline/wellhead |
| Surface access compensation | Negotiated with landowner (not a royalty) |
| Ring Fence Corporation Tax | Effectively captures a portion of profits |
| Supplementary Charge | Additional charge on ring fence profits |

**No US-style royalty structure** - under the UK system, the Crown (public) receives the benefit of production through taxation and NSTA licence fees, not through a percentage royalty to a private landowner.

### Step 5: Operations, Surface Use & Environmental

- Planning consent required from local authority (Scottish planning system)
- Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) under the Planning etc. (Scotland) Act 2006
- SEPA permits for water discharge, waste management, emissions, HSE regulations for well design and safety, DECC/OPRED for offshore environmental jurisdiction, Public consultation requirements, Decommissioning obligations under the Petroleum Act 1998

### Step 6: Warranties, Indemnities & Assignment

- **Operator warranty**: compliance with licence conditions, regulatory obligations, environmental standards
- **Indemnification**: injury, property damage, environmental liability
- **Assignment**: NSTA consent required for any licence assignment or change of control
- **Decommissioning**: security arrangements may be required for decommissioning obligations

### Step 7: Default, Termination & Post-Termination

- NSTA may revoke licence for non-compliance, Notice-and-cure period as per licence terms, Surface access agreements terminate per their own terms
- **Decommissioning obligations** (well plugging, site restoration): must be approved by NSTA; decommissioning programme required

### Step 8: General Provisions & Execution

- Governing law: Scotland (or England, per licence terms)
- Notices: recorded delivery to registered office, Dispute resolution: arbitration or court of session, Agreement is subject to the licence conditions

## Guidelines

- **UK petroleum rights vest in the Crown** - there is no private mineral ownership
- **Onshore oil and gas in Scotland**: subject to a moratorium on unconventional oil and gas (including fracking) since 2015 - verify current policy
- **PEDL is not a lease** - it is a statutory licence granted under the Petroleum Act 1998
- **Surface access is separate** - landowner negotiations cover site access, pipelines, and compensation
- **Decommissioning** is a significant liability, operators must provide security
- **Do not** draft as a US-style mineral lease, the legal framework is entirely different
- **Consult specialist UK oil and gas solicitor** before any commitment

### Key Differences

| US Concept | UK/Scottish Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Mineral rights owned by landowner | Petroleum rights vest in the Crown (Petroleum Act 1998) |
| Oil and gas lease | PEDL (Petroleum Exploration and Development Licence) |
| Lessor / Lessee | NSTA (licensor) / Operator (licensee) |
| Royalty to landowner | No landowner royalty; RCF Tax + Supplementary Charge |
| Delay rentals / shut-in royalties | Licence fees to NSTA |
| Pooling / unitisation | NSTA-approved field development plans |
| BLM (onshore federal) | NSTA + Crown Estate Scotland |
| State-specific oil and gas law | Petroleum Act 1998 + Energy Act 2016 (UK-wide) |
| RCRA, Clean Water Act (environmental) | Environmental Protection Act 1990, Water Environment (Controlled Activities) (Scotland) Regulations |
| Surface damage acts | Accommodation works / planning conditions |

### Forms
UK PEDL licence applications are made through the NSTA's online portal. Model clauses are set out in statutory instruments. Land access agreements (options, wayleaves) are not prescribed forms, they should be drafted by a solicitor experienced in UK energy law.
