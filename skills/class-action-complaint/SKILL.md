## Legislative and Procedural Context

### Current status, Read first

**Scotland does not have US-style class actions.** The multi-party action framework is:

1. **Court of Session Rule of Court 26 (Chapter 26 - Group Proceedings)** - The current operative procedure. Rules deal with group proceedings orders (GPOs), group registers, lead cases, and expenses. Group proceedings are case-managed.

2. **Group Procedure (Civil Litigation) (Scotland) Act 2020** - This Act was passed by the Scottish Parliament but **has NOT been fully commenced** (as at current date). [VERIFY] its current status before drafting. When commenced, it will introduce a statutory group procedure with opt-out (where appropriate) and additional case management powers. Until commencement, Chapter 26 remains the applicable procedure.

3. **Simple multi-party action** - Where Chapter 26 does not apply, a simple multi-party action (multiple pursuers with a common interest jointly raising proceedings, or a representative party) remains possible under the ordinary Court of Session procedure.

4. **No opt-out class action** (pre-2020 Act) - Under Chapter 26, group proceedings are **opt-in** (pursuers must register on the group register to be bound). The 2020 Act may introduce opt-out for certain actions - [VERIFY] commencement.

5. **No punitive damages** - Scots law does not award punitive damages. Aggravated damages may be available for certain wrongs (defamation, personal injury with conduct element). Restitutionary damages are limited.

6. **Expenses (costs): loser pays** - The general rule in Scottish civil litigation is that the unsuccessful party pays the successful party's judicial expenses. This is a fundamental difference from US practice.

7. **No pre-judgment interest** - Interest on damages is governed by the Courts Reform (Scotland) Act 2014 (judicial interest). The court has discretion.

## Prerequisites

Collect before drafting:

1. **Named pursuer facts** - individual harm, title and interest to sue (Scottish standing requirement), timeline, no conflicts with other group members
2. **Defender identification** - corporate structure, registered address (Companies House / Scottish register), principal place of business
3. **Common conduct** - documents/policies/data showing systematic wrongdoing affecting multiple persons
4. **Proposed group definition** - objective inclusion/exclusion criteria; Scotland territorial nexus
5. **Jurisdictional basis** - the Court of Session has jurisdiction for high-value actions (£100,000+ or other exceptional basis); Sheriff Court may be applicable for lower-value multi-party claims under Simple Procedure
6. **Case files** - contracts, correspondence, expert reports, damages data, pre-action correspondence

## Pleading Structure

**Caption**: Court of Session (Outer House); "[Named Pursuer] for and on behalf of [himself/herself/themselves] as [representative party / registered group member]; "PURSUERS' CONDESCENDENCE"; Defender(s); "GROUP PROCEEDINGS".

### Structure of the Condescendence (Statement of Claim)

| # | Section | Content |
|---|---------|---------|
| 1 | Introduction | 2 to 3 sections: parties, wrongdoing summary, group-wide harm |
| 2 | Parties | Named pursuer title and interest to sue + defender corporate details; territorial nexus to Scotland |
| 3 | Jurisdiction & Venue | Court of Session jurisdiction (subject matter, value, territorial); \_forum non conveniens\_ considerations |
| 4 | Factual Background | Chronological narrative with dates, amounts, document quotes, defenders' common conduct |
| 5 | Group Allegations | Chapter 26 / 2020 Act requirements per checklist below |
| 6 | Pleas-in-Law (Causes of Action) | Each legal basis as a separate plea-in-law; each plea supported by the relevant facts and law |
| 7 | Craves (Prayer for Relief) | Itemised orders and remedies sought |

## Chapter 26 Group Proceedings Checklist

### Requirements for Group Proceedings Order (GPO)

Under Rule of Court 26.3, the court must be satisfied:

- **Same/similar circumstances** - claims arise from the same or similar circumstances of fact or law (broader test than numerosity + typicality)
- **Common/number >10** - where there are more than 10 claims involving the same or similar circumstances, a GPO may be sought
- **Suitability** - group proceedings are a suitable means of resolving the claims efficiently and justly
- **Representative party** - the pursuer who makes the application must be suitable to represent the group (no conflicts, adequate resources, appropriate counsel)
- **Group definition** - clear, objective criteria for inclusion; register of group members maintained
- **Opt-in requirement** - under Chapter 26, group proceedings are opt-in. Members must register on the group register. The 2020 Act (if commenced) may provide for opt-out in appropriate circumstances.

### Additional considerations

- **Lead case** - the court may select a lead case (or test cases) from the group register to determine common issues.
- **Case management** - Chapter 26 gives the court extensive case management powers: timetable, disclosure, preliminary issues, bifurcation of common and individual issues.
- **Expenses** - the court has wide discretion on expenses in group proceedings. A failure on common issues by a lead case may have costs implications for the wider group. The loser pays principle applies.
- **Sist (stay)** - proceedings may be sisted pending the determination of common issues.

### Post-2020 Act considerations (if commenced)

If the Group Procedure (Civil Litigation) (Scotland) Act 2020 is fully commenced, also consider:

- **Opt-out proceedings** - the court may order opt-out group proceedings where appropriate (including for claims for damages)
- **Allocation to open register** - similar to Chapter 26 group register but with opt-out rather than opt-in
- **Case management powers** - expanded judicial case management
- **Funding** - the Act addresses certain aspects of funding and expenses in group proceedings

## Group Definition Template

> All [persons/entities] in [Scotland / UK territorial scope] who [purchased/used/were subjected to] [product/service/practice] [from/by] [Defender] during the period [start date] through [end date/present] [excluding the Defender's directors, officers, and employees; and any person who has already raised separate proceedings in respect of the same matters].

The definition must use objective, administrative criteria. The territorial nexus to Scotland (residence, damage suffered in Scotland, contract governed by Scots law) is critical for jurisdiction.

## Pleas-in-Law (Causes of Action)

Per plea-in-law: (1) incorporate preceding articles by reference, (2) legal basis with authority, (3) defender's wrongful conduct, (4) element-by-element allegations, (5) group-wide harm and causation, (6) remedy type (damages, specific implement, interdict, declarator).

## Craves (Prayer for Relief)

Include: (1) declarator of defenders' liability on group-wide basis, (2) decree for payment of damages to each registered group member, (3) interdict (injunction) if appropriate, (4) specific implement, (5) interest on sums decerned for (Courts Reform (Scotland) Act 2014 judicial interest rate), (6) expenses (judicial expenses) - note loser pays principle, (7) such further and other orders as the court deems fit.

## Pitfalls and Checks

- **No US-style class action** - Do not use US terminology. The correct terms are: multi-party action, group proceedings, representative party, group register, group proceedings order.
- **No punitive damages** - Do not claim punitive damages. Aggravated damages may be available in limited circumstances.
- **Loser pays expenses** - Scottish litigation follows the loser pays (expenses follow the event) principle, not the US rule. Address funding and after-the-event (ATE) insurance where applicable.
- **Pre-action protocol** - The pre-action protocol for personal injury actions or debt claims may apply; comply before raising proceedings.
- **Title and interest to sue** - The pursuer must show title (standing) and interest under Scots law (the equivalent of US Article III standing, but distinct). Enough connection to the subject matter of the action is required.
- **Pleading standard** - Under Scots law, condescendences require specific averments (not bare legal conclusions). The rule in \_Henderson v. Henderson\_ (pleading standard) applies.
- **Forum non conveniens** - The court may decline jurisdiction if another forum is more appropriate. Where the defender is outside Scotland, jurisdiction rules under the Civil Jurisdiction and Judgments Act 1982 apply.
- **Time bar (prescription and limitation)** - Prescription and Limitation (Scotland) Act 1973; shorter limitation periods than many US civil actions. Check carefully for each claim.
- **Concurrent proceedings in England** - A group action may also be litigated in England; coordinate to avoid \_lis alibi pendens\_ issues.
- **Funding** - Third-party litigation funding and CFAs are available in Scotland but subject to regulation. Address funding structure early.
- **On-demand / certainty** - Do not allege that group certification is "certain." State supporting facts and request a Group Proceedings Order in the craves.

### Jurisdictional notes

- **Scotland only**: The Court of Session (Outer House) is the primary forum for multi-party / group proceedings. The Sheriff Court has limited multi-party capacity (Simple Procedure for claims up to £5,000; Ordinary Cause up to £100,000 for personal injury).
- **Opt-in vs opt-out**: Under Rule of Court 26, group proceedings are **opt-in** only. The 2020 Act would introduce opt-out for certain categories. Verify commencement.
- **Damages cap**: No US-style treble damages for antitrust/competition claims. Follow-on damages actions under the Competition Act 1998 provide for single damages.
- **Time bar (prescription)**: The Prescription and Limitation (Scotland) Act 1973 applies, typically 5 years for delict (tort), 5 years for contract (short negative prescription). Shorter than many US limitation periods.
- **Group register**: Under Chapter 26, a group register is maintained. The group proceedings order may set a cut-off date for registration.

### Derivation

The Scottish group proceedings framework derives from the Lord Gill Review of Civil Justice in Scotland (2006 to 2009) and subsequent reforms. It is fundamentally different from US Rule 23 class actions. The Group Procedure (Civil Litigation) (Scotland) Act 2020, when fully commenced, will represent a significant evolution but will still not create a US-style class action regime.
