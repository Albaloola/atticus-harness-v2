Drafts an EIA Report analysing the likely significant effects of a proposed development on the environment, in compliance with the Town and Country Planning (Environmental Impact Assessment) (Scotland) Regulations 2017 (the "2017 Regulations") and, where applicable, the Environmental Assessment (Scotland) Act 2005 (SEA).

## Authority status (read first)

Scottish EIA practice derives from **EU Directive 2011/92/EU** (as amended by Directive 2014/52/EU) retained and transposed into Scots law after EU exit. The process remains similar in structure (screening, scoping, EIA Report, consultation, decision) but the legal authority structure is entirely different from US NEPA practice.

1. **Primary legislation** - Town and Country Planning (Scotland) Act 1997 (as amended) and the Environmental Assessment (Scotland) Act 2005 (for plans and programmes).
2. **Principal regulations** - The Town and Country Planning (Environmental Impact Assessment) (Scotland) Regulations 2017 (SSI 2017/169) (the "2017 Regulations"). These are the Scottish EIA regulations that transpose the EU EIA Directive.
3. **Other EIA regimes** - The Electricity Works (Environmental Impact Assessment) (Scotland) Regulations 2017 (for electricity generating stations); The Marine Works (Environmental Impact Assessment) (Scotland) Regulations 2017 (for marine developments); The Transport and Works (Scotland) Act 2007 (for major infrastructure).
4. **Planning authority** - The relevant planning authority (local authority) is the competent body for EIA determination for most developments. Scottish Ministers act as the competent body for major infrastructure (National Developments / Section 36 Electricity Act consents).
5. **Statutory consultees** - SEPA, NatureScot (formerly Scottish Natural Heritage), Historic Environment Scotland, Scottish Water, relevant Community Councils, adjoining planning authorities.
6. **Habitats Regulations Appraisal (HRA)** - Where a development may affect a European site (SPA, SAC, Ramsar), a separate HRA is required under the Conservation (Natural Habitats, &c.) Regulations 1994 (as amended) - **not** the same as NEPA Section 7 consultation.

### Key differences from NEPA

| NEPA Concept | Scottish EIA Equivalent |
|---|---|
| NEPA (42 U.S.C. § 4321) | EIA Directive / 2017 Regulations |
| EIS | **EIA Report** (formerly "Environmental Statement" - term changed under 2017 Regulations to align with Directive language) |
| CEQ regulations (40 CFR Parts 1500 to 1508) | **No equivalent** - EIA procedures are in the 2017 Regulations themselves, not a separate agency rulemaking body |
| Fiscal Responsibility Act 2023 | **Not applicable** - NEPA-specific US statute |
| Lead agency | **Planning authority** (for most developments) or **Scottish Ministers** (for major infrastructure/Section 36 consents) |
| No-action alternative | **Do-nothing / No-development alternative** - same concept, must be considered |
| Cumulative impacts | **Cumulative effects** - same concept under EIA Directive/2017 Regulations |
| Environmental Justice (E.O. 12898) | **No direct statutory equivalent** - socio-economic effects may be considered under equality duties / Scottish Public Sector Equality Duty |
| Tribal consultation | **Not applicable** - replaced by **Community Council consultation** and **public consultation** requirements (Regulations 19 to 22 of the 2017 Regulations) |
| Section 106 NHPA (heritage) | **Different regime** - Listed building consent / Scheduled Monument Consent / Conservation Area consent under the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) (Scotland) Act 1997 |
| Section 7 ESA (Endangered Species) | **Habitats Regulations Appraisal (HRA)** under Conservation (Natural Habitats, &c.) Regulations 1994 |
| EPA | **SEPA** (Scottish Environment Protection Agency) - statutory consultee under the 2017 Regulations |
| USDA, DOE, DOI, FHWA, FAA, USACE | **Statutory consultees**: SEPA, NatureScot, Historic Environment Scotland, Scottish Water, relevant planning authority consultees |

## Prerequisites

Gather before drafting:

1. **Project identification** - development name, applicant, location, planning authority, proposed description of development.
2. **EIA determination** - copy of screening opinion (or direction by Scottish Ministers) confirming EIA is required.
3. **Scoping opinion** - copy of scoping opinion (Regulation 16 of the 2017 Regulations) from the planning authority.
4. **Screening / Scoping responses** - consultee responses from SEPA, NatureScot, HES, the community.
5. **Proposed development** - full description: location, site area, phasing, construction methodology, operational activities, decommissioning.
6. **Alternatives** - all alternatives considered (location, design, layout, technology, operational strategies), including the do-nothing alternative.
7. **Baseline data** - environmental inventories by topic: air quality, water environment (The Water Environment and Water Services (Scotland) Act 2003), geology/soils, biodiversity (NatureScot datasets), landscape/visual (Landscape Character Assessment), cultural heritage (Historic Environment Record), population/human health, material assets, climate factors (GHG emissions / climate resilience).
8. **Consultation records** - pre-application and formal consultation correspondence.
9. **Page-budget plan** - while the 2017 Regulations do not prescribe page limits (unlike US § 4336a), proportionate and focused presentation is essential in practice. The EIA Report should be concise but comprehensive.

## Quick Start

1. Confirm development description and whether EIA is required (Schedule 1 / Schedule 2 / Schedule 3 criteria of the 2017 Regulations).
2. Obtain the screening and scoping opinions from the planning authority.
3. Confirm statutory consultee bodies and consultations undertaken.
4. Draft description of development and alternatives (including do-nothing).
5. Describe the baseline environment by receptor/topic.
6. Assess likely significant effects (direct, indirect, secondary, cumulative, short/medium/long-term, permanent/temporary, positive/negative) per receptor × alternative.
7. Specify mitigation measures (embedded and additional) with monitoring commitments.
8. Document all consultation responses (pre-application and formal).
9. Provide a non-technical summary (required by the 2017 Regulations).
10. Compile list of assessors with relevant competencies (Regulation 17 requires adequate expertise).

## Document Structure

An EIA Report under the 2017 Regulations must contain (Schedule 4 of the Regulations):

1. **Non-technical summary** - accessible summary for public.
2. **Introduction and project description** - the development in its setting.
3. **Alternatives considered** - reasonable alternatives studied; reasons for chosen option (including do-nothing).
4. **Baseline environment** - current state per environmental receptor.
5. **Likely significant effects** - assessment per receptor with methodology, significance criteria, residual effects.
6. **Mitigation measures** - measures to avoid, prevent, reduce, or offset significant adverse effects; monitoring arrangements.
7. **Cumulative effects** - interaction with other existing/approved developments.
8. **Transboundary effects** - where applicable (Scotland/rest of UK / Republic of Ireland).
9. **Consultation summary** - issues raised and how addressed.
10. **References and technical appendices**.

## Assessment Methodology

Assessment should use a structured significance framework. Typical approach:

- **Sensitivity/importance of receptor** (high, medium, low, negligible)
- **Magnitude of effect** (major, moderate, minor, negligible)
- **Significance of effect** (matrix of sensitivity × magnitude) - major/moderate typically significant; minor/negligent typically not significant
- **Nature of effect** - positive/negative; direct/indirect; short/medium/long-term; permanent/temporary; reversible/irreversible

_There is no single prescribed methodology under the 2017 Regulations, but the methodology must be clearly stated and justified._

## Pitfalls and Checks

- **Proportionality** - the EIA Report must be proportionate to the nature, scale, and likely effects of the development. Over-scoping is a common problem.
- **Significance framework** - define clearly; a confused or arbitrary significance matrix is a challenge target.
- **No hard-look equivalent** - Scottish judicial review is under Wednesbury unreasonableness / irrationality, not US "hard look" APA review, but the test remains real: an EIA that fails to address a likely significant effect is challengeable.
- **Environmental Statement → EIA Report** - since the 2017 Regulations, the correct term is "EIA Report" not "Environmental Statement" (though both terms appear in practice).
- **Mitigation certainty** - mitigation measures must be clearly described and enforceable (typically through planning conditions or s.75 planning obligations). Uncosted, aspirational mitigation is a weakness.
- **Consultation timing** - pre-application consultation with the community (PAC) is mandatory for major developments under the Planning etc. (Scotland) Act 2006 and associated Regulations. Document it properly.
- **Scottish specific** - the Scottish Government's EIA guidance (PAN 1/2013 or successor) is advisory but practically authoritative.
- **HRA and EIA** - if a Habitats Regulations Appraisal (Appropriate Assessment) runs in parallel, cross-reference clearly; do not conflate the two regimes.
- **Public participation** - the 2017 Regulations require public notice, inspection of documents, and at least 30-day public consultation period.
- **Cumulative effects** - must include other developments (not just the subject development's phases); this is frequently challenged.
- **Climate change** - consideration of both GHG emissions (climate change mitigation) and climate resilience/vulnerability (adaptation) is required.
- **No page limits** - unlike NEPA § 4336a (150/300 pages), the 2017 Regulations do not impose statutory page limits. However, take a proportionate, targeted approach.

## Troubleshooting

- **Screening opinion already issued a negative EIA determination.** If no EIA is required, this skill may not apply. Consider whether the development has changed materially (Schedule 3 re-evaluation).
- **Multiple competent authorities** (e.g., planning authority + Marine Scotland). Coordinate so the EIA Report addresses both regimes; a single report can satisfy both sets of requirements.
- **Sixty-year life and beyond.** No fixed statutory page or time limit under the 2017 Regulations. Extensions agreed with the planning authority as needed.
- **Pre-application consultation not completed.** This must be completed before the EIA Report is submitted. The Report must include the PAC report.
- **Cumulative effects methodology varies.** No single prescribed methodology. State the methodology and apply it consistently across all receptors.
- **Community Council consultation.** The 2017 Regulations require the planning authority to consult relevant Community Councils. The applicant should also engage early.

### Jurisdictional notes

- **Scotland only**: This skill addresses EIA under Scottish planning legislation. Separate but similar EIA regimes exist for England and Wales (the Town and Country Planning (Environmental Impact Assessment) Regulations 2017) and for Northern Ireland. The Scottish Regulations are the governing instrument for developments in Scotland.
- **EU exit**: Following Brexit, retained EU law (including the EIA Directive as transposed) continues to apply via the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018. The Scottish Regulations implement the Directive and remain in force. Future divergence is possible; verify current regulations.
- **No page limits**: The 2017 Regulations do not impose statutory page limits on EIA Reports. Proportionality is encouraged.
- **SEA (Strategic Environmental Assessment)**: For plans, programmes, and strategies (not projects), use the Environmental Assessment (Scotland) Act 2005.

### Derivation

The UK/Scottish EIA regime derives from **EU Directive 2011/92/EU** (as amended by Directive 2014/52/EU). The structure (screening, scoping, EIA Report, consultation, public participation, decision) mirrors the Directive framework but differs fundamentally from the US NEPA framework in legal authority, institutional structure, and implementing regulations.
