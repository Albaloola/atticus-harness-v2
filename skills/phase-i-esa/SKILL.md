# Phase I Environmental Site Assessment, Scotland/UK Adaptation

## Atticus UK/Scots Legal Excellence Overlay

Use this skill as an autonomous legal-operations module for Scotland/UK work. Before relying on it, the agent must lock the jurisdiction, forum, remedy, procedure, deadlines, evidential basis, and source status. Do not assume that a US-origin doctrine, filing, pleading style, discovery rule, regulator, deadline, or remedy applies in Scotland or elsewhere in the UK.

### Mandatory operating rules

1. **Jurisdiction lock.** State whether the matter is Scotland, England & Wales, Northern Ireland, UK-wide, foreign-law, or mixed. If Scotland is plausible, distinguish sheriff court, Court of Session, tribunals, regulators, ombudsmen, and internal institutional processes.
2. **Official-source hierarchy.** Prefer legislation.gov.uk, Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service rules/forms, Court of Session and sheriff court rules, tribunal/regulator guidance, UK Supreme Court materials, GOV.UK, Scottish Government, ICO, FCA, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, Land Register of Scotland, registers of Scotland, and other primary public sources. Treat secondary commentary as orientation only.
3. **Live verification.** Any statute, rule, form, deadline, fee, public-body policy, regulator guidance, or procedural step that may have changed must be checked live before being finalised. Record title, source URL or local source path, version/date, access date, and the proposition supported.
4. **Evidence discipline.** Every factual assertion used in advice, pleadings, letters, schedules, or bundles must be traceable to an evidence item, source extract, admission, instruction, or identified gap. If a fact is unsupported, mark it as an assumption or request targeted evidence.
5. **Element-by-element reasoning.** Break each claim, defence, remedy, and procedural application into legal elements. Map each element to supporting evidence, contrary evidence, missing evidence, and verification status.
6. **Autonomous depth.** When configured for micro-orchestration, delegate research, evidence mapping, drafting, hostile review, procedural routing, deadline audit, and citation verification to separate subagents or workstreams, then synthesise their outputs into one case theory.
7. **External-action boundary.** Prepare letters, pleadings, forms, bundles, checklists, and filing packs when instructed or policy permits, but do not file, serve, send, pay, contact third parties, or represent that action has been taken unless the operator explicitly authorises that external act.
8. **Uncertainty handling.** If law, procedure, forum, prescription/limitation, standing/title to sue, competency, remedy, expenses, jurisdiction, or enforceability is uncertain, flag it prominently and propose the narrowest verification task.

### Expected work product

Where proportionate, produce a chronology, issue map, source log, evidence matrix, merits/risk table, remedy/damages table, procedural route note, draft document, bundle index, service/filing checklist, and operator handoff note. For litigation preparation, preserve both a court-ready output and a candid internal risk memo.

Drafts a Phase I-style contaminated land assessment for Scottish property transactions, establishing environmental due diligence under the Environmental Protection Act 1990 Part IIA, the Contaminated Land (Scotland) Regulations 2000 (as amended), and the Water Environment (Controlled Activities) (Scotland) Regulations 2011.

## Prerequisites

Collect before drafting:

1. **Property identification** - legal description, address, title number, coordinates, zoning
2. **Client purpose** - acquisition, lending, corporate compliance, or other
3. **Site reconnaissance data** - date, observations, photos, access limitations
4. **Historical sources** - aerial photos, historic Ordnance Survey maps, fire insurance maps, city/trade directories, historic mining records
5. **Regulatory database results** - SEPA public register, local authority contaminated land register, historical landfill records
6. **User questionnaires** - seller/occupant environmental questionnaires
7. **Interview notes** - current/past owners, operators, SEPA, local authority environmental health
8. **Assessor qualifications** - relevant professional credentials (e.g., chartered environmental surveyor, chartered geologist, SI(S) registered)
9. **Prior reports** (if any) - previous environmental assessments, remediation documents, monitoring data

## Quick Start

1. Confirm property ID, client purpose, and transaction timeline.
2. Verify all prerequisite data is available; flag missing items as data gaps.
3. Draft each report section sequentially per the structure below.
4. Classify every identified condition as a land contamination risk.
5. State conclusions unambiguously; recommend intrusive investigation if risks identified.

## Contamination Risk Classifications

| Type | Definition | Must Include |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Contamination, significant possibility** | Significant possibility of significant harm (SPOSH) to human health or controlled waters, triggers Part IIA regulation | Factual basis; evidence sources; contaminant ID; pathway-receptor linkage |
| **Contamination, possibility** | Possible contamination requiring further investigation but not at SPOSH level | Identified risks; data gaps; recommended next steps |
| **Past remediation** | Past contamination remediated to standard with residual liabilities | Remediation performed; validation data; closure documentation; residual liability |
| **De minimis** | Minor or localised contamination not requiring regulatory action | Document with rationale |

## Historical Use Red Flags

Flag current or historical presence of: gas works, oil storage/distribution, chemical works, engineering/foundries, dry cleaners, metal plating/finishing, tanneries, paper mills, textile manufacturing, printing works, landfill/waste disposal, railway land, garages/vehicle repair, mining (coal, metal, oil shale), agricultural operations (pesticide/fuel storage, sheep dip).

**PFAS-relevant uses**: firefighter training areas / AFFF storage, military bases and airports (AFFF), electroplating / metal finishing, textile manufacturing / coating, paper manufacturing, semiconductor manufacturing, certain landfills accepting industrial waste.

## PFAS and emerging contaminants

PFAS evaluation should form part of the site reconnaissance and historical review where property history suggests possible PFAS use. Unlike the US, PFAS is not yet categorically listed under Part IIA but represents an emerging regulatory concern in UK water quality regulation. SEPA is actively developing PFAS monitoring and regulatory guidance.

- Where SEPA has published PFAS screening values or has data in the local catchment, these should be considered, Groundwater pathways for PFAS differ from petroleum / chlorinated solvents; analytical methods (LC-MS/MS) and UKAS-accredited labs required, The Water Environment (Controlled Activities) (Scotland) Regulations are the relevant discharge control regime

## Part IIA Shelf-Life

- **Full report**: typically considered valid 6 to 12 months from completion to acquisition (practice standard, not statutory)
- **Site reconnaissance**: should be within 6 months of report date
- **Regulatory records update**: should be refreshed if 6+ months old

## Pitfalls and Checks

1. **Part IIA vs planning regime** - distinguish between Part IIA regulatory determination and planning-condition remediation (often more onerous)
2. **Contamination classification precision** - use exact Part IIA definitions; distinguish SPOSH from possibility
3. **Evidence-backed findings** - every identified risk must cite specific observations, records, history, or interviews
4. **Data gaps** - identify all; assess significance; recommend resolution
5. **No legal advice** - direct liability questions to solicitor (conveyancing / environmental law)
6. **Non-invasive scope only** - never imply intrusive investigation was performed
7. **Point-in-time limitation** - acknowledge the assessment reflects conditions as of assessment date
8. **Welsh/English differences** - note if the property is in Scotland vs. England vs. Wales (different Part IIA regimes)
9. **Vapour intrusion** - evaluate any property near known groundwater contamination
10. **Facts vs. opinions** - clearly distinguish; state basis for professional judgment
11. **Japanese knotweed and other invasive species** - a distinct and common real-world risk in the UK; note separately from contamination
12. **Radon** - UK Health Security Agency radon maps; Scotland has lower radon risk than SW England but affected areas exist
13. **Asbestos** - not categorised as "contaminated land" under Part IIA, but a practical risk for pre-2000 buildings

### Regulatory Framework

- **Primary legislation**: Environmental Protection Act 1990, Part IIA (Contaminated Land) - applied separately for Scotland, England, Wales, NI
- **Scottish statutory guidance**: Scottish Government "Contaminated Land, Statutory Guidance" (2022 edition replaces 2006/2012 versions); Water Environment (Controlled Activities) (Scotland) Regulations 2011
- **Key differences from EPA Part IIA regime**: Scotland has a different definition of "significant possibility of significant harm" and "controlled waters" than England; the statutory guidance is separate
- **Planning regime**: Local authority planning conditions frequently require investigation and remediation on redevelopment, often more onerous than Part IIA trigger
- **Liabilities**: Contaminated land liability falls on Class A (causer/permiter) or Class B (owner/occupier) persons; no US-style buyer protections

### Practitioner Notes

- No US-style CERCLA defence exists in Scotland; environmental due diligence is transactional and lender-driven, The Coal Authority (mining-related issues) is a distinct risk in Scottish coal-mining areas, Japanese knotweed and other invasive non-native species (INNS) are a routine transaction concern not captured by Part IIA, Radon: check UKHSA radon maps; affected areas do exist in Scotland (esp. Highland, Grampian)
- The Law Society of Scotland Conveyancing Committee issues guidance on environmental searches, Commercial Environmental Searches (e.g., Landmark, Groundsure) provide database-style reports as standard in UK transactions, Professional indemnity insurance for environmental assessors is standard practice
