[SCOTS: Note] This skill is adapted from the US mortgage/deed of trust framework. Scotland does not use mortgages or deeds of trust. The equivalent security instrument is the **Standard Security** under the Conveyancing and Feudal Reform (Scotland) Act 1970. There is no separate trustee concept, no judicial/non-judicial foreclosure distinction, and the regulatory framework is the FCA's MCOB sourcebook (for regulated mortgage contracts), the Consumer Credit Act 1974, and Scottish property law.

Drafts a recording-ready Standard Security for residential or commercial property in Scotland, following the statutory forms in Schedule 2 to the 1970 Act.

## Prerequisites

Gather before drafting:

1. **Property jurisdiction** - confirm the property is in Scotland (different security law from England/Wales)
2. **Note / Loan Agreement terms** - principal, interest rate, payment schedule, maturity, loan purpose
3. **Title sheet** - Land Register Title Number; full legal description and property address; burdens and servitudes
4. **Parties** - all proprietors/borrowers (exact names as on title deed), lender/creditor (full legal name, FCA regulated status)
5. **Property** - legal description (as in title sheet), postal address, local authority area
6. **Special circumstances** - flat/tenement (common property issues), commercial property, buy-to-let, second home

## Instrument Selection

In Scotland, the only security instrument for heritable (real) property is the **Standard Security** under the Conveyancing and Feudal Reform (Scotland) Act 1970.

| Factor | Standard Security |
|--------|-------------------|
| Title | Borrower (proprietor) retains title; lender (creditor) holds security interest |
| Enforcement | Calling-up notice (Form A, Schedule 6); notice of default (Form B); power of sale; decree of foreclosure |
| Parties | Proprietor (borrower/granter) + Creditor (lender) |
| Statutory forms | Form A (personal obligation + grant of security) or Form B (straight grant of security) per Schedule 2 |
| Registration | Land Register of Scotland (or Register of Sasines for older titles) |

Use Scots law; never default, always confirm property jurisdiction.

## Document Structure

### 1. Recitals & Granting Clause

- All parties with full legal names and capacities (as in title sheet)
- Recite the loan agreement / promissory note: date, principal, lender, Granting clause: "I/We [Proprietor(s)] grant in favour of [Creditor] a Standard Security over [Property]"
- Reference to the personal obligation secured (Form A includes the personal covenant; Form B requires a separate deed)
- "For the purposes of the Conveyancing and Feudal Reform (Scotland) Act 1970"

### 2. Property Description

- Legal description matching Land Register Title Number exactly (title number controls over address)
- Registered: Land Register Title Number, Ordnance Survey map reference, postal address, Unregistered: description as in Sasines search, Local authority area

### 3. Standard Conditions (Schedule 3, the 1970 Act)

The Standard Conditions set out in Schedule 3 to the 1970 Act apply automatically. Key conditions include:

| Condition | Requirements |
|---|---|
| 1, Personal obligation | Debtor must repay principal, interest, and expenses |
| 2, Maintenance | Debtor to maintain property in good repair; no waste |
| 3, Insurance | Insure to full reinstatement value; policy in joint names or creditor's interest noted |
| 4, VAT | Debtor to pay VAT due on expenses |
| 5, Enforcement expenses | Debtor to repay creditor's enforcement costs |
| 6, Calling-up / Default | See Default section |
| 7, Realisation | Creditor's powers of sale, lease, management |
| 8, Removal of fixtures | No removal of fixtures without creditor consent |
| 9, Default | Events of default trigger creditor's powers |
| 10, Obligations | General compliance with all obligations |

[SCOTS: Note] These conditions are statutory and apply automatically unless expressly varied in the security deed. Any variation must be clearly stated and is uncommon for residential loans.

### 4. Due-on-Sale / Calling-Up

- The creditor may serve a calling-up notice (Form A, Schedule 6) requiring repayment within 2 months, On expiry of the notice period without payment, the creditor may exercise enforcement powers, Transfer of property without repayment: calling-up notice served on new proprietor if notice of transfer is not received

**Statutory transfers** (no calling-up required):
- Transfer to surviving spouse / civil partner on death, Transfer by inheritance, Transfer to trustee in sequestration / liquidator

### 5. Default & Calling-Up Procedure

**Calling-up (Schedule 6, Form A):**
- Serve calling-up notice requiring discharge of debt within 2 months, Notice must be served per s. 19 of the 1970 Act delivery rules, If debt not discharged: creditor may exercise enforcement powers (sale, etc.)

**Notice of Default (Schedule 6, Form B):**
- Where calling-up is not used, serve notice of default (Form B) specifying the default, Default notice must state the breach and give at least one month to remedy, If breach not remedied: creditor may exercise enforcement powers

**Remedies on default:**
- Power of sale (by private bargain or public roup/auction)
- Power to enter into possession, Power to let the property, Power to carry out necessary repairs, Power to apply for decree of foreclosure (s. 28)
- Application to court for warrant to sell or take possession

### 6. Enforcement Remedies

| Remedy | Procedure |
|---|---|
| Calling-up notice (Form A) | 2-month notice; automatic on expiry |
| Notice of default (Form B) | Usually 1+ month to remedy |
| Power of sale | After notice expires; court warrant may be needed |
| Foreclosure decree | Court application (s. 28); transfers title to creditor |
| Entry into possession | Physical possession to manage/let |
| Appointing receiver | For commercial property; not statutory |

**Priority of proceeds from sale:**
1. Expenses of sale / enforcement
2. Principal, interest and expenses due to creditor
3. Subsequent security holders (in ranking order)
4. Surplus to proprietor

### 7. Additional Clauses

- **All sums secured** - standard security secures all sums due from debtor to creditor (current and future), not just the initial loan
- **Ranking** - specify ranking priority over/subordinate to other securities
- **Further advances** - whether the security covers subsequent advances (common in "all sums" securities)
- **Assignation/Transfer** - prohibition or restrictions on assignation; creditor may assign without consent
- **Prescribed rate of interest** - default / contractual interest rates specified
- **Property management** - creditor's powers to manage, repair, insure on default
- **Waiver of common law rights** - debtor waives common law rights inconsistent with the security (e.g., right to fixtures)

### 8. Schedules/Riders (Scottish Context)

| Schedule | When |
|----------|------|
| Flat/Tenement | Common property, scheme burdens, maintenance obligations |
| Commercial Property | Different standard conditions; rent security, assignation |
| Buy-to-Let | Tenancy restrictions, rent assignments |
| Personal Guarantee | Separate deed; guarantor's personal obligation |
| Form A / Form B election | Choose statutory form per Schedule 2 |

Riders control over main instrument on conflict. Incorporate by reference.

### 9. Governing Law & Regulatory Compliance

- Governing law: Scotland (the law of Scotland)
- FCA regulation: if a regulated mortgage contract (Regulated Mortgage Contract under FSMA 2000 / MCOB), ensure compliance with:
  - FCA handbook MCOB (Mortgages and Home Finance: Conduct of Business)
  - Consumer Credit Act 1974 (if applicable)
  - Financial Services and Markets Act 2000
- The Mortgage Rights (Scotland) Act 2001 - gives the court power to suspend calling-up / enforcement in certain circumstances, The Home Owner and Debtor Protection (Scotland) Act 2010 - amended calling-up and default notice requirements, Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR (processing borrower data)

### 10. Execution & Registration

- Signature by all registered proprietors (granter(s))
- Must be attested: witnessed by one witness (Scots law, not notarisation unless executing for a company under seal)
- Registration: Land Register of Scotland (Registers of Scotland) - application for registration of the standard security, Registration creates the security interest; without registration it is void, LBTT (Land and Buildings Transaction Tax) return and registration, lien on title if unpaid

## Pitfalls

- **Jurisdiction first** - never assume Scotland and England share the same security law. They do not. Scotland uses Standard Securities under the 1970 Act; England uses mortgages/charges under the Law of Property Act 1925
- **Legal description must match title sheet exactly** - discrepancies can delay or void registration
- **All proprietors must sign** - missing signatures create title defects
- **Mark blanks clearly** - use `[PROPRIETOR NAME]`, `[PRINCIPAL AMOUNT]`, `[TITLE NUMBER]`
- **`[VERIFY]` all statutory citations** - the 1970 Act, standard conditions, MCOB rules, consumer credit rules
- **FCA compliance is mandatory** - regulated mortgage contracts must comply with MCOB, including pre-contract disclosure (ESIS), affordability assessment, and right to withdraw
- **The Mortgage Rights (Scotland) Act 2001** - court can suspend enforcement if debtor can repay within a reasonable period; mandatory consideration for residential properties
- **The Home Owner and Debtor Protection (Scotland) Act 2010** - requires 3 months' arrears before calling-up for residential owner-occupied properties; notice to debtor's spouse/civil partner, Format as a formal legal document ready for solicitor review, execution, and registration at Registers of Scotland

## Scotland/UK Adaptation

This skill was originally based on US mortgage/deed of trust practice (state law variations, TILA/RESPA, Garn-St. Germain, judicial/non-judicial foreclosure). It has been substantially rewritten for Scottish property law.

**Key changes:**
- Replaced US mortgage/deed of trust with Scottish Standard Security under the Conveyancing and Feudal Reform (Scotland) Act 1970
- Replaced state-law instrument selection with statutory forms (Form A/Form B per Schedule 2)
- Replaced TILA/RESPA/ECOA with FCA MCOB (regulated mortgage contracts) and Consumer Credit Act 1974
- Replaced judicial/non-judicial foreclosure distinction with calling-up (Form A) / notice of default (Form B) / foreclosure decree procedure, Replaced Garn-St. Germain due-on-sale exceptions with the 1970 Act's statutory framework and the Mortgage Rights (Scotland) Act 2001
- Replaced US property legal description (county, parcel ID) with Land Register Title Number, Ordnance Survey map reference, Replaced state recording offices with Registers of Scotland / Land Register, Replaced notarization/witness requirements with Scots execution rules (witnessed; notarisation for companies with seal)
- Replaced US redemption periods with Scottish equivalents (calling-up 2-month period; court power to suspend under the 2001 Act)
- Added the Mortgage Rights (Scotland) Act 2001 - key consumer protection not present in US mortgage law, Added the Home Owner and Debtor Protection (Scotland) Act 2010 - key consumer protections, Added LBTT, Stamp Duty Land Tax equivalent for Scottish property, Replaced US dollars with GBP, Replaced state and federal anti-deficiency rules with Scottish enforcement restrictions (calling-up notice periods, court suspension powers)
- Changed language to British English spelling (standard security, calling-up, proprietor, creditor, deed of assignation)
- Date format: DD/MM/YYYY

**Relevant forms:**
- Standard Security Form A (personal obligation + grant) / Form B (straight grant) - Schedule 2, Conveyancing and Feudal Reform (Scotland) Act 1970
- Calling-up Notice (Form A, Schedule 6)
- Notice of Default (Form B, Schedule 6)
- Registers of Scotland Standard Security templates, available from ros.gov.uk, See `/scots-forms/` directory for downloaded Scottish standard security forms

## Foreign-Law / US-Origin Guardrail

This skill may contain inherited US terminology. For Scotland/UK use, translate rather than copy. Examples: discovery is not Scots commission and diligence/recovery of documents; tort is generally delict in Scots civil analysis; summary judgment is not automatically the Scots summary decree test; bankruptcy concepts may map to sequestration, liquidation, administration, or restructuring depending on party and forum; HIPAA/CCPA/SEC/EEOC/FTC/CFPB concepts require UK GDPR, DPA 2018, FCA, ICO, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, tribunal, or sector-regulator mapping as appropriate. If the matter is genuinely US or foreign-law, quarantine the foreign-law analysis and warn that local counsel/source verification is required.
