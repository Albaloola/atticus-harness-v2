# UK Guidance: Shareholder Rights / Takeover Defences in the UK

## Key Differences from US Practice

### Poison Pills Are Heavily Restricted in the UK

**Unlike the US, poison pills (shareholder rights plans) are effectively prohibited** for companies subject to the UK Takeover Code.

The **City Code on Takeovers and Mergers (the Takeover Code)** administered by the **Panel on Takeovers and Mergers** severely restricts defensive measures:

**Rule 21 (Restrictions on Frustrating Action)** : Once an offer is made or is reasonably believed to be imminent, the board of the offeree company must not, without shareholder approval, take any action which could frustrate a bid. This includes:
- Issuing shares or granting options (would trigger a poison pill)
- Acquiring or disposing of material assets, Entering into contracts outside the ordinary course
- **Any action** that would frustrate the offer

**Result**: Poison pills / shareholder rights plans as known in US practice are **not used** for UK-incorporated companies subject to the Takeover Code.

### UK Takeover Defence Framework

Instead of poison pills, UK companies use:

1. **Pre-offer defence**: "Just say no" - board recommends rejection; persuade shareholders
2. **White knight** - find a friendly alternative bidder
3. **Shareholder engagement** - active investor relations to maintain support
4. **Pension fund trustees** - consultation on impact
5. **Competition / regulatory challenges** - UK CMA review or sectoral regulator
6. **Contractual protections** - change-of-control clauses in key contracts
7. **Poison put** - debt covenants triggered by change of control (limited use)

### Anti-Takeover Statute

- **Companies Act 2006, s.172** - director's duty to promote the success of the company for the benefit of shareholders (including stakeholder considerations)
- **No DGCL 203 equivalent** - the UK does not have a statutory moratorium on business combinations with significant shareholders (no "control share" statute)
- **Takeover Code General Principle 3**: The board must act in the interests of the company as a whole and not frustrate an offer

### Regulatory Framework

| US Concept | UK Equivalent |
|---|---|
| SEC | FCA (Financial Conduct Authority) |
| Exchange filing (Form 8-K) | RNS notification via RIS (Regulatory Information Service) |
| DGCL (Delaware) | Companies Act 2006 (UK-wide) |
| State anti-takeover statutes | Takeover Code (rules-based; no state-level variation) |
| Proxy advisors (ISS/Glass Lewis) | ISS / Glass Lewis (same firms, different policies) |
| SEC reporting (Schedule 13D/13G) | DTR 5 disclosure of major holdings (FCA) |
| Unocal/Revlon duties | Directors' duties under Companies Act 2006 s.172 |

### Disclosure of Major Holdings

- **DTR 5** - UK equivalent of Schedule 13D: thresholds at 3%, 5%, 10%, 15% etc.
- **Takeover Panel** - regulates takeovers (not the FCA)
- **Public announcements**: Required at offer announcement, consideration posting, acceptance levels

### Practical Note

The US poison pill skill has **very limited** direct application to UK practice because the core mechanism (flip-in/flip-over rights triggering on ownership thresholds) is incompatible with the Takeover Code's prohibition on frustrating action.

The **general corporate governance principles** - board fiduciary analysis, procedural documentation, and shareholder communication, are transferable, but the specific rights plan mechanics are not.

For UK practitioners:
- Shareholder rights plans may be used only for specific limited purposes (e.g., protecting tax losses, NOL planning, and even then, must comply with Takeover Code Rule 21)
- Any defensive measure requires shareholder approval if implemented after a bid is imminent, The Panel on Takeovers and Mergers will scrutinise and may waive Rules 9 and 21 in limited circumstances

Sources: City Code on Takeovers and Mergers (Rule 21); Companies Act 2006 ss.171-177 (directors' duties); FCA Disclosure Guidance and Transparency Rules (DTR 5); Practical Law UK, Takeover Defence.
