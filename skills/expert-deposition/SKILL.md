# Expert Witness Examination on Commission / Recovery of Evidence

## Atticus UK/Scots Legal Excellence Overlay

Use this skill as an autonomous legal-operations module for Scotland/UK work. Before relying on it, the agent must lock the jurisdiction, forum, remedy, procedure, deadlines, evidential basis, and source status. Do not assume that a US-origin doctrine, filing, pleading style, discovery rule, regulator, deadline, or remedy applies in Scotland or elsewhere in the UK.

### Mandatory operating rules

1. **Jurisdiction lock.** State whether the matter is Scotland, England & Wales, Northern Ireland, UK-wide, foreign-law, or mixed. If Scotland is plausible, distinguish sheriff court, Court of Session, tribunals, regulators, ombudsmen, and internal institutional processes.
2. **Official-source hierarchy.** Prefer legislation.gov.uk, Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service rules/forms, Court of Session and sheriff court rules, tribunal/regulator guidance, UK Supreme Court materials, GOV.UK, Scottish Government, ICO, FCA, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, Land Register of Scotland, registers of Scotland, and other primary public sources. Treat secondary commentary as orientation only.
3. **Live verification.** Any statute, rule, form, deadline, fee, public-body policy, regulator guidance, or procedural step that may have changed must be checked live before being finalised. Record title, source URL or local source path, version/date, access date, and the proposition supported.
4. **Evidence discipline.** Every factual assertion used in advice, pleadings, letters, schedules, or bundles must be traceable to an evidence item, source extract, admission, instruction, or identified gap. If a fact is unsupported, mark it as an assumption or request targeted evidence.
5. **Element-by-element reasoning.** Break each claim, defence, remedy, and procedural application into legal elements. Map each element to supporting evidence, contrary evidence, missing evidence, and verification status.
6. **Autonomous depth.** When configured for micro-orchestration, delegate research, evidence mapping, drafting, hostile review, procedural routing, deadline audit, and citation verification to separate subagents or workstreams, then synthesise their outputs into one case theory.
7. **External-action boundary.** Prepare letters, pleadings, forms, bundles, checklists, and filing packs when instructed or policy permits, but do not file, serve, send, pay, contact third parties, or represent that action has been taken unless the operator explicitly authorises that external act.
8. **Uncertainty handling.** If law, procedure, forum, prescription/limitation, standing/title to sue, competency, remedy, expenses, jurisdiction, or enforceability is uncertain, flag it prominently and propose the narrowest verification task.

### Expected work product

Where proportionate, produce a chronology, issue map, source log, evidence matrix, merits/risk table, remedy/damages table, procedural route note, draft document, bundle index, service/filing checklist, and operator handoff note. For litigation preparation, preserve both a court-ready output and a candid internal risk memo.

Builds a motion-ready expert examination record or defence-preparation plan targeting Scottish common law admissibility. Covers qualification limits, methodology attacks, bias/compensation exposure, and opinion lock-in.

[SCOTS: This skill has been adapted from US deposition practice to the Scottish legal system. The core principles of expert challenge remain valid, but the procedural mechanisms differ fundamentally. ## Prerequisites

1. Venue admissibility standard (Scottish common law, qualifications and necessity, no direct Daubert/Frye equivalent)
2. Expert report(s) and production materials (lodged as productions before proof under rules of court)
3. Expert CV, publications, and prior testimony list
4. Case facts and key documents (reviewed and not reviewed by expert)
5. Alternative theories, critiques, or rebuttal materials

[SCOTS: Note: Scotland has no direct equivalent of FRCP 26(a)(2) expert disclosure. Expert reports in Scotland must be lodged as productions before proof. Commission and Diligence (the Scottish equivalent of discovery) is governed by the Administration of Justice (Scotland) Act 1972 s 1 and rules of court (RCS Ch 35 for Court of Session; OCR Ch 27 for Sheriff Court).]

## Deliverable A: Taking Expert Examination on Commission

### Outline Structure

```
I.   Qualifications and Boundaries
II.  Engagement, Scope, and Compensation
III. Materials Reviewed and Assumptions
IV.  Methodology and Reliability
V.   Each Opinion (state, basis, certainty)
VI.  Application to Case Facts
VII. Prior Testimony and Publications
VIII.Lock-In and Completeness
```

### Question Bank

| Topic | Objective | Key Lines |
|---|---|---|
| Qualifications | Define limits | "Expertise is X, not Y?" / "How recent is hands-on work?" |
| Engagement/Bias | Expose incentives | "Rate?" / "Percent income from litigation?" / "Prior exclusions?" |
| Materials | Show gaps | "What did you review?" / "Did you see [critical doc]?" / "Independent investigation?" |
| Methodology | Build admissibility record | "Tested? Peer-reviewed? Error rate?" / "Governing standards?" / "Alternatives considered?" |
| Opinions | Lock in | "State opinion precisely." / "Basis and data?" / "Degree of certainty?" |
| Application | Test fit | "Inputs used?" / "If X fact wrong, opinion changes?" |
| Impeachment | Show inconsistency | "In [case/publication] you said ___?" / "What changed?" |

### Lock-In Questions

Always close with:
- "Are those all opinions you intend to offer?"
- "Any opinions not in your report?"
- "Any bases not stated in your report?"

### Admissibility Record Checklist

Flag each ground that applies (Scottish common law approach):

- [ ] Qualifications limited in relevant sub-field
- [ ] Method not testable or not tested
- [ ] No peer review or publication
- [ ] Error rate unknown or unacceptable
- [ ] No governing standards or standards not followed
- [ ] Opinion not necessary to assist the court (Scottish test)
- [ ] Unreliable application to case facts
- [ ] Insufficient facts/data or unverified assumptions
- [ ] Failure to consider contrary evidence
- [ ] Opinion strays beyond expert's field of expertise

### Work Product Boundaries (Scottish Rules of Court)

| Protected | Discoverable |
|---|---|
| Legal advice privilege, communications with solicitor for advice | Facts/data considered by expert |
| Litigation privilege, documents created for litigation purposes | Assumptions from counsel |
| Solicitor's mental impressions | Compensation terms |

[SCOTS: Note: Scotland does not have a direct equivalent of FRCP 26(b)(4) work product protection for draft expert reports. The Scottish protections are governed by legal advice privilege and litigation privilege (common law). There is no statutory protection for draft expert reports equivalent to FRCP 26(b)(4)(B)-(C). Counsel should proceed with caution and consider whether to commission expert reports through a solicitor to maximise privilege protection.]

### Objection Guide

- "Objection, privileged" for communications protected by legal advice privilege
- "Objection, not a proper question" for speculative or oppressive questioning, Preserve objections but allow testimony on facts/data considered

## Pitfalls

- **Wrong standard**: Confirm reliance on Scottish common law admissibility (qualifications + necessity); US Daubert/Frye jurisprudence has no direct application but may be referred to for persuasive value only.
- **Opinions beyond report**: Do not allow undisclosed opinions unless strategically beneficial; force explicit lock-in.
- **Evasive coaching**: Credibility depends on clarity and candour; never coach experts to evade.
- **Privilege waiver**: Preserve legal advice privilege and litigation privilege objections while allowing proper recovery into facts/data and assumptions.
- **Jurisdictional variance**: Flag differences between Court of Session ordinary procedure, Sheriff Court ordinary cause, and Sheriff Court simple procedure, each has different rules for recovery of evidence and commission for examination of witnesses.

[SCOTS: Note: The US concepts of "Daubert hearing" and "Frye hearing" have no direct Scottish equivalent. In Scotland, challenges to expert admissibility are typically made by preliminary motion or at proof (trial). The Scottish court's inherent discretion means there is no codified admissibility test equivalent to FRE 702. However, the principles of relevance, necessity, and proper qualification are well-established in Scots common law. Counsel may cite English authority (e.g., *Ikarian Reefer* [1993] or *Kennedy v Cordia (Services) LLP* 2016 SC (UKSC) 59) for persuasive guidance on expert duties.]

### Evidence Recovery Procedure (Scottish Equivalent of Discovery for Expert Materials)

1. **Commission and Diligence**: Apply by motion for commission to recover documents from havers (third parties holding relevant documents). Governed by RCS Ch 35 / OCR Ch 27.
2. **Optional Procedure**: For straightforward recovery without a hearing, lodged specification and order. RCS 35.3 / OCR 27.3.
3. **Commission for Examination of Witnesses**: Where a witness (including expert) cannot attend proof, apply for commission to take evidence on oath before a commissioner. Use CoS Form 35.4-A (citation of haver to commission) and 35.11-A (citation of witness to commission).

### How to Commission an Expert in Scotland

1. Identify need for expert evidence and field of expertise
2. Commission expert through solicitor, maximise litigation privilege by commissioning via solicitor's instruction letter
3. Expert produces report, this should be lodged as a production before proof
4. Expert should be cited to appear at proof or, if unavailable, commissioned for examination before commissioner
5. Opposing party may apply for commission and diligence to recover documents or specification of documents
6. Expert may be cross-examined at proof on qualifications, methodology, and opinions
