# UK/Scotland Expert Witness Guide

## Overview

This guide covers the key differences between US and UK/Scottish expert witness practice. Use alongside the main `expert-witness-report-analysis` skill when analysing expert reports under UK or Scottish law.

---

## 1. Admissibility Framework

### Scotland - _Kennedy v Cordia_ Test

The Scottish test for admitting expert evidence was authoritatively stated by the Supreme Court in _Kennedy v Cordia (Services) LLP_ [2016] UKSC 6:

**Five factors for admissibility:**
1. **Relevance** - Would the evidence help the court?
2. **Necessity** - Is it reasonably required to resolve proceedings?
3. **No usurpation** - Must not decide the ultimate issue (that is for the court)
4. **Reliable methodology** - Must be a recognised and sound discipline based on logical reasoning and evidence
5. **Expert's duty understood** - The expert must understand their overriding duty to the court

### England & Wales, CPR Part 35 / _Ikarian Reefer_

The same overriding duty applies under CPR Part 35, first clearly stated in _National Justice Compania Naviera SA v Prudential Assurance Co Ltd_ (The _Ikarian Reefer_) [1993] 2 Lloyd's Rep 68.

**CPR 35.3** - The expert's duty to the court:
- Overrides any obligation to the instructing party, Includes duty to provide independent, objective, unbiased opinion, Must state material facts and assumptions, Must state their qualifications, Must make clear if a question falls outside their expertise

### Key Differences from US Daubert/Frye

| US | UK/Scotland |
|----|-------------|
| Daubert hearing | No specific admissibility hearing; decided at proof |
| FRE 702 | CPR 35.5 / Chapter 35 RCS |
| Frye general acceptance | Relevant but not dispositive under _Kennedy v Cordia_ |
| Gatekeeping by trial judge | Gatekeeping by judge at proof (all evidence heard together) |
| Daubert factors checklist | _Kennedy v Cordia_ factors (less formalised) |
| FRCP 26(a)(2) disclosure | Chapter 35 RCS / CPR Part 35 disclosure rules |
| FRCP 37(c)(1) sanctions | Chapter 35.6 / CPR 35.13 sanctions |

### No Daubert Hearing in Scotland

There is no pre-trial admissibility hearing mechanism equivalent to a Daubert hearing in Scotland. The judge determines admissibility of expert evidence at proof (trial). Challenging an expert's evidence therefore typically requires:
- Written submissions on admissibility in advance, Submission to the judge during evidence, Cross-examination that exposes unreliability at proof

## 2. _The Ikarian Reefer_ Duties

The _Ikarian Reefer_ duties apply to all experts in UK and Scottish proceedings. They are more significant than anything in the US system. An expert who breaches these duties risks:
- Exclusion of evidence, Adverse costs orders, Referral to their professional body, Potential contempt of court

**The Seven Duties (from The _Ikarian Reefer_):**
1. Independent opinion, not influenced by litigant or instructions
2. Objective, unbiased opinion within expertise
3. State facts and assumptions on which opinion is based
4. Do not omit material facts that could detract from opinion
5. Make clear if a particular question falls outside expertise
6. If insufficient data, state that opinion is provisional
7. If opinion changes, notify instructing party and court promptly

These duties are now codified in the Civil Procedure Rules (England) and Chapter 35 RCS (Scotland) and endorsed by the Academy of Experts and Expert Institute.

## 3. Scottish Court Rules, Chapter 35 RCS

_Rules of the Court of Session 1994, Chapter 35 - Evidence_

**Key provisions:**

**Chapter 35.1 - Scope:** Applies to any action where a party intends to rely on expert evidence.

**Chapter 35.2 - Expert's duty:** Expert's overriding duty to the court; must be confirmed in the report.

**Chapter 35.3 - Content of report:**
- Statement of qualifications, Statement of expertise relevant to the opinion, Statement of the facts and instructions on which the opinion is based, Statement of the expert's duty to the court, List of cases in which the expert has given evidence in the preceding 5 years

**Chapter 35.4 - Single joint expert:** The court may appoint a single joint expert to report on any issue.

**Chapter 35.5 - Limitation of expert evidence:** Expert evidence is limited to what is reasonably required to resolve proceedings.

**Chapter 35.6 - Further information:** The court may order the expert to produce further information.

### Sheriff Court (Ordinary Cause Rules)

The Ordinary Cause Rules (OCR) contain similar provisions to Chapter 35 RCS, tailored for the Sheriff Court. Key differences:
- More emphasis on proportionality in lower-value claims, Simple Procedure (under £5,000) has very limited scope for expert evidence, The court may restrict expert evidence under the Courts Reform (Scotland) Act 2014

## 4. CPR Part 35 (England & Wales)

**CPR 35.1 - Duty to restrict:** Expert evidence shall be restricted to that which is reasonably required.

**CPR 35.3 - Experts' overriding duty:** Duty to the court.

**CPR 35.4 - Court's power to restrict:** Requires permission for expert evidence.

**CPR 35.5 - Form and content of report:**
- Must be addressed to the court, Must state qualifications and expertise, Must state facts and instructions, Must include a statement of truth (CPR 35.10)

**CPR 35.7 - Single joint expert:** Court may direct single joint expert.

**CPR 35.10 - Statement of truth:** Expert must sign a statement of truth confirming understanding of duty.

**CPR 35.12 - Discussion between experts:** Court may direct experts to discuss and prepare joint statement.

**CPR 35.13 - Consequence of failure to disclose:** Expert evidence may not be used without permission.

## 5. Single Joint Experts

Both Scottish and English/Welsh rules allow the court to appoint a single joint expert (SJE).

**Scotland (Chapter 35.4 RCS):**
- The court may, on its own initiative or on motion, appoint an SJE, The SJE is jointly instructed by the parties, The SJE's report is evidence for all parties, Less common in Scotland than in England

**England & Wales (CPR 35.7):**
- More common; the preferred approach where proportionate, Parties may agree on the SJE; if not, court decides, SJE gives written report; may be called for cross-examination

**Strategic considerations:**
- SJE reduces costs but limits adversarial challenge, SJE is appropriate when the issue is technical and uncontroversial, SJE is risky when the issue goes to the heart of the case and parties disagree on methodology, Both parties may still call their own experts with court permission

## 6. Productions Procedure (Scotland)

In Scotland, all documents, including expert reports, must be lodged as productions.

**Key features:**
- **No equivalent of US pre-trial disclosure of expert reports before trial for all purposes**
- Expert reports are lodged as productions under Chapter 4 of the Rules of the Court of Session, Productions must be lodged within prescribed timeframes, Opposing party can inspect productions, Expert reports not lodged as productions may not be admitted

**Process:**
1. Expert report prepared and provided to instructing solicitor
2. Solicitor lodges the report as a production
3. Counterparty can inspect and copy the production
4. At proof, the expert speaks to their production

**Commission and diligence** - Scotland's equivalent of discovery, can be used to recover underlying expert materials, drafts, instructions, and working files. This is broader than US discovery in some respects. Privilege in Scotland does not protect expert instructions to the same extent as the US.

## 7. Privilege Rules in Scotland

Privilege in Scotland differs from the US in important ways:

| Issue | Scotland | US |
|-------|----------|-----|
| Legal advice privilege | Similar scope | Broader scope |
| Litigation privilege | More limited | Broader |
| Expert instructions | Generally **not** privileged | Generally privileged |
| Expert drafts | May be recoverable | Protected by work product |
| Without-prejudice communications | Recognised (statutory basis) | Similar |
| Confidentiality vs. privilege | Distinct concepts | More unified approach |

**Practical implications:**
- Correspondence between solicitor and expert is more likely to be recoverable in Scotland than in the US, Expert's working files, drafts, and preliminary opinions may be subject to commission and diligence, The expert's duty to the court (Ikarian Reefer) overrides any claim of privilege for information relevant to the expert's opinion

## 8. Cross-Examination Themes (Scotland/UK)

In addition to the standard cross-examination themes, the following are particularly effective under UK/Scottish rules:

**Breach of duty to the court:**
- Was the expert instructed to reach a particular conclusion? (If yes, this breaches independence)
- Did the expert omit a material fact that contradicts their opinion? (Breach of duty)
- Is the expert's fee dependent on outcome? (Contingent fees for experts are prohibited)

**Ikarian Reefer checklist:**
1. Did they understand their overriding duty?
2. Did they state all material facts and assumptions?
3. Did they check their instructions were complete?
4. Did they make clear if a question fell outside their expertise?
5. Did they consider alternative hypotheses?

**Kennedy v Cordia checklist:**
1. Is the evidence relevant to the issues?
2. Is expert evidence necessary?
3. Does the opinion usurp the court's role (ultimate issue)?
4. Is the methodology reliable and logical?
5. Is the expert independent and unbiased?

## 10. Expenses / Costs

**Scotland:**
- Judicial expenses follow success ("loser pays" but qualified)
- Successful party generally entitled to expenses from the losing party, Expenses are taxed by the Auditor of Court, Legal Aid (Scotland) available for qualifying cases, No US-style contingency fees; speculative fee arrangements (SFAs) allowed, Damages-based agreements (DBAs) available (limited)

**England & Wales:**
- Loser pays (indemnity principle)
- Costs budgeting required in multi-track cases, Qualified one-way costs shifting (QOCS) in personal injury, Conditional fee agreements (CFAs) with success fees (limited post-Jackson)
- Damages-based agreements (DBAs)

## 11. Practice Checklist

- [ ] Confirm jurisdiction: Scotland (Chapter 35 RCS / OCR) or England & Wales (CPR Part 35)
- [ ] Check expert report content against Chapter 35.3 / CPR 35.10 requirements
- [ ] Verify statement of expert's duty to the court
- [ ] Check 5-year testimony list (Scotland) or "material change" statement
- [ ] Confirm report is addressed to the court (not the instructing party)
- [ ] Identify whether a single joint expert applies or has been proposed
- [ ] Check productions procedure compliance (Scotland)
- [ ] Note limitations period for the claim type (3 years PI / 5 years delict / 20-year long-stop)
- [ ] Flag any contingent, outcome-dependent, or litigation-only fee arrangements
- [ ] Identify commission and diligence targets for expert materials
- [ ] Consider whether an Ikarian Reefer / Kennedy v Cordia admissibility submission is warranted
- [ ] All damages in GBP (£)
