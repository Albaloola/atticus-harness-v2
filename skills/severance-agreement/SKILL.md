# Settlement Agreement (Scotland/UK)

## Atticus UK/Scots Legal Excellence Overlay

Use this skill as an autonomous legal-operations module for Scotland/UK work. Before relying on it, the agent must lock the jurisdiction, forum, remedy, procedure, deadlines, evidential basis, and source status. Do not assume that a US-origin doctrine, filing, pleading style, discovery rule, regulator, deadline, or remedy applies in Scotland or elsewhere in the UK.

### Mandatory operating rules

1. **Jurisdiction lock.** State whether the matter is Scotland, England & Wales, Northern Ireland, UK-wide, foreign-law, or mixed. If Scotland is plausible, distinguish sheriff court, Court of Session, tribunals, regulators, ombudsmen, and internal institutional processes.
2. **Official-source hierarchy.** Prefer legislation.gov.uk, Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service rules/forms, Court of Session and sheriff court rules, tribunal/regulator guidance, UK Supreme Court materials, GOV.UK, Scottish Government, ICO, FCA, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, Land Register of Scotland, registers of Scotland, and other primary public sources. Treat secondary commentary as orientation only.
3. **Live verification.** Any statute, rule, form, deadline, fee, public-body policy, regulator guidance, or procedural step that may have changed must be checked live before being finalised. Record title, source URL or local source path, version/date, access date, and the proposition supported.
4. **Evidence discipline.** Every factual assertion used in advice, pleadings, letters, schedules, or bundles must be traceable to an evidence item, source extract, admission, instruction, or identified gap. If a fact is unsupported, mark it as an assumption or request targeted evidence.
5. **Element-by-element reasoning.** Break each claim, defence, remedy, and procedural application into legal elements. Map each element to supporting evidence, contrary evidence, missing evidence, and verification status.
6. **Autonomous depth.** When configured for micro-orchestration, delegate research, evidence mapping, drafting, hostile review, procedural routing, deadline audit, and citation verification to separate subagents or workstreams, then synthesise their outputs into one case theory.
7. **External-action boundary.** Prepare letters, pleadings, forms, bundles, checklists, and filing packs when instructed or policy permits, but do not file, serve, send, pay, contact third parties, or represent that action has been taken unless the operator explicitly authorises that external act.
8. **Uncertainty handling.** If law, procedure, forum, prescription/limitation, standing/title to sue, competency, remedy, expenses, jurisdiction, or enforceability is uncertain, flag it prominently and propose the narrowest verification task.

### Expected work product

Where proportionate, produce a chronology, issue map, source log, evidence matrix, merits/risk table, remedy/damages table, procedural route note, draft document, bundle index, service/filing checklist, and operator handoff note. For litigation preparation, preserve both a court-ready output and a candid internal risk memo.

[SCOTS: Note] This skill is adapted from a US severance agreement framework. The Scottish/UK equivalent is a **Settlement Agreement** (formerly known as a Compromise Agreement) governed by section 147 of the Equality Act 2010 and section 203 of the Employment Rights Act 1996. The legal architecture is significantly different from the US OWBPA/DTSA/NLRA framework.

Drafts a litigation-resistant Settlement Agreement combining a comprehensive statutory-compliant waiver of employment claims, confidentiality protections, and post-employment obligations.

## Prerequisites

Collect before drafting:

1. **Employee**: full legal name, position, start date, termination date
2. **Compensation**: salary, accrued holiday pay/payment in lieu of notice (PILON), bonus/commission structure, share scheme (EMI/CSOP)
3. **Existing agreements**: employment contract, staff handbook, NDA, restrictive covenants
4. **Termination context**: individual redundancy, settlement of dispute, performance exit, mutual separation; group redundancy (collective consultation obligations under TULRCA 1992)
5. **Settlement terms**: payment amount/structure, agreed reference, PILON, contribution to legal fees, benefits continuation, outplacement
6. **Employer**: full legal name, any group companies
7. **Adviser**: details of independent legal adviser (solicitor or authorised representative)

## Drafting Workflow

### 1. Parties and Dates

- Employer legal name; define "Group Companies" if releasing subsidiary entities, Employee full legal name matching HMRC records, Termination date (last day of active employment)
- Effective date / settlement date (once signed by both parties + adviser's certificate received)

### 2. Settlement Payment / Severance Package

- **Termination payment**: amount, whether gross or net of tax (first £30,000 generally tax-free per HMRC, Employment Income Manual 14000)
- **Payment in lieu of notice (PILON)**: if contractual PILON clause; otherwise wages/salary to termination date plus statutory or contractual notice
- **Accrued holiday pay**: statutory minimum 5.6 weeks per year (Working Time Regulations 1998); contractual entitlement if greater
- **Bonus/commission**: any pro-rata or discretionary payment; check scheme rules
- **Share schemes**: treatment of EMI, CSOP, SAYE options per plan rules
- **Benefits continuation**: private medical insurance, income protection, life assurance, car/allowance, typically to notice period end
- **Legal fees contribution**: capped amount paid direct to employee's independent legal adviser (requirement for settlement agreement validity)
- **Agreed reference**: terms agreed in a schedule to the agreement
- **Payment conditioned on**: execution + adviser certificate + ongoing compliance

### 3. Release of Claims (Waiver)

[SCOTS: Note] In Scotland/UK, the waiver of employment claims is governed by the statutory conditions for settlement agreements. The employee must receive independent legal advice on the terms and effect of the agreement, and the adviser must hold a current practising certificate or be a relevant authorised person.

**Statutory conditions for a valid Settlement Agreement** (section 147 Equality Act 2010; section 203 Employment Rights Act 1996):
1. Must be in writing
2. Must relate to the particular proceedings / particular complaint
3. The employee must have received independent legal advice from a qualified adviser
4. The adviser must be identified and have appropriate professional indemnity insurance
5. The agreement must state the relevant statutory provisions

**Claims that can be waived** (subject to the statutory conditions):
- Unfair dismissal (Employment Rights Act 1996, section 111)
- Discrimination (Equality Act 2010)
- Whistleblowing detriment (Employment Rights Act 1996, Part IVA)
- Unlawful deduction from wages (Employment Rights Act 1996, Part II)
- Redundancy pay (Employment Rights Act 1996)
- Breach of contract

**Claims that CANNOT be waived**:
- Personal injury claims (unless specifically known and quantified)
- Future claims (waiver only applies to past claims)
- Certain statutory pension rights (Pensions Act 1995)
- TUPE transfer-related dismissals (limited exceptions)

### 4. Acas Code Compliance

[SCOTS: Note] The US OWBPA has no direct UK equivalent. Instead, the Acas Code of Practice on Settlement Agreements applies, along with these requirements:

- Employee must have time to consider the terms (Acas recommends 10 calendar days)
- Employee must have the opportunity to take independent legal advice (employer typically contributes to costs)
- The agreement must be "without prejudice" or use section 111A of the Employment Rights Act 1996 (pre-termination negotiations, not admissible in unfair dismissal claims except in cases of improper behaviour)
- No requirement for a minimum consideration period (unlike OWBPA's 21/45 days), but Acas guidance recommends "reasonable time"
- No statutory revocation period, revocation is a matter of contract (usually until signed by both parties)

### 5. Confidentiality Obligations

- **Scope**: trade secrets, confidential information, customer lists, pricing, financials, strategies, business plans, employee data, supplier terms
- **Duration**: trade secrets indefinite (common law / Trade Secrets Regulations 2018); other confidential information usually 2 to 5 years
- **Exceptions**: publicly available (not via breach), legally compelled (with advance notice), **protected disclosures** under Employment Rights Act 1996 section 43A to 43L, whistleblowing

**Protected disclosure carve-out, mandatory**:
The confidentiality clause must not restrict the employee's right to make a protected disclosure under Part IVA Employment Rights Act 1996 (whistleblowing), nor to report criminal offences to law enforcement. This is essential - *Bannerjee v The Royal Bank of Scotland* and related authorities.

### 6. Non-Disparagement

- **Employee scope**: no disparaging statements re company, group companies, officers, directors, products, services, including social media
- **Employer scope**: named individuals only, agreed reference sets the standard
- **Carve-outs**: truthful testimony, legally compelled statements, protected disclosures under ERA 1996

### 7. Restrictive Covenants

**General principle** (Scots/UK common law): A non-compete clause is unenforceable unless the employer can show it is no wider than necessary to protect a legitimate business interest. The default position is that restrictions in restraint of trade are void (*Nordenfelt v Maxim Nordenfelt Guns & Ammunition Co Ltd* [1894] AC 535).

| Type | Typical Limit | Enforceability |
|---|---|---|
| Non-compete | 3 to 6 months | Enforceable if reasonable; UK government consulting on 3-month cap |
| Customer non-solicitation | 6 to 12 months | More likely enforceable |
| Employee non-solicitation | 6 to 12 months | Enforceable if limited to material contacts |
| Non-dealing / non-employment | 6 to 12 months | More restrictive than non-solicitation; harder to justify |

**Key considerations**:
- The employer's legitimate business interests must be identified (trade connections, confidential information, workforce stability)
- Duration, geographic scope, and restricted activities must be no more than necessary, No consideration is required for post-termination restrictions if included in the original contract (but if added on termination, separate consideration is needed)
- Garden leave may be used as an alternative, Non-compete reform: The UK Government is consulting on limiting non-compete clauses to 3 months (see Law Commission report and ongoing consultation)
- **Blue pencil doctrine**: Scottish courts may strike unenforceable parts if the clause can be severed without rewriting it (*First PM Ltd v MacFarlane* 2015)

[SCOTS: Note] Unlike California, Scotland has no statutory ban on non-competes. Unlike Colorado/WA/IL, there are no income thresholds. The test is purely the common law restraint of trade doctrine applied to the particular facts.

### 8. Dispute Resolution

- Governing law: Scotland (the law of Scotland)
- Jurisdiction: Scottish courts (Sheriff Court or Court of Session depending on value) - but Employment Tribunal has exclusive jurisdiction for most employment claims, Arbitration: less common in Scotland/UK for employment disputes (Employment Tribunal is the primary forum)
- Mediation: increasingly common (Acas early conciliation is mandatory before ET claim)

### 9. Cooperation and Transition

- Employee cooperates in pending/future litigation, investigations, regulatory proceedings, Employer reimburses expenses; reasonable compensation for post-termination cooperation (unless under statutory obligation)

### 10. Representations and Boilerplate

**Employee represents**: read and understood agreement, consulted or had opportunity to consult independent legal adviser, signing voluntarily, property returned, no retained confidential information, no undisclosed claims known to them, received all wages and holiday pay due.

**Boilerplate**: entire agreement, no variation except in writing signed by both parties, severability (notwithstanding the blue pencil approach), no assignment by employee, no-waiver, non-admission of liability.

**Annexes** (required for a valid Scottish settlement agreement):
- **Annex A**: List of Particular Claims (the statutory claims being settled)
- **Annex B**: List of relevant statutory provisions (legislation under which claims are waived)
- **Annex C**: Adviser's certificate (including details of the independent adviser, confirming advice given, and insurance details)

### 11. Signature Block

Standard dual signature block (employee + authorised company signatory with title). Plus **Adviser's Certificate** (Annex C) signed by the independent legal adviser.

## Critical Compliance Checks

- **Settlement agreement validity**: the agreement must satisfy ALL conditions in section 147 Equality Act 2010 and section 203 Employment Rights Act 1996. Missing any one element renders the waiver void.
- **Independent legal advice**: the adviser must be independent (not the employer's solicitor, not the employer's in-house counsel). The adviser must have a current practising certificate and professional indemnity insurance.
- **Adviser's certificate**: this is a critical document, the employer will not pay the settlement sum until it is received.
- **£30,000 tax exemption**: the first £30,000 of a termination payment is generally tax-free (HMRC EIM 14000). PILON is fully taxable. Ensure proper HMRC reporting through RTI.
- **Protected disclosures**: confidentiality clauses must not restrict whistleblowing. *Bannerjee v The Royal Bank of Scotland* and *Parkins v Sodexho* are relevant authorities.
- **Without prejudice / section 111A**: pre-termination negotiations are protected but must be conducted in accordance with statutory requirements, improper behaviour (such as harassment, discrimination) loses protection.
- **TUPE**: if the exit is connected to a TUPE transfer, additional requirements apply (Regulation 4 TUPE 2006).
- **Collective redundancy**: if 20+ employees affected, collective consultation obligations under TULRCA 1992 must be followed. Failure can lead to protective awards.
- **COT3 agreements**: where Acas has been involved in early conciliation, a COT3 agreement (Acas-formulated settlement) is an alternative to a private settlement agreement.
- **Scottish court jurisdiction**: claims are brought in the Employment Tribunal (Scotland) or, for breach of contract claims over £5,000, the Sheriff Court or Court of Session.

## Scotland/UK Adaptation

This skill was originally based on US employment severance agreement practice. It has been substantially rewritten for the Scottish/UK legal environment.

**Key changes:**
- Replaced "severance agreement" with "Settlement Agreement" (the correct UK/Scotland term)
- Replaced OWBPA (US age discrimination waiver) with the statutory conditions under Equality Act 2010 / Employment Rights Act 1996
- Replaced DTSA (US Defend Trade Secrets Act) with Trade Secrets (Enforcement, etc.) Regulations 2018 / common law breach of confidence, Replaced NLRA Section 7 with Part IVA Employment Rights Act 1996 (protected disclosures / whistleblowing)
- Replaced US state-specific non-compete laws with Scots common law restraint of trade doctrine, Replaced EEOC (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission) with Employment Tribunal (Scotland) / Acas, Replaced Title VII, ADA, ADEA with Equality Act 2010
- Replaced COBRA with UK NHS (no equivalent)
- Replaced California § 1542 waiver with Scots/UK approach to unknown claims (can waive claims known to exist, but blanket waivers of unknown claims are less certain)
- Replaced "at-will employment" with statutory employment protections (unfair dismissal, wrongful dismissal)
- Replaced Federal/State courts with Employment Tribunal, Sheriff Court, Court of Session, Added requirement for independent legal advice and adviser's certificate (unique to UK/Scotland)
- Added reference to HMRC tax treatment of termination payments (£30,000 exemption)
- Date format changed to DD/MM/YYYY, Currency changed from USD to GBP (£)

**Relevant forms:**

## Foreign-Law / US-Origin Guardrail

This skill may contain inherited US terminology. For Scotland/UK use, translate rather than copy. Examples: discovery is not Scots commission and diligence/recovery of documents; tort is generally delict in Scots civil analysis; summary judgment is not automatically the Scots summary decree test; bankruptcy concepts may map to sequestration, liquidation, administration, or restructuring depending on party and forum; HIPAA/CCPA/SEC/EEOC/FTC/CFPB concepts require UK GDPR, DPA 2018, FCA, ICO, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, tribunal, or sector-regulator mapping as appropriate. If the matter is genuinely US or foreign-law, quarantine the foreign-law analysis and warn that local counsel/source verification is required.
