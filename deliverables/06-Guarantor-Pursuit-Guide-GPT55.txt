2. Legal arguments to stop or pause pursuit of Leila

A. Vulnerability and proportionality

Leila's disability, PIP status, refugee family reunion status, lack of assets, and benefits-only income do not automatically cancel a guarantee. They do, however, make active enforcement difficult to justify as a practical and reputational decision.

Omar's point should be:

"You may say the guarantee exists. I am asking you to exercise judgment about enforcement. Leila has no disposable income, no assets, and disability-related vulnerability. A sheriff decree would not create money she does not have. It would only increase distress, costs and complaint risk. Please pause enforcement and review whether continuing against her is proportionate."

B. Equality Act 2010

If Leila is disabled, the university should not treat her unfavourably because of something arising from disability unless it can justify the treatment. The university should also make reasonable adjustments to the way it communicates, assesses affordability, and handles collection where disability creates disadvantage.

This does not mean the university must automatically write off a valid debt. It does mean it should consider disability properly before taking enforcement steps, especially where it has been told that Leila is on PIP and benefits-only income.

Practical Equality Act points:

1. Ask for a vulnerability and equality impact review before further enforcement.
2. Ask for communication adjustments: written communication, extra time, no pressure calls, one named contact, and no demand for unaffordable payments.
3. Ask the university to explain how it considered Leila's disability before accepting or pressing any payment plan.
4. Ask whether the £150 per month plan was assessed against her actual benefits income and disability-related expenses.

C. Public sector equality duty

Edinburgh Napier University is a public university and may have public-sector equality duties when exercising public functions. Omar should use this carefully. The point is not "the Equality Act cancels the debt". The point is:

"Before enforcing against a disabled guarantor with no assets and benefits-only income, the university should be able to show that it had due regard to disability-related disadvantage and considered a less harmful option."

D. Affordability and consent to the £150 plan

ANF-SRC-0102 records that the university has accepted a £150 per month plan from Leila. Omar should not argue that the plan never exists if the document says it does. He should instead say:

1. Omar did not agree it.
2. The family needs the university to confirm who proposed it, who accepted it, and on what affordability evidence.
3. If Leila agreed under stress, without understanding the consequences, or without affordability assessment, the university should not treat it as a safe basis for enforcement.
4. A PIP recipient with benefits-only income may not be able to sustain £150 per month without hardship.

E. Underlying complaint and arrears dispute

The guarantor issue should be tied to the wider complaint. The arrears did not arise in isolation. The record includes accommodation arrears, hardship, counselling closure, damp and mould, and urgent accommodation pressure. That gives Omar leverage to ask the university to treat guarantor enforcement as part of the complaint outcome rather than as a detached finance recovery exercise.

3. Scottish law on guarantors in this situation

In Scotland, a guarantor is often described as a cautioner. If a valid written guarantee was signed, the creditor can usually pursue the guarantor when the principal debtor does not pay, depending on the wording. The guarantor's poverty, disability, or benefits income does not by itself make the guarantee void.

The main legal and practical points are:

1. The university must prove the guarantee, the debt, the amount, and that the conditions for calling on the guarantee have been met.
2. Omar should ask for the signed guarantee, full accommodation agreement, arrears ledger, notices, payment history, hardship decisions, and any correspondence where Leila allegedly agreed the £150 plan.
3. If the wording is unclear, unsigned, not properly incorporated, or does not cover the sum now claimed, that can be challenged.
4. If Leila did not understand what she was signing, was pressured, or was not given the relevant documents, those facts may support a challenge, but they need evidence. They should not be overstated without proof.
5. Even if decree is granted, enforcement against someone with no wages, no assets, and benefits-only income may recover little or nothing.

5. Likely Sheriff Court outcome

The realistic court risk is mixed.

If the university has a properly signed guarantor document, a clear accommodation debt, a reliable ledger, and no strong defence to the guarantee, a sheriff may grant decree for the sum proved. A sheriff is unlikely to refuse decree only because Leila is disabled, on PIP, a refugee, or has no assets.

However, those facts matter in three ways:

1. They may support time-to-pay or a very low instalment position if decree is granted.
2. They make enforcement less useful if Leila has no wages, no attachable assets, and benefits-only income.
3. They strengthen the argument that the university should resolve the matter through complaint handling rather than court action.

If the sum is over £5,000, ordinary cause may be relevant unless the claim is restricted. If the claim is £5,000 or less, simple procedure may be relevant. This must be checked against the exact sum the university intends to claim, any interest or costs, and current Scottish court rules before any court-facing document is finalised.

Practical prediction:

1. Best outcome before court: university pauses or abandons guarantor enforcement as part of complaint resolution.
2. Realistic negotiated outcome: hold on enforcement, reduced payment, write-off of costs or interest, or no action while Anfal's complaint is reviewed.
3. Court risk if documents are strong: decree may be granted, but recovery may be minimal.
4. Court risk if documents are incomplete or poor: Omar can press for proof of the guarantee, amount, notices, and fairness of enforcement.

6. Debt collection process and protections

Likely stages:

1. Internal finance demands.
2. Referral to legal services or external debt collection.
3. Letter before court action or formal demand.
4. Sheriff court claim.
5. Decree if the university succeeds or if there is no defence.
6. Charge for payment and possible enforcement by sheriff officers.
7. Possible bank arrestment, earnings arrestment, attachment, or other diligence depending on circumstances.

Protections and practical limits:

1. Benefits-only income limits what can realistically be recovered.
2. No wages means earnings arrestment is not useful.
3. No assets means attachment is unlikely to recover much and essential items have protections.
4. Bank arrestment has protections and may be challengeable where funds are benefits or needed for basic living.
5. Time-to-pay may be available in Scottish debt proceedings, depending on the procedure and timing.
6. Vulnerability should be raised early and repeated in writing.
7. Omar should keep all communication written or confirm phone calls by email.
