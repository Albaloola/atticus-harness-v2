# [SCOTS] Jury Directions (Judge's Charge)

## Atticus UK/Scots Legal Excellence Overlay

Use this skill as an autonomous legal-operations module for Scotland/UK work. Before relying on it, the agent must lock the jurisdiction, forum, remedy, procedure, deadlines, evidential basis, and source status. Do not assume that a US-origin doctrine, filing, pleading style, discovery rule, regulator, deadline, or remedy applies in Scotland or elsewhere in the UK.

### Mandatory operating rules

1. **Jurisdiction lock.** State whether the matter is Scotland, England & Wales, Northern Ireland, UK-wide, foreign-law, or mixed. If Scotland is plausible, distinguish sheriff court, Court of Session, tribunals, regulators, ombudsmen, and internal institutional processes.
2. **Official-source hierarchy.** Prefer legislation.gov.uk, Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service rules/forms, Court of Session and sheriff court rules, tribunal/regulator guidance, UK Supreme Court materials, GOV.UK, Scottish Government, ICO, FCA, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, Land Register of Scotland, registers of Scotland, and other primary public sources. Treat secondary commentary as orientation only.
3. **Live verification.** Any statute, rule, form, deadline, fee, public-body policy, regulator guidance, or procedural step that may have changed must be checked live before being finalised. Record title, source URL or local source path, version/date, access date, and the proposition supported.
4. **Evidence discipline.** Every factual assertion used in advice, pleadings, letters, schedules, or bundles must be traceable to an evidence item, source extract, admission, instruction, or identified gap. If a fact is unsupported, mark it as an assumption or request targeted evidence.
5. **Element-by-element reasoning.** Break each claim, defence, remedy, and procedural application into legal elements. Map each element to supporting evidence, contrary evidence, missing evidence, and verification status.
6. **Autonomous depth.** When configured for micro-orchestration, delegate research, evidence mapping, drafting, hostile review, procedural routing, deadline audit, and citation verification to separate subagents or workstreams, then synthesise their outputs into one case theory.
7. **External-action boundary.** Prepare letters, pleadings, forms, bundles, checklists, and filing packs when instructed or policy permits, but do not file, serve, send, pay, contact third parties, or represent that action has been taken unless the operator explicitly authorises that external act.
8. **Uncertainty handling.** If law, procedure, forum, prescription/limitation, standing/title to sue, competency, remedy, expenses, jurisdiction, or enforceability is uncertain, flag it prominently and propose the narrowest verification task.

### Expected work product

Where proportionate, produce a chronology, issue map, source log, evidence matrix, merits/risk table, remedy/damages table, procedural route note, draft document, bundle index, service/filing checklist, and operator handoff note. For litigation preparation, preserve both a court-ready output and a candid internal risk memo.

Drafts proposed jury directions tailored to the case, jurisdiction, and charges/issues at trial.

## Prerequisites

Gather before drafting:

- **Indictment / pleadings** - charges, counterclaims (civil), agreed facts, preliminary rulings
- **Forum** - High Court of Justiciary, Sheriff Court (solemn), or Court of Session (civil jury)
- **Charges / issues** - all charges/heads, defences, statutory/common law basis
- **Verdict form** - criminal: guilty / not guilty / not proven; civil: majority answers to questions/interlocutor
- **Key evidence** - expert witnesses, productions, admissions, agreed minutes
- **Scottish Jury Manual** - current edition for criminal; Practice Note for civil

## Direction Sequence

Draft in this order using numbered paragraphs with descriptive headings.

### 1. Preliminary Directions

Cover: jury's role as sole judge of fact, evidence limitations (no speculation/prejudice/outside research), conduct rules (no independent enquiry, no social media), deliberation expectations.

- [ ] Use Scottish Jury Manual standard preliminary directions
- [ ] Adapt to procedure (solemn criminal / civil jury)
- [ ] Specifically: Scotland has no opening charge, preliminary directions are given at close of evidence before closing speeches (criminal) or after speeches and before the charge (civil)

### 2. Case Statement

- Neutral, non-argumentative summary from the indictment/pleadings, agreed facts, and judicial admissions, Identify parties by name and role; state nature of charges/dispute and chronology
- **Never** use characterisations favourable to Crown or defence / pursuer or defender

### 3. Legal Definitions

- Define every legal term before it appears in the directions and instructions on law, Source hierarchy: (1) statute (Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995, etc.) → (2) Scottish Jury Manual → (3) binding authority (High Court of Justiciary / Court of Session)
- Build progressively, foundational terms first, compound concepts second, Key Scottish-specific terms corroboration, sufficiency, the Moorov doctrine, mutual corroboration, special capacity, art and part liability

### 4. Directions on Elements

For **each** charge/issue/defence:

1. Identify the essential facts the Crown/pursuer must prove
2. State the standard of proof
3. Explain each element in case-specific context
4. Address circumstantial evidence inferences for mental state
5. State corroboration requirements (criminal, Scotland requires corroboration for every fact in issue)
6. Make contingent-charge or alternative-verdict options explicit

- [ ] Cite statute or binding authority per element
- [ ] Cross-reference Scottish Jury Manual directions
- [ ] Flag special defences (alibi, self-defence, automatism, incrimination) and the incidents of proof
- [ ] Solicit admissions on uncontroversial directions from the Crown/defence (criminal) or both solicitors (civil)

### 5. Burden and Standard of Proof

| Standard | Context | Core Language |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| Beyond reasonable doubt | Criminal, all charges | The Crown must prove every essential fact beyond reasonable doubt; if you are not satisfied, you must acquit |
| Balance of probabilities | Civil jury (Court of Session) | The pursuer must prove their case on the balance of probabilities, more likely than not |

- **Corroboration (criminal only):** In Scots law, no essential fact may be proved by the evidence of a single witness. Where the evidence of one witness requires to be corroborated, you must ask: is there other evidence, direct or circumstantial, that confirms or supports that account in a material respect?
- **Burden never shifts** - the Crown/pursuer bears the burden throughout, Use Scottish Jury Manual approved language for each standard

### 6. Evidence Evaluation

**Evidence types:** Direct and circumstantial carry equal weight. Agreed facts (admissions, minutes of agreement) accepted without further proof.

**Credibility and reliability:** Distinguish credibility (truthfulness) from reliability (accuracy). Factors: opportunity to observe, consistency, motive/bias, demeanour, corroboration.

**Expert witnesses:** Weigh qualifications, methodology, basis of opinion. Jury may accept or reject expert evidence in whole or part.

**Locked box direction (criminal):** In cases involving silence or no comment (Criminal Justice (Scotland) Act 2016 s. 32; Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 s. 266), direct the jury on what inferences may properly be drawn and the limits.

### 7. Deliberation and Verdict

**Criminal:**
- [ ] Unanimity not required, majority verdict is sufficient (per s. 90A Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995)
- [ ] 15 jurors: simple majority convictions possible (8+ votes)
- [ ] Three verdicts: guilty, not guilty, not proven
- [ ] Verdict options for each charge, indicate alternative verdicts (e.g. murder → culpable homicide) (s. 102 / common law)
- [ ] Foreperson selection, junior juror (traditionally); ask court's practice
- [ ] Walk through the verdict paper(s) with routing logic

**Verdict routing pattern (criminal):**

```
Charge 1: Murder
Available verdicts: (a) Guilty of Murder - (b) Not Guilty of Murder but Guilty of Culpable Homicide - (c) Not Guilty - (d) Not Proven

Directions on the elements distinguishing murder from culpable homicide
must be given separately.
```

**Civil (Court of Session jury):**
- [ ] 12 jurors; majority of 9 required for verdict
- [ ] Questions for the jury, proposed by the court in consultation with counsel
- [ ] Questions are answered yes/no or with a quantum of damages
- [ ] Standard: questions + damages assessment

If damages at issue: explain each head of damages (solatium, past/future wage loss, services, etc.) with jury guidelines on reasonable compensation.

## Checks

- [ ] **Scottish Jury Manual first** - work from current edition; note all departures and justify them
- [ ] **Plain language** - formal but accessible; define every technical term before use
- [ ] **Internal consistency** - definitions match usage in elements; burden and corroboration align with element assignments
- [ ] **Neutral throughout** - no argumentative or prejudicial language; avoid hinting at the judge's view
- [ ] **Citations verified** - every cite current and binding; mark uncertain with [VERIFY]
- [ ] **Appellate durability** - when in doubt, hew to approved Scottish Jury Manual language
- [ ] **Numbered paragraphs** - for charge conference and note of directions reference
- [ ] **Corroboration direction** - mandatory in criminal charges; must be tailored to the evidence
- [ ] **Not proven direction** - if the jury is not satisfied of guilt but also not satisfied of innocence, the not proven verdict is open

## Scotland/UK Adaptation

This skill has been adapted for use under Scottish criminal and civil jury procedure. Key differences from the original US approach:

| US Concept | Scottish/UK Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Jury instructions (civil and criminal) | Judge's charge / jury directions (criminal, High Court of Justiciary / Sheriff Court solemn); judge's charge (civil, Court of Session) |
| Pattern jury instructions | Scottish Jury Manual (current edition, published by the Judicial Institute for Scotland) |
| Unanimous verdict | Majority verdict: criminal, simple majority (8 of 15 for conviction); civil, majority of 9 of 12 |
| Preponderance of evidence / clear and convincing / beyond reasonable doubt | Criminal: beyond reasonable doubt only; civil (balance of probabilities only) / civil jury rare in Scotland |
| Special verdict / general verdict | Criminal: guilty / not guilty / not proven; Civil: questions for jury + damages assessment |
| Fifth / Sixth Amendment rights | Article 6 ECHR; Criminal Justice (Scotland) Act 2016 (right to silence and inferences); s. 266 Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 (accused as witness) |
| FRCP / state rules of criminal procedure | Act of Adjournal (Criminal Procedure Rules); Rules of the Court of Session (civil jury); Sheriff Court Rules |
| No equivalents, corroboration requirement (most US states have abolished) | Corroboration is a fundamental requirement in Scottish criminal law, every essential fact must be corroborated |
| No equivalents, three-verdict system | Guilty / not guilty / not proven, unique to Scotland |
| Federal Rules of Evidence | Law of evidence in Scotland (common law / statutory) - different hearsay, character, and corroboration rules |
| Opening statement / closing argument | Crown speech / defence speech (criminal, in that order); counsel's submissions (civil) - after closing speeches, the judge charges |

For criminal bench: The Scottish Jury Manual is the authoritative source. For civil jury trials: Court of Session practice notes and case law govern. Civil jury trials in Scotland are limited to certain categories of action (personal injury, defamation, etc.) and are rare.

## Foreign-Law / US-Origin Guardrail

This skill may contain inherited US terminology. For Scotland/UK use, translate rather than copy. Examples: discovery is not Scots commission and diligence/recovery of documents; tort is generally delict in Scots civil analysis; summary judgment is not automatically the Scots summary decree test; bankruptcy concepts may map to sequestration, liquidation, administration, or restructuring depending on party and forum; HIPAA/CCPA/SEC/EEOC/FTC/CFPB concepts require UK GDPR, DPA 2018, FCA, ICO, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, tribunal, or sector-regulator mapping as appropriate. If the matter is genuinely US or foreign-law, quarantine the foreign-law analysis and warn that local counsel/source verification is required.

## Final Quality Gate (Mandatory)

Before marking the task complete, confirm:

- Jurisdiction/forum/procedure have been identified and are not imported from the wrong legal system.
- Current law, rules, forms, fees, deadlines, and public-body guidance have been verified from official sources where necessary.
- Every material factual assertion is tied to evidence, a source, an admission, an instruction, or a clearly labelled assumption.
- Prescription, limitation, time bar, appeal periods, service rules, competency, standing/title to sue, expenses/costs exposure, and enforcement have been considered where relevant.
- The output separates client-facing conclusions from internal risk analysis.
- Drafts include placeholders only where evidence or instructions are genuinely missing; no fabricated citations, authorities, quotes, dates, forms, or procedural steps are allowed.
- A hostile reviewer could reconstruct the reasoning from the evidence matrix and source log.
