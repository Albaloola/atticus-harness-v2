# UK Guidance: Legal Letters in Scotland

## Key Differences from US Practice

### Personal Injury Claims, Scotland

- **Pre-litigation Protocol**: Scotland has the Pre-action Protocol for Personal Injury Claims (voluntary best practice under the Courts Reform (Scotland) Act 2014)
- **Limitation Period**: Prescription and Limitation (Scotland) Act 1973 - personal injury: 3 years from injury date or date of knowledge (not 2 years as in some US states)
- **No contingency fees**: Conditional fee agreements (CFAs) and speculative fee arrangements are available, but damages-based agreements are limited. No US-style contingency (typically 30-40%) - Scottish solicitors use "no win, no fee" arrangements with a success fee (uplift) capped at 100%
- **Judicial expenses**: "Loser pays" principle, the unsuccessful party typically pays the successful party's costs (judicial expenses). Parties may seek sanction for counsel.
- **No punitive damages**: Aggravated damages may be available in some delict cases, but punitive/exemplary damages are not recognised in Scots law

### Settlement Letters / Offers

- **Judicial tender**: A formal offer to settle (tender) made to court, if pursuer beats the tender at proof, expenses follow success; if not, pursuer may be liable for defender's expenses from the tender date
- **Without Prejudice**: Same concept as US, but Scottish courts may consider without-prejudice communications for limited purposes (per common law)
- **Section 2 (Civil Evidence) letters**: Letters written "without prejudice" but marked to reserve the right to refer to them for costs arguments (similar to Part 36 in England, not a Scottish term)

### Cease and Desist

- **Interdict** is the Scottish equivalent of injunction, Can apply for **interim interdict** (temporary) on an ex parte or on notice basis
- **Intimations**: In Scotland, interdict proceedings require serving the interdict and a copy of the petition/court documents
- **Breach of interdict**: Can result in arrest, fine, or imprisonment (contempt of court, Scottish procedure)

### Statutory Notices / Formal Correspondence

- **Data Protection**: UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018 - use the ICO template for subject access requests and data breach notifications
- **Termination letters**: Requirements of Writing (Scotland) Act 1995 for formal notices in property contexts
- **Missives**: In property disputes, a formal letter may constitute or breach concluded missives
- **Formal demands**: Charge for payment (a formal demand served by sheriff officer) is the Scottish equivalent of a demand letter before enforcement

### Address Format for Scotland

- City/town, POSTCODE (not ZIP)
- County not used, replace with local authority area if needed, e.g., "Edinburgh, EH1 1AA" or "Glasgow, G2 5SG"

### Court References

- **Sheriff Court**: Most personal injury cases start here (Monetary limit raised by Courts Reform (Scotland) Act 2014 - currently claims up to £100,000 for PI, up to £200,000 for other claims)
- **Court of Session**: Higher value or complex cases
- **Case numbers**: Format differs, e.g., "FAL/123/24" (Sheriff Court) or "CS123/24" (Court of Session)
- **All-Scotland Personal Injury Court**: Since 2015, some PI cases can be raised in Edinburgh Sheriff Court as the All-Scotland court

## Practical Note

The skill's general framework for professional legal correspondence transfers well. The main adaptations are:
1. Replace US limitation periods with Scottish prescription rules
2. Replace FRE 408 with without-prejudice / judicial tender rules
3. Replace US court references with Sheriff Court / Court of Session
4. Remove contingency fee references and explain Scottish fee arrangements
5. Use interdict instead of injunction
6. Use UK GDPR instead of HIPAA where relevant
7. Replace State-specific statute cites with Scottish equivalents

Sources: Prescription and Limitation (Scotland) Act 1973; Courts Reform (Scotland) Act 2014; Civil Evidence (Scotland) Act 1988; Scottish Civil Justice Council guidance; Law Society of Scotland practice notes.
