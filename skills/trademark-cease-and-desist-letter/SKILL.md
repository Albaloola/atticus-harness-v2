# Trademark Cease-and-Desist Letter (UK/Scotland Adaptation)

## Atticus UK/Scots Legal Excellence Overlay

Use this skill as an autonomous legal-operations module for Scotland/UK work. Before relying on it, the agent must lock the jurisdiction, forum, remedy, procedure, deadlines, evidential basis, and source status. Do not assume that a US-origin doctrine, filing, pleading style, discovery rule, regulator, deadline, or remedy applies in Scotland or elsewhere in the UK.

### Mandatory operating rules

1. **Jurisdiction lock.** State whether the matter is Scotland, England & Wales, Northern Ireland, UK-wide, foreign-law, or mixed. If Scotland is plausible, distinguish sheriff court, Court of Session, tribunals, regulators, ombudsmen, and internal institutional processes.
2. **Official-source hierarchy.** Prefer legislation.gov.uk, Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service rules/forms, Court of Session and sheriff court rules, tribunal/regulator guidance, UK Supreme Court materials, GOV.UK, Scottish Government, ICO, FCA, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, Land Register of Scotland, registers of Scotland, and other primary public sources. Treat secondary commentary as orientation only.
3. **Live verification.** Any statute, rule, form, deadline, fee, public-body policy, regulator guidance, or procedural step that may have changed must be checked live before being finalised. Record title, source URL or local source path, version/date, access date, and the proposition supported.
4. **Evidence discipline.** Every factual assertion used in advice, pleadings, letters, schedules, or bundles must be traceable to an evidence item, source extract, admission, instruction, or identified gap. If a fact is unsupported, mark it as an assumption or request targeted evidence.
5. **Element-by-element reasoning.** Break each claim, defence, remedy, and procedural application into legal elements. Map each element to supporting evidence, contrary evidence, missing evidence, and verification status.
6. **Autonomous depth.** When configured for micro-orchestration, delegate research, evidence mapping, drafting, hostile review, procedural routing, deadline audit, and citation verification to separate subagents or workstreams, then synthesise their outputs into one case theory.
7. **External-action boundary.** Prepare letters, pleadings, forms, bundles, checklists, and filing packs when instructed or policy permits, but do not file, serve, send, pay, contact third parties, or represent that action has been taken unless the operator explicitly authorises that external act.
8. **Uncertainty handling.** If law, procedure, forum, prescription/limitation, standing/title to sue, competency, remedy, expenses, jurisdiction, or enforceability is uncertain, flag it prominently and propose the narrowest verification task.

### Expected work product

Where proportionate, produce a chronology, issue map, source log, evidence matrix, merits/risk table, remedy/damages table, procedural route note, draft document, bundle index, service/filing checklist, and operator handoff note. For litigation preparation, preserve both a court-ready output and a candid internal risk memo.

Draft a formal demand letter requiring immediate trademark-use cessation, corrective action, and preserving all UK/Scottish legal remedies.

**⚠️ This template is a letter before action in the UK/Scottish context. The Pre-Action Protocol for Intellectual Property Claims (Court of Session / Sheriff Court) or the IPO Tribunal pre-action correspondence should be considered. This is not a court document and is not filed, it is a demand letter preparatory to proceedings.**

## Quick Start

Collect these before drafting:

- [ ] Jurisdiction is UK; primary Scottish courts if litigation is anticipated (Court of Session for high-value; Sheriff Court for claims under £100k)
- [ ] Sender authority: trademark owner, principal, legal representative
- [ ] **Rights evidence**: UKIPO registration number/class/dates OR common-law goodwill built up through use (passing off)
- [ ] **Infringement evidence**: 3+ instances with dates, locations, URLs, screenshots, ads, SKUs
- [ ] **Claimed harm**: confusion indicators, reputational damage, diversion, dilution (if mark qualifies under s.10(3) Trade Marks Act 1994), competitive injury
- [ ] **Requested relief**: cease window, removal scope, inventory destruction/delivery-up, accounting period, proof format
- [ ] Delivery method: email + recorded delivery post (or Sheriff Officer service in Scotland if formal service required)

- [ ] **Verify UKIPO registration is in force** - check renewal status
- [ ] **Verify mark not vulnerable to revocation** - check non-use period (5 years continuous non-use is a revocation ground under s.46 TMA 1994)

## Letter Sections

Draft every section below in order.

| # | Section | Content |
|---|---------|---------|
| 1 | **Header** | Sender/recipient identities, date, `Re: Letter before action, Trade Mark Infringement of "[MARK]"`. If counsel represents sender, include firm and Law Society of Scotland / SRA contact block. |
| 2 | **Rights basis** | *Registered*: mark, UKIPO registration number, filing/registration date, classes, goods/services, channels, territory. *Unregistered (Passing Off)*: establish goodwill, first/commercial use date, continuous use, promotion, reputation, distinctiveness (claimant's goodwill in the mark). |
| 3 | **Infringing acts** | Fact-based timeline; tie each instance to date, location, and evidence exhibit. |
| 4 | **Legal analysis** | **Registered marks:** Identity/similarity of sign and goods under s.10(2)/10(3) Trade Marks Act 1994 - likelihood of confusion including likelihood of association; for marks with reputation, take unfair advantage or detriment to distinctive character/repute under s.10(3). **Unregistered marks:** The classic trinity of passing off: (i) goodwill, (ii) misrepresentation, (iii) damage, as established in *Reckitt & Colman v Borden* [1990] 1 WLR 491. [SCOTS: Note] Scots law on passing off is the same as English law on this point. |
| 5 | **Demands** | Permanent cessation across all channels (packaging, web, social, domains, marketplaces, email, advertising). 14 to 21 calendar day cure window unless user specifies otherwise. [SCOTS: Note] 21 days is standard for pre-action protocol compliance. |
| 6 | **Remedies warning** | Interdict (interim/perpetual, Scots equivalent of injunction), delivery-up or destruction, damages or account of profits, interdict expenses, and reservation of all rights. Refer to s.14 to 16 Trade Marks Act 1994 for available remedies. |
| 7 | **Response mechanics** | Deadline, contact method, required documents: written cure confirmation, removal screenshots, destruction certificate (or delivery-up affidavit), distributor notices, revenue/unit-sales accounting for infringing period. |

## Drafting Rules

1. Use only verified facts; no vague threats or unsupported conclusions.
2. Tone: firm, professional. No coercive, extortionate, or inflammatory language. **Groundless threats** under s.21 Trade Marks Act 1994: making unjustified threats of infringement proceedings can itself give rise to a claim, avoid overstating risk when registration is not confirmed valid.
3. **Registered marks**: cite registration particulars and class-specific scope. S.10(1) for identical mark/identical goods; s.10(2) for similarity.
4. **Unregistered marks**: establish goodwill in the jurisdiction; show misrepresentation causing damage. Secondary meaning arguments apply to descriptive/laudatory terms.
5. Preemptively rebut apparent defences (descriptive fair use, own name defence under s.11(2), prior use under s.11(3), non-use under s.46, honest concurrent use) with evidence-based reasoning when facts support it.
6. Include governing-law clause; flag cross-border complexity (UK-wide but Scottish court venue for Scotland-based defendant). [SCOTS: Note] Courts of Scotland, Ireland, England & Wales, and Northern Ireland are separate jurisdictions, specify venue correctly.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Omitting exhibit references** - every infringement allegation must cite specific evidence.
- **Groundless threats** - s.21 TMA 1994 gives a remedy for unjustified threats; be measured and verify registration is not obviously invalid.
- **Vague demands** - specify exact channels, formats, and proof required for compliance.
- **Missing accounting demand** - always require written revenue/profit accounting for the infringing period (or destruction certificate if goods are seized).
- **Temporary language** - all demands must be permanent (cessation), not temporary.
- **Dilution confusion** - UK dilution operates under s.10(3) TMA 1994 (marks with reputation). This requires a higher threshold than mere confusion, confirm the mark qualifies.
- **Cross-border issues** - if the infringer operates outside UK, separate considerations apply; Irish jurisdiction issues if relevant.
- **Scottish pre-action protocol** - The Pre-Action Protocol for IP Claims in Scotland requires a formal letter of claim 21 days before raising proceedings, ensure compliance if litigation is contemplated.

## Scotland/UK Adaptation

**Adapted for:** Scotland and UK law
**Changes made:**
- Replaced Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 1116 to 1117) with Trade Marks Act 1994 (ss. 10 to 16, 21, 46)
- Replaced USPTO with UKIPO (UK Intellectual Property Office)
- Replaced federal court system with Court of Session (Outer House for IP) / Sheriff Court (claims under £100k)
- Replaced "injunction" with "interdict" (interim/perpetual) - Scots law term, Added passing off (unregistered mark protection) replacing common-law trademark and state-law claims, Replaced likelihood-of-confusion analysis with s.10(2)/(3) TMA 1994 and passing-off trinity (*Reckitt & Colman v Borden*)
- Added groundless threats warning (s.21 TMA 1994) - no US equivalent, Added 5-year non-use revocation exposure (s.46 TMA 1994)
- Replaced "damages" with "damages or account of profits" (alternative remedies under UK law)
- Added delivery-up / destruction court orders (s.16 TMA 1994) replacing US inventory-destruction language, Replaced USPTO registration numbers with UKIPO registration numbers, Replaced USPTO filing/registration dates with UKIPO filing/registration dates, Replaced class system with UKIPO trade mark classes (Nice Classification, same system)
- Updated pre-action protocol: UK IP Pre-Action Protocol / 21-day letter of claim, Replaced "fees" cost-shifting with "judicial expenses" (Scots costs rule, loser pays taxed costs)

**Key Scottish/UK considerations:**
- Groundless threats (s.21 TMA 1994) is a material risk, do not threaten proceedings without confirmed valid registration, Passing off requires established goodwill in the jurisdiction, this is jurisdiction-specific within the UK, Court of Session (Outer House) is the primary IP court in Scotland; Sheriff Court handles lower-value claims, Interdict (interim) requires a prima facie case and balance of convenience test, UK-wide rights: a UKIPO registration covers Scotland, England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, No separate state-level trademark registration in Scotland/UK, Pre-action protocol compliance is essential before raising proceedings, failure to follow can result in expenses (costs) sanctions, Section 10(3) (dilution for marks with reputation) requires proof of reputation in a substantial part of the UK, including Scotland

## Foreign-Law / US-Origin Guardrail

This skill may contain inherited US terminology. For Scotland/UK use, translate rather than copy. Examples: discovery is not Scots commission and diligence/recovery of documents; tort is generally delict in Scots civil analysis; summary judgment is not automatically the Scots summary decree test; bankruptcy concepts may map to sequestration, liquidation, administration, or restructuring depending on party and forum; HIPAA/CCPA/SEC/EEOC/FTC/CFPB concepts require UK GDPR, DPA 2018, FCA, ICO, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, tribunal, or sector-regulator mapping as appropriate. If the matter is genuinely US or foreign-law, quarantine the foreign-law analysis and warn that local counsel/source verification is required.
