# AI tells in Scottish legal and formal procedural writing

Use this checklist when reviewing a draft. These are not automatic errors. They become problems when they make the draft sound synthetic, inflated, unsafe, or procedurally wrong.

## High-risk tells

1. Imported terminology
   - defendant, costs, county court, particulars of claim, CPR, injunction, disclosure.
   - Check whether the correct Scottish term is defender, expenses, sheriff court, initial writ, condescendence, interdict, recovery of documents, specification of documents, productions, or lodging documents.

2. Evidence-free legal claims
   - "well-established case law", "the legislation is clear", "this is manifestly unlawful".
   - Fix by naming the authority, rule, policy, order, or document. If none is provided, narrow the wording.

3. Overstated allegations
   - fraud, dishonesty, bad faith, misconduct, discrimination, criminality, contempt, perjury.
   - Fix by stating the facts and asking the decision-maker to draw the inference, unless the allegation is expressly supported.

4. Generic public law language
   - irrational, unreasonable, procedurally unfair, natural justice, legitimate expectation.
   - Fix by stating what actually happened: no reasons, no notice, no chance to respond, relevant evidence ignored, wrong policy applied.

5. Pompous legal ornament
   - aforesaid, herein, wherefore, said letter, the same, with the greatest respect, this Honourable Court in every paragraph.
   - Fix with precise nouns and simple references.

6. Fake authority through tone
   - "It is respectfully submitted" repeated, long abstract paragraphs, dramatic conclusions.
   - Fix by using a numbered structure and direct propositions.

7. AI chronology fog
   - repeated requests, numerous occasions, prolonged period, extensive correspondence.
   - Fix with dates, senders, recipients, and production numbers.

8. Symmetrical templates
   - every section has the same length, every bullet has the same grammatical shape, every paragraph ends with a conclusion.
   - Fix by choosing the structure the document needs, not the structure AI prefers.

9. Wrong level of formality
   - party litigant text turned into Victorian legalese, or Court of Session argument turned into a casual complaint.
   - Fix by matching the audience.

10. Weak remedy
    - long complaint but no clear ask.
    - Fix by stating the order, decision, review, apology, correction, repayment, compensation, or service improvement sought.

11. Typographic dramatic flourishes
    - em dashes (—) used in prose as dramatic breaks
    - curly or smart quotation marks where straight marks fit the context
    - decorative arrows or symbols (→, ⇒, •, ✦)
    - en dashes used as decorative breaks rather than genuine range indicators
    - Fix by restructuring the prose: split the sentence, use a conjunction, use a semicolon, or rewrite so the break is not needed. Never use `--` as a replacement.

## Scottish legal phrase replacements

| AI or imported phrase | Safer Scottish/formal alternative |
|---|---|
| defendant | defender or respondent, depending on procedure |
| plaintiff | pursuer, petitioner, claimant in simple procedure |
| claimant | claimant only in simple procedure or another scheme that uses it |
| costs | expenses |
| judgment | judgment for reasons, decree/interlocutor for order where appropriate |
| injunction | interdict, if Scots law context |
| disclosure | recovery of documents, productions, specification of documents, or lodging documents, depending on context |
| trial date in civil proof context | proof or hearing, depending on procedure |
| the aforementioned letter | the letter dated [date] |
| pursuant to | under, in terms of, or because of |
| notwithstanding | despite, unless quoting law |
| the respondent blatantly ignored | the respondent did not answer, did not comply, or did not explain |
| this caused profound distress | describe the actual effect |
| at its core | the issue is |
| it is important to note | delete |
| in light of the above | for these reasons |
| I seek justice | I ask the court/body to [specific remedy] |

## Natural Scottish legal tone markers

The writing should sound human because it is:

- dated
- evidenced
- local to the procedure
- proportionate
- specific about impact
- careful with allegations
- clear about remedy
- brief where possible

Avoid trying to make court writing "warm" in the ordinary content-marketing sense. In litigation, warmth often means fairness, candour, and restraint.
