# Welfare Power of Attorney & Anticipatory Care Planning in Scotland, Guidance Note

## Scottish Legal Framework

### Key Legislation

1. **Adults with Incapacity (Scotland) Act 2000** - The primary statute governing powers of attorney and guardianship in Scotland
2. **Mental Health (Care and Treatment) (Scotland) Act 2003** - Mental health detention and compulsory treatment
3. **Adult Support and Protection (Scotland) Act 2007** - Protection of adults at risk of harm

### Power of Attorney Types (Scotland)

Under the 2000 Act, two distinct types:

| Type | Scope | When Effective |
|---|---|---|
| **Continuing Power of Attorney** | Property, financial, and business affairs | Can be effective immediately or on incapacity (as specified) |
| **Welfare Power of Attorney** | Personal care, health care, medical treatment decisions | **Only on incapacity** - never before |

Solicitors typically recommend granting **both** powers in a single document (combined Continuing and Welfare Power of Attorney).

### Key Differences from US Health Care Proxy System

| US Concept | Scottish Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Health Care Proxy / Medical POA | Welfare Power of Attorney (Adults with Incapacity Act 2000) |
| Living Will / Advance Directive | Advance Directive (no statutory form, recognised at common law under the 2000 Act s.6(4)) |
| HIPAA Authorisation | No direct equivalent, Access to medical records under Data Protection Act 2018 |
| POLST / MOLST (medical orders) | No Scottish equivalent, Decision-making follows AWI Act principles |
| DNR / DNACPR | Clinical decision (not legally binding directive); discussed with welfare attorney |
| "Clear and convincing evidence" (NY *Storar*) | No equivalent standard, AWI Act principles govern |
| Agent may refuse treatment | Welfare attorney has power to refuse treatment (certification of incapacity required) |
| MAID (Medical Aid in Dying) | Assisted dying is **not legal** in Scotland (as of 2026; proposed bill under consideration) |

### The Welfare Attorney Role in Scotland

**Powers granted** (must be specified in the power):
- Decide where the adult lives, Decide on medical treatment (including refusal)
- Decide on day-to-day care and personal care, Access the adult's personal information (subject to data protection)

**Limitations:**
- Can ONLY act when the adult lacks capacity to make that specific decision (AWI Act s.1 - functional test)
- Must apply the **principles of the Act** (benefit, minimum intervention, take account of past and present wishes, consult relevant others)
- Cannot make decisions about **forced psychiatric treatment** (Mental Health Act route)
- Cannot consent to **sterilisation** or **termination of pregnancy** without court authority, Cannot act if there is a guardianship order in place with conflicting powers

### Capacity Assessment

- Decision-specific (functional test, not status-based)
- Must be certified by a medical practitioner (usually GP or psychiatrist) - Form AWI(1) or equivalent, Welfare attorney can only act once capacity is lost for that particular decision, Fluctuating capacity: adult makes decisions when able, attorney steps in when not

### Anticipatory Care Planning (ACP)

The Scottish equivalent of advance care planning / values letters:

- **Key Communication**: Summary of person's wishes (anticipatory care plan)
- **Hospital Passport** / **My Care My Way** - Personalised planning documents
- **Healthcare Improvement Scotland** - Promotes ACP nationally, Not legally binding in the same way as an advance directive in England, but carries significant weight, Best to have ACP reviewed by GP and placed in **Key Information Summary (KIS)** - shared health record accessible by NHS 24, ambulance, out-of-hours

### Registration Requirements

- Welfare Power of Attorney must be registered with the **Office of the Public Guardian (Scotland)** before it can be used, Registration takes 6 to 10 weeks, Must notify the adult and certain relatives of the application, Solicitor involvement is **highly recommended** (and virtually universal practice)

### Values Letter / Instruction Letter in Scotland

A Scottish equivalent of the US values letter can serve as:
- Supplement to the Welfare Power of Attorney, Part of an Anticipatory Care Plan, Guidance for the welfare attorney in applying the substituted judgment

However, it is **critical to note**: Scottish law applies the **"benefit" principle** (AWI Act s.1(2)) - the welfare attorney must act in the adult's **best interests**, rather than pure substituted judgment. The values letter helps establish what the adult would consider to be in their best interests.

### Conflict Resolution

- Office of the Public Guardian (OPG Scotland) - Oversight body, Mental Welfare Commission for Scotland, For welfare concerns, Sheriff Court, For disputes or applications for guardianship, Independent advocacy services (Scottish independent advocacy)

### Practitioner Notes

- Never use US-origin "health care proxy" terminology in Scottish documents, use "Welfare Power of Attorney"
- The POA document itself must be **signed by the granter** in the presence of a solicitor (or other prescribed person)
- Witness requirements are strict, certain people cannot witness (family members, medical practitioners involved in care)
- Registration with OPG is mandatory before the document becomes effective, If no POA exists and the adult loses capacity, an application for **guardianship** to the Sheriff Court is required
