## Key Scottish Framework

### Duty to Preserve in Scotland

Unlike the US (FRCP 37(e)), Scottish civil procedure does not have a codified rule imposing a duty to preserve evidence in anticipation of litigation. However:

1. **Common law duty**: Once litigation is reasonably anticipated, a party is under a common law duty not to destroy or dispose of relevant documents/evidence
2. **Court rules**: Under the Rules of the Court of Session (RCS) and Sheriff Court Ordinary Cause Rules (OCR), parties are required to disclose relevant documents on specification
3. **Spoliation consequences**: Scottish courts may draw adverse inferences from the destruction of evidence; the court may also grant commission and diligence to recover evidence before destruction
4. **Administration of Justice (Scotland) Act 1972, s. 1**: Provides for inspection and preservation of documents and property before proceedings are raised
5. **No FRCP 37(e) equivalent**: Scottish courts have inherent power to address spoliation through adverse inferences and expenses orders, but there is no specific rule comparable to FRCP 37(e)

### Scottish vs US Preservation Terminology

| US Term | Scottish Equivalent/Context |
|---|---|
| Litigation hold | Common law preservation obligation; formal instruction |
| FRCP 37(e) sanctions | No direct equivalent; adverse inferences / expenses sanctions |
| Spoliation | Destruction of evidence, common law remedy |
| ESI preservation | Same concept; UK GDPR may impose additional data retention considerations |
| Reasonable anticipation | Same principle but developed through common law |
| Penalty for destruction | Expenses (costs) sanctions; adverse inference directions |

## Prerequisites

Gather before drafting:

1. **Case identification** - parties, court reference (if raised), or anticipated claims
2. **Key facts** - relevant dates, transactions, events establishing the dispute
3. **Known custodians** - individuals likely possessing relevant information
4. **Data sources** - IT systems, communication platforms, cloud services, retention policies
5. **Temporal scope** - preservation start date through present

## Quick Start

Format as formal business correspondence: letterhead, date, recipient block (name/title/org/address), re-line with matter description.

## Required Sections

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Opening Notice** | Solicitor, client, dispute nature. If raised: caption, court, reference number. If pre-action: state client reasonably anticipates litigation (see Commercial Actions Procedure / pre-action protocol). |
| **Matter Description** | Factual context sufficient for recipient to identify custodians and sources. Do not disclose litigation strategy. |
| **Preservation Scope** | Categorised instructions per scope table below |
| **Implementation Instructions** | Preservation procedures per checklist below |
| **Spoliation Consequences** | Legal consequences of non-compliance under Scots law |
| **Response Requirements** | Written acknowledgment deadline (5 to 10 business days), hold confirmation, contact info |
| **Closing** | Formal closing, signature block with professional details |

## Preservation Scope Categories

Include only categories applicable to the dispute:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Paper** | Contracts, memoranda, notes, financials, invoices, drafts |
| **Electronic comms** | Email, texts, IMs, social media, voicemails, all platforms/devices including personal if used for business |
| **Electronic documents** | Documents, spreadsheets, databases, presentations, calendars, native format with metadata intact |
| **Cloud/storage** | Cloud services, shared drives, backups, archived data, servers, mobile devices |
| **Physical** | Products, equipment, machinery, vehicles (if applicable) |
| **Audio/visual** | Recordings, photos, videos, surveillance footage |

Key emphasis: metadata should be preserved where practical; no format conversions; personal devices/accounts included if used for business. Note: UK GDPR data retention requirements may need to be balanced against preservation obligations.

## Preservation Checklist

Instruct recipient to:

- [ ] Suspend all routine destruction, auto-deletion, email purging, and retention schedules immediately (subject to UK GDPR data minimisation obligations, seek legal advice if conflict)
- [ ] Issue written preservation instruction to all relevant individuals by name and position
- [ ] Direct IT to disable auto-deletion, protect systems from data-destructive maintenance
- [ ] Preserve all newly created documents relating to the matter
- [ ] Maintain records of all preservation efforts
- [ ] Continue preservation until matter resolved or written release provided

## Spoliation Consequences (Scots Law)

Address with appropriate firmness:

- Scottish courts have inherent power to draw adverse inferences from the destruction or loss of evidence, The court may order the party responsible to pay expenses (judicial expenses) on an indemnity basis, In egregious cases, the court may grant decree in default (default judgment)
- The Administration of Justice (Scotland) Act 1972, s. 1(1A) provides for inspection and preservation of documents and property before proceedings, non-compliance may result in court enforcement, Routine business practices are not a defence once litigation is reasonably anticipated, Criminal sanctions may apply for destruction of evidence in certain circumstances (e.g. fraud investigations)

## Pitfalls and Checks

- **Scope calibration** - omit inapplicable categories (e.g. no physical evidence in a pure contract dispute)
- **No strategy disclosure** - provide enough context for compliance, nothing more
- **Ongoing duty** - emphasise preservation is continuous, not one-time
- **Questions ≠ delay** - state that scope questions do not excuse or delay compliance
- **Temporal scope** - define explicitly with start and end dates
- **Jurisdiction** - consider Court of Session vs Sheriff Court rules; Commercial Court procedures (RCS Chapter 47) may impose stricter obligations
- **UK GDPR interaction** - data retention obligations under UK GDPR may conflict with preservation demands; flag that recipient should seek independent legal advice on this
- **Tone** - professional courtesy with unmistakable firmness
- **Citations** - verify all case law and statutory references; mark uncertain ones with [VERIFY]
- **Pre-action protocols** - check if any relevant pre-action protocol applies (e.g. Personal Injury, Construction, Professional Negligence)

## Scotland/UK Adaptation

**Legal framework:**
- Administration of Justice (Scotland) Act 1972, s. 1 - inspection and preservation of documents and property, Rules of the Court of Session (RCS) - Chapter 35 (commission and diligence for recovery of documents)
- Sheriff Court Ordinary Cause Rules (OCR) - Chapter 28
- Common law, duty to preserve evidence in reasonably anticipated litigation, UK GDPR / Data Protection Act 2018 (Art. 17 right of erasure; Art. 5(e) retention limitation, must be balanced against preservation obligation)
- Prescription and Limitation (Scotland) Act 1973 - time bar considerations affecting preservation duration

**Key differences from US preservation model:**
- **[SCOTS: No FRCP 37(e) equivalent]** - Scottish courts do not have a codified spoliation rule comparable to FRCP 37(e). Remedy is through common law adverse inferences and expenses sanctions, No specific e-discovery rules, Scottish courts apply general preservation principles to ESI, UK GDPR imposes data retention limitations that may conflict with preservation demands, a balance must be struck, No FRCP 26(a)(1) initial disclosure, preservation obligation arises from common law, not court rules, Pre-action protocols exist in some areas (personal injury, professional negligence, construction) - these may govern pre-litigation preservation, Commercial actions (RCS Ch. 47) may involve more stringent disclosure obligations and judicial case management, Legal costs: expenses orders follow the event (loser pays) - preserving party may seek expenses if opponent destroys evidence, No US-style sanctions regime (monetary sanctions, adverse inference jury instruction) - Scottish procedure relies on judicial discretion

## Foreign-Law / US-Origin Guardrail

This skill may contain inherited US terminology. For Scotland/UK use, translate rather than copy. Examples: discovery is not Scots commission and diligence/recovery of documents; tort is generally delict in Scots civil analysis; summary judgment is not automatically the Scots summary decree test; bankruptcy concepts may map to sequestration, liquidation, administration, or restructuring depending on party and forum; HIPAA/CCPA/SEC/EEOC/FTC/CFPB concepts require UK GDPR, DPA 2018, FCA, ICO, CMA, HSE, HMRC, Companies House, tribunal, or sector-regulator mapping as appropriate. If the matter is genuinely US or foreign-law, quarantine the foreign-law analysis and warn that local counsel/source verification is required.
