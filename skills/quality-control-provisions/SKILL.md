Drafts an exhibit to a Trademark License Agreement establishing quality standards, monitoring procedures, and compliance obligations that preserve the licensor's trademark rights under the **Trade Marks Act 1994 (UK), s. 46 (revocation for non-use)** and the common law of passing off.

> [SCOTS: Note] This skill is adapted for UK and Scottish law. The core methodology (quality standards, inspection rights, sample approval, remedies) is largely jurisdiction-agnostic. All statutory references and regulatory bodies below use UK equivalents.

## Prerequisites

1. **Executed or draft Trademark License Agreement** - defined terms, exhibit numbering, territory, licensed marks, product categories
2. **Brand standards / style guide** - Pantone/CMYK values, approved mark forms, trademark designations (™ for unregistered UK marks / ® for UK registered trademarks)
3. **Approved pre-production samples** (if any) - definitive quality benchmark
4. **Applicable regulatory framework** - UK regulatory bodies: MHRA (medicines/medical devices), OPSS (Office for Product Safety and Standards, product safety/general consumer goods), ASA (Advertising Standards Authority, advertising/marketing), or industry-specific standards
5. **Quality metrics** - material specs, durability requirements, performance criteria

> [SCOTS: Note] Unlike the US (which has a use-based registration system), the UK operates a registration-based system under the Trade Marks Act 1994. A UK registered trademark does not require prior use before registration but requires **genuine use** within 5 years of registration to avoid revocation under s. 46(1)(a) TMA 1994. Quality control provisions in license agreements are essential to demonstrate that use by the licensee accrues to the licensor for genuine-use purposes.

## Core Sections

### 1. Foundational Quality Standards

- Applies to all products, services, packaging, and materials bearing the licensed marks, State compliance is a **material term** of the Agreement, Quality floor: meet or exceed quality of licensor's comparable products under the same marks, Reference approved pre-production samples as the definitive benchmark, Licensee bears sole regulatory responsibility; must maintain all licences/permits; must promptly notify licensor of any regulatory inquiry or enforcement action

Include applicable regulatory requirements (MHRA, OPSS, ASA, environmental, industry-specific) and quality metrics covering materials, workmanship, durability, performance, and aesthetics.

> [SCOTS: Note] Replace "licenses" with "licences" (UK spelling) and "Licensee" with "Licensee" (this is consistent, the UK practice uses "licence" for the noun/permit and "license" for the verb, though in contract drafting the party is typically "Licensee" as in US practice).

### 2. Trademark Usage Standards

- Marks displayed only in approved forms per brand standards manual, Proper designations: ™ (UK unregistered mark) for common law / passing-off rights; ® (UK registered trademark) for registered marks, No alteration without prior written consent

Prohibited uses: connection with unlawful/objectionable content, comparative advertising (subject to UK Comparative Advertising Directive / Business Protection from Misleading Marketing Regulations 2008), registration of marks or confusingly similar variations, use in corporate/domain names without authorisation, unauthorised sublicensing. All advertising and packaging require the same approval process as product samples.

> [SCOTS: Note] UK comparative advertising is regulated by the Business Protection from Misleading Marketing Regulations 2008 (SI 2008/1276) and the ASA CAP Code. This differs from US Lanham Act §43(a) standards.

### 3. Sample Submission & Approval

**Scope:** all items before production, distribution, or commercial use, finished products, packaging, labels, advertising/promotional materials, POS displays.

| Parameter | Standard |
|-----------|----------|
| Format | Physical samples (3 per variation) and/or high-res digital |
| Lead time | 30 to 60 days before production/launch |
| Review period | 15 to 30 business days from complete submission |
| Approval form | Written only; oral approvals insufficient |

Outcomes: Approve (may be conditional) · Request revisions · Reject. Approval does not waive inspection rights, is revocable if production materially differs, and both parties retain approved samples as the quality standard.

### 4. Inspection & Monitoring Rights

- Licensor may inspect licensee facilities on **5 to 10 business days' notice** (immediate access for suspected material violations or safety concerns)
- Access to personnel, QC records, production logs, testing results, Random sampling at no charge for lab/performance/safety testing, Compliant inspection costs: licensor. Non-compliant inspection costs: licensee, Licensee must maintain documented QC procedures, designate a QC officer, and submit periodic quality reports

### 5. Non-Compliance & Remediation

**Graduated cure periods:**

| Severity | Cure Period | Examples |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Minor | 15 to 30 days | Cosmetic deviations, minor labelling errors |
| Serious | 5 to 10 days | Material quality shortfall, unapproved mark usage |
| Critical | Immediate; no cure | Safety defects, regulatory violations |

Adequate cure requires: corrective action plan approved by licensor, cessation of non-conforming production, destruction or rework of non-conforming inventory (documented), enhanced QC procedures, and new samples demonstrating correction.

Failure to cure triggers cumulative remedies: suspension, termination, monetary damages, injunctive relief.

> [SCOTS: Note] In Scotland, interim interdict (equivalent to interim injunction in England/Wales) is available from the Court of Session and may be obtained on shorter notice with material breach evidence. The Sheriff Court also has limited IP expertise, though most significant trademark matters proceed in the Outer House of the Court of Session.

### 6. Product Recall & Market Withdrawal

- Licensor may require immediate cessation and recall in its sole discretion, Licensee obligations: notify distributors/retailers, consumer notification per licensor direction, establish return/refund process, retrieve and destroy recalled products (certified)
- **All recall costs borne exclusively by Licensee**
- Licensee indemnifies licensor from all claims arising from non-conforming products, quality deficiencies, regulatory violations, recalls, or breach of these provisions (survives termination)

> [SCOTS: Note] UK product recall obligations may also arise under the General Product Safety Regulations 2005 and the OPSS recall guidance. Consider cross-referencing these regulations in the recall provisions.

### 7. Licensee Acknowledgments

Licensee expressly acknowledges:
1. Quality control is essential to trademark validity under the Trade Marks Act 1994 and the law of passing off
2. Failure to control may result in revocation of the trademark registration or loss of passing-off rights
3. These provisions are **material terms**; material breach entitles licensor to immediate termination
4. All goodwill vests exclusively in licensor
5. Licensee will not challenge mark validity or claim ownership
6. Upon termination, licensee retains no rights in marks or associated goodwill

> [SCOTS: Note] The UK "genuine use" requirement under s. 46(1)(a) TMA 1994 provides that a registered trademark may be revoked if, within 5 years from completion of registration, it has not been put to genuine use in the UK by the proprietor or with their consent (which includes licensed use). Proper quality control ensures that licensed use is with the proprietor's consent. The UK has no equivalent to the US "naked licensing" doctrine; instead, lack of quality control may be relevant to whether use is "with the consent of the proprietor" for genuine-use purposes.

## Scotland/UK Adaptation

### Key statutory framework

- **Trade Marks Act 1994 (TMA 1994)** - primary UK legislation governing registered trademarks
  - **s. 46** - revocation for non-use (including "genuine use" requirement within 5-year period)
  - **s. 25** - registration of transactions (licences must be recorded to bind third parties)
  - **s. 28-31** - licensing provisions (licensee rights, sub-licensing, exclusive vs non-exclusive)
- **Common law of passing off** - unregistered mark protection (equivalent to US unfair competition / Lanham Act §43(a))
- **Business Protection from Misleading Marketing Regulations 2008** - comparative advertising
- **Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008** - consumer protection in advertising/trade
- **General Product Safety Regulations 2005** - product safety obligations

### Key differences from US law

| Aspect | US Law | UK Law |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Registration system | Use-based (intent-to-use applications) | Registration-based (no use required before registration) |
| Non-use period | 3 years for abandonment | 5 years for revocation (s. 46 TMA 1994) |
| Licensing doctrine | "Naked licensing" may cause abandonment | No equivalent naked licensing doctrine; quality control relevant to "consent" analysis |
| Unregistered rights | State law / federal unfair competition | Common law passing off |
| Mark designations | ™ (common law), ® (federal registration) | ™ (unregistered UK mark), ® (UK registered trademark) |
| Regulatory bodies | FDA, CPSC, FTC | MHRA, OPSS, ASA |

### Scottish courts and IP jurisdiction

- The **Court of Session (Outer House)** has jurisdiction for all UK trademark matters where the infringer is based in Scotland or infringing acts occur in Scotland, The **Sheriff Court** has limited IP expertise; most significant trademark matters are raised in the Court of Session, The **Intellectual Property Office (UK IPO)** handles registration and certain dispute proceedings (opposition, invalidation, revocation)
- There is no separate Scottish trademark register, UK IPO registrations (covering England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland) are unitary, Pre-action protocols: Scotland has no equivalent to English pre-action protocols; there is no automatic disclosure/discovery, it is application-based and typically narrower, Interim interdict (equivalent to interim injunction) is available from the Court of Session, IP actions in the Court of Session are subject to specific case management rules

### UK IPO forms relevant to quality-controlled licensing

| Form | Purpose | Fee (approx.) |
|------|---------|---------------|
| **TM50** | Application to record a licensee (s. 25 TMA 1994) | £50 |
| **TM16** | Application to record assignment/change of ownership | £50 |
| **TM3** | Application to register a trade mark | £170 (one class) |
| **TM31R** | Request for certified copy | £50 |

Forms are available from: <https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/trade-mark-forms>

### UK licensing vs US licensing, practical notes

1. **Recording licences**: Under s. 25 TMA 1994, until a licence is recorded at UK IPO, it is ineffective against a person who acquires a conflicting interest in the registration and was unaware of the licence. Recording using Form TM50 (fee £50) is recommended.
2. **Genuine use**: The 5-year "genuine use" period under s. 46(1)(a) runs from completion of registration. Licensed use by the licensee counts as use by the proprietor, but only if the quality control provisions demonstrate the licensor's ongoing control and consent.
3. **Exclusive vs non-exclusive**: UK law distinguishes between exclusive, sole, and non-exclusive licences. An exclusive licensee has the same rights as the proprietor regarding infringement (s. 31 TMA 1994).
4. **GBP references**: Where drafting financial penalties or liquidated damages, use GBP (£) and ensure compliance with UK law on penalty clauses (the common law penalty rule following *Cavendish Square Holding BV v Makdessi* [2015] UKSC 67).
5. **Goodwill**: Under UK law (and Scottish common law), goodwill in a licensed mark vests in the licensor. Passing-off claims require reputation/goodwill in the relevant UK market.
